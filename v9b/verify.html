Method · Three layers

How a service gets verified.

Layer 01

Biometric & identity.

Unique identification confirms real people received real services. PersonalID prevents duplicates, ghost beneficiaries, and re-submission under different names.

Layer 02

GPS & coverage.

Geolocation confirms where a visit happened. Microplan execution pushes workers door-to-door. A Service Area can't be closed until every Delivery Unit in it is addressed.

Layer 03

Algorithmic detection.

AI-powered analysis across submissions flags anomalies. Independent of GPS and photo layers — a fabricated visit has to beat all four layers to pass.

Learnings · What verification catches

Three learnings from how the layers actually hold up.

01

Workers naturally cluster. Microplans force them outward.

CHC campaigns · 2024–26 · Multi-country
SourceFP Final Report
Geographic-coverage analysis

Give FLWs a target area and a visit quota. They'll figure it out. That was the assumption. In practice, workers cherry-pick. Given a neighbourhood, they average 0.4 visits per estimated eligible child — visiting accessible households and skipping the difficult ones. The populations missed were systematically those with the lowest baseline coverage.

Microplanning closes this. We overlay a grid using Google Open Buildings + OpenStreetMap + Microsoft data. Cells with buildings become Delivery Units. DUs group into Service Areas sized for 1–2 weeks of work. A worker cannot move to the next Service Area until every DU in the current one is closed — by visit or confirmation of no eligible children.

02

We paid people to defeat our fraud detection. Then we watched them fail.

Adversarial testing · 3 rounds · 33 paid fabricators
SourceFP Final Report
Adversarial-testing section

Our verification stack — biometrics, GPS, photos — handles fraud risk. Good enough. That was the assumption. But what if a motivated insider tried to fake data? Could statistical patterns alone — no GPS, no timestamps, no photos — tell us?

We paid experienced FLWs to submit batches of 100 fake visit records each, across three rounds, with coaching between rounds and bonuses for the most convincing fakers. Using only three data elements — age, gender, MUAC — a Random Forest model achieved AUC 0.91 ± 0.09. Only 2.5% of real workers had fraud risk scores higher than the lowest-scoring fabricator.

Fraud detection isn't a promise. It is an empirically validated layer.

03

Guardrails are not hard stops. This is a design philosophy.

KMC · 2026 · Program-design memo
SourceKMC Conceptual Model
March 2026

When a FLW misses a target, the simple response is to reject the visit or withhold payment. This is what most platforms do. We don't.

We distinguish payment criteria (hard — must be met to be paid) from guardrails (soft — trigger audits and performance conversations, but don't block payment). KMC requires 4 mandatory home visits per SVN as a payment criterion. But "first visit within 3 days of discharge" is audited, not enforced. Current performance: 50% against a 70% target. We're working on it through LLO coaching, scheduling support, discharge-notification integration — not through punishment.

Platforms that punish missed guardrails don't get honest data. FLWs learn to game the system. A platform that separates audits from payment gets the real data it needs to improve.
