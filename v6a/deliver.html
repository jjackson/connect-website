The Lede

Once a worker passes training, they begin delivering in their community — door-to-door, using a mobile app that guides each visit step by step. The app is both a job aid and a data entry tool: it walks through registration, counseling, screening, and treatment, and captures GPS, timestamps, and images as it goes. There's no paper to lose, no end-of-day data entry, no uncertainty about what happened.

But the more important design choice sits outside the app. Connect doesn't directly employ frontline workers. Instead, the platform contracts locally-led organizations — nonprofits, community health networks, social enterprises — who recruit workers, manage government approvals, procure smartphones, and handle the on-the-ground work a remote platform can't. The platform handles the rest: verification, payment, training infrastructure, supervision tooling.

The Three Features

The shape of delivery.

01

Guided workflows

CommCare guides FLWs step by step through each visit — from registration through counseling, screening, and treatment. The app captures GPS, timestamps, and images for verification. No paper. No end-of-day data entry. Every interaction is structured as it happens.

02

Locally-led

LLOs — local nonprofits, community health organizations, social enterprises — manage government approvals, smartphones, localization, and FLW recruitment. They know the community. Connect provides the platform; they provide the relationships.

03

Performance-based

LLOs are paid per verified service delivered — not per FLW on staff. Connect has scaled to 44 LLOs across 11 countries with minimal growth in Dimagi's team, because costs track delivery rather than organizational size.

A closer look

How we find — and scale — the LLOs.

The LLO activation model is deliberately light. A simple Expression of Interest — a brief scope doc and a Google Form circulated through Dimagi's professional network — yielded about 100 applications for CHC alone. From there, a Trial Run: $3,000 to deliver 3,000 verified visits. Organizations that pass earn larger campaigns. Organizations that don't, don't.

Of 37 LLOs contracted under the Founders Pledge grant, 24 entered Trial Runs and were offered larger campaigns — a 65 percent pass rate. Ten underperformed and weren't continued. Three skipped the trial because they had a track record already. The model lets funders scale participation without guessing who can deliver.

The Innovation · CHC

Microplanning pushes workers into the hardest-to-reach cells.

Before each CHC campaign, Connect overlays a grid onto the target area using open-source satellite data from Google Open Buildings, OpenStreetMap, and Microsoft. Grid cells with buildings become Delivery Units. Units group into Service Areas sized for a worker's week or two of work. Workers can't unlock a new area until they close the current one.

"Microplanning pushes workers into areas they'd otherwise skip."

Founders Pledge Final Report · 2026

The effect: campaigns with microplans reach 94 percent of the estimated population in visited cells, against 82 percent for well-run comparable non-Connect programs. The populations missed by loose campaigns are typically those with lower baseline service coverage — exactly the ones funders most want to reach.

Thoroughness follows too. In microplan-supported campaigns, workers averaged 1.4 visits per estimated child across visited cells. In Trial Run campaigns without geographic saturation requirements, the average fell to 0.4.

A second innovation

AI supervision, at scale.

Beyond training, Dimagi is piloting a conversational AI supervision tool — built on its Open Chat Studio platform and accessed via WhatsApp. Workers ask questions about dosing, eligibility, or procedures and get a contextually grounded answer in seconds. In a Kenya pilot, the chatbot handled 500 of 511 queries — 97.8 percent — without needing human escalation.

Independent evaluation put accuracy at 88.7 percent and usefulness at 81.5 percent. Average response time: 5 seconds. This is what scalable frontline supervision looks like when the supervisor never sleeps.
