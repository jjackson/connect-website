Learning 01 · Layered training · Platform mechanism

The knowledge test wasn't enough. We had to build around it.

Our first training version assumed that if a worker could pass an in-app multiple-choice test, they could deliver the visit. They couldn't. Workers who aced the test still struggled with the nuances of in-person counseling — handling a skeptical caregiver, recognizing subtle danger signs, practicing motivational interviewing instead of reading a script. Supervised observations revealed a category of skill the multiple-choice format couldn't reach. We were selecting for people who could memorize facts, not for people who could make a hesitant mother feel heard.

Rather than make the test harder, we added layers around it. Workers now complete self-paced digital learning in the Learn app, pass an in-app knowledge test, interact daily with an AI coach bot built on Dimagi's Open Chat Studio, practice with peers through the Connect network, and complete one supervised observation visit rated by an LLO supervisor1. Each layer addresses something the previous one missed.

The AI coach is the pivot. It fills the space between text-based knowledge and real encounters: scenario-based practice ("a caregiver tells you they're already doing talk-and-play — how do you respond?"), motivational-interviewing drills with open-ended questioning and reflective listening, daily reflection on real visit experiences, and conversational identification of knowledge gaps that multiple-choice questions don't reach. In a Kenya CHC pilot, the same chatbot architecture handled 500 of 511 FLW queries (97.8%) without human escalation, with a 5-second average response time2. Independent evaluation of the responses: 88.7% accuracy, 81.5% usefulness.

85% of FLWs progress from training to service delivery. 88% score above 70% on their first observed visits. 77% pass the supervised observation gate.

Learning 02 · Target the right gap · ECD evidence

Caregivers already believe. Training has to move knowledge and behavior instead.

A good training program addresses the actual gap, not the one in the proposal. For ECD, baseline data told us we were aiming at the wrong thing — and reallocating the intervention mattered more than expanding it. The standard ECD design lens is "build belief, then build skill." Our pilot plan followed that convention. The Malawi data refused to cooperate.

Between April and July 2025, 76 FLWs in Malawi conducted 9,510+ visits. Baseline and endline surveys covered 50 caregivers across three domains — attitudes, knowledge, and observed behavior1. At baseline, nearly all caregivers already held positive attitudes: they agreed that talk-and-play supports brain development, endorsed responsive caregiving, rejected harsh correction. At endline, the attitude scores were equally high. The intervention didn't move attitudes — because there was nothing to move. They were already at ceiling.

Where the intervention did move the needle:

  • Knowledge of rapid child development timing: +33%
  • Knowledge of ways to support mental development: +41%
  • Observed teaching behavior: +21%
  • Observed encouragement of autonomy: +21% (still only 47% at endline — the hardest sub-domain)
The intervention didn't need to convince caregivers ECD matters. It needed to close the gap between belief and practice.

The finding reshaped the program. We're expanding the pilot from 3 visits to 8–10 visits across 12 months — not because caregivers need more time to be convinced ECD matters (they know), but because behavior change requires sustained practice and feedback1. Digital training content shifted weight away from attitude modules toward knowledge scaffolding and behavior rehearsal. The program is now evidence-guided, not just evidence-based. The Learn layer's job is no longer "convince" — it's "equip and reinforce."

For Connect's broader training methodology, this is a corrective. Training programs frequently default to attitude content because it's the easiest to write and the easiest to test. The lesson from ECD is to baseline the gap before you design the intervention. If attitudes are already correct, building more attitude content is wasted dosage. The same principle now informs how we approach the Learn layer for new programs: measure first, write content against the actual delta.
