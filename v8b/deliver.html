II. Deliver · Platform essay · April 2026

Locally-led delivery,
industrialized without
being centralized.

Connect's delivery surface isn't a field-ops team. It's an activation model — the Trial Run — that turns new locally-led organizations into operating partners with minimal risk on either side. This essay covers how the model works, what LLOs bring that Dimagi can't, and the place we tried it and it didn't hold.

Scoped: Platform (Trial Run reused across CHC, Readers, KMC, chlorine). CHC-scoped numbers named as such. · Source: FP Final Report, Feb 2026
Insight 01 · Operating model · Platform

LLO performance can't be pre-vetted. Only proven.

◆ Scope · Platform

The Trial Run is the single most-reused mechanism we have. It is the reason we can activate new partners in fragile contexts without betting the grant on them. It began as procurement orthodoxy: before our first large campaigns, we did due-diligence packages, reference checks, capacity assessments, prior-grant histories. For each candidate locally-led organization we assembled a picture of whether they looked like an organization that could deliver — and we contracted the ones that looked right.

The problem revealed itself as we looked back at our first year. The LLOs who looked right on paper and the LLOs who actually delivered were only loosely correlated. Reputation, team size, organizational maturity — none of it reliably predicted whether an LLO would actually run the campaign well. Some of our best partners came from quiet organizations we nearly overlooked. Some of the worst came from organizations with impressive prior work and institutional credentials to match.

The response was the Trial Run model. Every new LLO gets the same starting package: a small initial contract of $3,000 setup fee for 3,000 verified child visits, payment on delivery. If the LLO delivers cleanly — microplan executed, fraud flags within normal range, worker completion rates solid — they graduate to a larger campaign, on earned evidence. If they don't, we have spent less than the old diligence process would have cost, and everyone knows why.

Across 37 LLOs contracted under the model, 24 entered Trial Runs, 10 underperformed and were not continued, and 3 went straight to larger campaigns based on prior track records we had real evidence for. A roughly 60% Trial Run pass rate sounds low until you compare it to the counterfactual of contracting everyone who looked right and removing partners mid-campaign, which is worse for everyone. The Trial Run's economic argument rests not on saving money (though it does) but on making the cost of a miss cheaper than the cost of the process. That's what makes it repeatable.

The final thing about this model is that it turned out to be sector-agnostic. The same contract structure — small payment-based trial with a graduation path — now runs reading glasses distribution, kangaroo mother care, and chlorine dispenser monitoring, without modification. Whatever the mechanism is actually testing — can this LLO coordinate frontline workers against a measurable target in their own context — is program-independent.

Insight 06 · What LLOs add · CHC

LLOs navigate vaccine hesitancy with religious leaders. We didn't teach them this.

◆ Scope · CHC, CLP Stage 2 vaccine campaign

This is the insight that clarified why the L in LLO is load-bearing. Going into the CLP Stage 2 vaccine campaign — run inside the CHC program — we believed the Learn app's module on vaccine-hesitancy myths was sufficient preparation. If FLWs had absorbed the content, they would be equipped for the conversations at the door. This was a reasonable belief about what the platform could teach, and a naive belief about how those conversations actually go.

What we found when the campaign ran was that 5 to 6 of the 7 LLOs in the campaign independently leveraged religious leaders, community leaders, or existing community relationships as part of their outreach strategy. They did this before we asked. This was not a training output. This was operating knowledge the LLO already had and applied without being prompted. The Learn app's myth-busting content was content. What moved the door-to-door conversation was context.

The tactics varied by context. One FLW countered hesitancy with a personal example: "We were immunized, we have children." Another LLO had FLWs who were recommended by the community itself, lending immediate credibility that a platform-appointed stranger would not have had. Religious leaders endorsed the campaign in Friday sermons. Community leaders walked the first few houses with FLWs to open the doors that mattered most. None of this was scriptable from Cambridge, Massachusetts. All of it mattered more than the script.

What we changed as a result was the direction of flow between platform and LLO. We had been trying to push "outreach strategy" content from the platform down to the LLO. We now build the Learn plus AI coach system to be receptive to what LLOs bring — prompting FLWs to reflect on what worked in their context, surfacing successful local tactics to other LLOs through the coach, and leaving the actual outreach strategy in local hands. The platform's job is to enable what the LLO already knows how to do, not to replace it.

This is the point where we have to be careful about generalizing. The 7-LLO finding is from CLP Stage 2 inside CHC. The underlying cultural observation — that locally-led organizations bring locally-grounded outreach strategy — we believe to be general. The specific religious-leaders tactic is specific to its context. The platform's posture of receptivity is general and is now how we design across every program on Connect.

Insight 09 · Published failure · CHC (CAR)

We documented our failure in CAR. Publishing it is part of the practice.

◆ Scope · CHC, Central African Republic

The Trial Run model makes new partnerships low-risk. It does not make every context workable. That distinction is the subject of this essay, and it's the reason we chose to publish what happened in the Central African Republic.

After the Trial Run model had worked across multiple Nigerian states, Kenya, and early work in other LMIC settings, we faced external pressure to claim that the activation model was context-agnostic. Internally, we believed it. The evidence we had was consistent with that belief. The evidence we did not yet have — from genuinely fragile contexts — we treated as a matter of scaling rather than of kind.

In the Central African Republic, part of the CHC vaccine work, we contracted two LLOs under the Trial Run model. Both dropped out. The primary reasons were staffing (LLOs could not keep field teams stable under security and displacement pressures) and connectivity (intermittent network and power made the Deliver app's verification flow harder to sustain than in other settings). Remote coordination from Dimagi, which had been adequate elsewhere, wasn't enough to compensate for in-country fragility.

The revision is plain. Activating partners in fragile contexts likely requires dedicated in-country presence — someone who can support LLOs through the exact local disruptions that Trial Run economics alone cannot buffer against. We are not repeating the same remote-only approach in similar geographies without that in-country support in place.

A word on why this is in a marketing site. Presenting only the wins is a tell. The CAR experience is shaping our approach to fragile contexts now, and the funders and partners we want to work with are the ones who prefer reading this over reading around it. If you are a funder whose portfolio is weighted toward fragile contexts, start the conversation here. We will tell you honestly where the model needs a different shape.
