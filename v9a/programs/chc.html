Child Health Campaigns. The platform's proving ground.

The largest program on Connect. Door-to-door Vitamin A, deworming, ORS, MUAC screening, and immunisation promotion for vulnerable children aged 0–59 months. CHC is where microplanning, adversarial fraud testing, and Trial Run activation were first proved — and where the 22% cost-per-visit reduction was measured.

The challenge

Reaching the children most likely to be missed.

Traditional child health campaigns average 82% population coverage. The 16–18% missed are systematically the populations with lowest baseline health service access — the same children the campaign was designed to reach. Closing that gap, at scale, across fragile contexts, is the problem CHC was built to solve.

Field notes · CHC

Five learnings that made this program.

Learning 01 · 37 LLOsFounders Pledge grant

The Trial Run model turned partner vetting into partner selection.

The Founders Pledge grant gave us 37 LLO contracts to work with. In traditional procurement, the hard part is vetting — capacity assessments, reference checks, organisational due diligence. In Trial Run mode, the hard part is operational: a $3,000 setup fee, 3,000 verified visits, evaluated on actual delivery speed and quality. Payment is performance-based, so we bear minimal risk if the LLO fails.

Of 37 LLOs

24 entered Trial Runs. 10 underperformed and were not continued. 3 went straight to larger campaigns based on prior track record.

Of 24 Trial Runs

17 organisations completed larger campaigns following successful trials. A 65% pass rate — higher than our pre-vetting accuracy ever was.

What we changed Trial Run is now the default activation mode across all Connect programs. It has been reused for reading glasses distribution, KMC, and chlorine dispensers without modification. Sector-agnostic infrastructure for partner selection.

Source: Founders Pledge Final Report, Feb 2026.

Learning 02 · MicroplanningMulti-country CHC

Microplans forced workers into the neighbourhoods they'd have skipped.

Without microplans, FLWs averaged 0.4 visits per estimated eligible child — visiting accessible households, skipping the difficult ones. With microplans, that rose to 1.4. A 3.5× increase in thoroughness, and a jump in population coverage from 82–84% to 94%.

The mechanism is procedural. We overlay a grid onto the operational area using Google Open Buildings + OpenStreetMap + Microsoft data. Grid cells with at least one building become Delivery Units. DUs group into Service Areas sized for 1–2 weeks of work. A worker cannot move to the next Service Area until every DU in the current one is closed.

Learning 03 · Adversarial testing3 rounds, 33 fabricators

We paid FLWs to beat our fraud detection. The data caught them.

Biometrics, GPS, and photos handle the obvious fraud vectors. But what about a motivated insider? We paid experienced FLWs to submit batches of 100 fake visit records each, across three rounds, with coaching between rounds and bonuses for the most convincing fakers.

Random Forest model

Using only 3 data elements — age, gender, MUAC — the model achieved AUC 0.91 ± 0.09. Excellent discrimination between real and fabricated data, with no access to GPS or photo layers.

Real-worker false-positive risk

Only 2.5% of real workers received fraud risk scores higher than the lowest-scoring fabricator. Near-perfect discrimination.

What we changed Algorithmic detection runs first, before GPS and photo verification. Because verification now has four independent layers (biometric, GPS, photo, algorithmic), a fabricated visit has to beat all four to pass. We publish the method — real verification needs to withstand adversarial scrutiny, not assert it.

Source: Founders Pledge Final Report, Feb 2026.

Learning 04 · Cost trajectory2024–2026 grant period

Cost per verified visit dropped 22% as the campaign scaled.

Most development programs get less lean as they scale. Connect inverted the pattern. Cost per verified visit started at $2.20 and ended at $1.70. During the same period the platform scaled to millions of visits per year without substantial growth in Dimagi's team.

Learning 05 · Vaccine campaign · CLP Stage 27 LLOs

LLOs navigated vaccine hesitancy with religious leaders. We didn't teach them this.

Of 7 LLOs in the CLP Stage 2 vaccine campaign, 5–6 independently leveraged religious leaders, community leaders, or existing community relationships as part of their outreach strategy. They did this without being prompted. One FLW countered vaccine hesitancy with a personal example: "We were immunised, we have children." Another LLO had FLWs recommended by the community itself, lending immediate credibility.

What we changed We stopped treating the app as the intervention. The app is infrastructure. The intervention is the relationship between a community and the people who serve it — which is why the L in LLO is load-bearing. Our job is to enable locally-led organisations to do what they already know how to do.

Source: CLP Stage 2 Report, Feb 2026.
