Programs/Child Health Campaign
Child health & nutrition · Flagship program

One million verified child health visits. The richest program on the platform.

The Child Health Campaign is where most of what we know about Connect at scale actually comes from. Door-to-door visits for children 0–59 months across eight countries, under the Founders Pledge grant and beyond. Vitamin A, deworming, oral rehydration salts, malnutrition screening, vaccine promotion. This page tells the five stories we could not tell without this program.

The challenge.

Child health campaigns — the door-to-door mass distributions of Vitamin A, deworming tablets, oral rehydration salts, and malnutrition screening — have been one of the most effective tools in global health for decades. The interventions themselves are extensively validated. A Vitamin A supplement for a child under five in a deficient population is one of the highest-yield investments in global health per dollar spent.

The hard part is not the intervention. The hard part is getting it to the households that need it, verifying that it was delivered, and paying the workers who did the delivery in a way that aligns incentives. Traditional campaign models rely on self-reported data, periodic audits, and cash logistics that are opaque by the time they reach the ground. That is the set of problems the Child Health Campaign, running on Connect, was built to solve.

CHC is the oldest and largest program on the platform. It is why the verification stack looks the way it does, why the LLO activation model looks the way it does, and why the cost curve bends the way it does. Five of the insights on this page originated here.

Program mechanics

What a CHC round actually looks like.

Microplanned grid

Before a campaign starts, Connect overlays a grid on the operational area from Google Open Buildings, OpenStreetMap, and Microsoft data. Cells with buildings become Delivery Units. DUs group into Service Areas of 1–2 weeks of work per FLW.

Door-to-door delivery

FLWs visit every household in their Service Area. Vitamin A dosing, deworming, ORS/Zinc where bundled, MUAC screening for malnutrition, vaccine promotion where applicable. Structured step-by-step in the CommCare app.

Verification stack

Biometric confirmation of child identity. GPS of the visit location. Photo capture. Algorithmic pattern analysis on the back end (AUC 0.91 from 3 data elements — see the Verify page).

Performance-based payment

$0.78/visit outside Nigeria (VAS + deworming). $1.30/visit in Nigeria with ORS/Zinc. One Sierra Leone LLO at $1.00/visit. Paid to workers via mobile money after verification.

AI supervisor (Kenya)

WhatsApp-based coach on Dimagi's Open Chat Studio. Handles dosing questions, eligibility edge cases, and procedure reminders. 97.8% of queries resolved without human escalation.

RCT in progress

A $1.5M PRO Impact-funded RCT for CHC runs in 2026 with IPA as evaluation partner. The evidence base grows from a quasi-experimental story to a formally randomized one.

How do you vet an LLO you have never worked with, in a country you have never run a program in?

This was the first structural problem CHC had to solve. The early version of Connect assumed we could do it the usual way — reference checks, capacity assessments, organizational diligence. What we learned, over the first wave of contracts, was that none of those predicted actual field performance with the reliability we needed.

So we replaced the pre-vetting step with a performance-based one. The Trial Run model contracts a new LLO for a small, well-defined piece of work: $3,000 setup fee for 3,000 verified child visits. This is a one- to two-month engagement. It is large enough to be taken seriously, small enough that a failure does not cost the grant, and structured enough that the performance data — speed, coverage, verification pass rate — is the hiring decision, not a meeting.

Under the Founders Pledge grant we contracted 37 LLOs through this model. Twenty-four entered Trial Runs. Seventeen completed their trial well enough to move to larger campaigns. Ten underperformed and were not continued — a 27% filtering rate that would have been invisible under traditional pre-vetting. Three went straight to larger campaigns based on prior track record and were held to the same performance bar.

The result is that of the 880,000+ verified visits under the Founders Pledge grant, a large share was delivered by LLOs we would not have been able to confidently pre-select on paper. Some had organizational capacity we would have worried about. Some had no prior CHC experience. What they had was the willingness to take a Trial Run contract, and the operational capacity to deliver under it. That became the qualifying signal.

The generality is the part that matters. This model is sector-agnostic. It has been reused without modification for Kangaroo Mother Care, Readers, and chlorine dispensers. A funder asking whether Connect can activate partners in a new domain is really asking whether the Trial Run model will work there — and so far, the answer has been yes.

What the model does not solve is the very top of the funnel. Our EOI for CHC drew ~100 applications, from which 37 were contracted. The filtering from 100 to 37 is still opinion work — judgments about organizational fit, regional need, and capacity, made by people rather than by performance data. We are the weakest link in that step. The Trial Run is honest about the ones we did pick. It tells us nothing about the ones we should have picked and didn't.

Source: Founders Pledge Final Report, Feb 2026 — LLO activation analysis; Connect insight 1.

Why are we achieving 94% population coverage when other campaigns plateau at 82–84%?

The short answer is that we are forcing our workers into areas that, given a choice, they would skip. The longer answer is a story about what equity looks like when it is enforced by software rather than requested in a briefing.

Before a CHC campaign begins, Connect overlays a grid on the target area using Google Open Buildings, OpenStreetMap, and Microsoft building data. Grid cells with at least one building become Delivery Units. DUs group into Service Areas sized for 1–2 weeks of work per FLW. The key constraint is this: a worker cannot unlock the next Service Area until they have closed every Delivery Unit in the current one — either completing visits or confirming there are no eligible children at home.

That constraint matters because, without it, workers cherry-pick. Given a neighborhood and a quota, they visit the accessible households and skip the harder ones. The coverage comparison table from the Founders Pledge grant is the cleanest evidence we have: microplanned CHC campaigns reached 94% of the estimated population in visited cells. Non-microplanned experienced-LLO campaigns reached 84%. Even well-run non-Connect CommCare campaigns reached only 82%. Same workers, same program, different constraint structure. Ten points of coverage came from making it operationally harder to skip.

The thoroughness metric moved even more sharply. Under microplans, workers averaged 1.4 visits per estimated eligible child in each cell. Without microplans, that number was 0.4. The 1.4 number is not a mistake — it includes revisits, sibling capture, and the second-pass behavior that happens when a worker has to keep coming back to close a DU. The 0.4 number is what happens when you do not.

Where this matters ethically is in who we were missing before. The 10–15% of the population that non-microplanned campaigns systematically skipped was, as far as we could tell, the population with the lower baseline service coverage. The furthest homes, the steepest paths, the communities that the health system generally reaches last. Microplanning does not add new households to the country. It adds them to our country, in the sense that they are now reached by the workers we are paying for.

And microplanning fails in specific, identifiable ways. In dispersed settlement patterns, workers walked long distances to Delivery Units that turned out to have no actual buildings — the satellite data was misleading. Some DUs fell on factory grounds, prison grounds, or other inaccessible land. One campaign removed microplan execution entirely due to partner constraints. Microplans are a sharp instrument; they are not a universal one. We are iterating on the sparse-settlement edge case, and we will be for a while.

Source: Founders Pledge Final Report, Feb 2026 — coverage comparison table; Connect insight 2.

If a motivated insider tried to fake visits, would we catch them?

This is the question that matters most to funders who have been burned by self-reported data in other programs, and it is the reason we ran the most uncomfortable study in the CHC research program to date. We paid experienced Frontline Workers to defeat our fraud detection. We watched them try.

The setup was deliberate. We recruited experienced FLWs — people who had delivered real visits, who knew what a real record looked like, and who were therefore the most plausible bad actors we could find. We paid each of them to submit 100 fake visit records. We ran three rounds. Between rounds, we coached the fakers on what had given them away, so that by round three they were producing records that were, as far as they knew, indistinguishable from real ones. We offered bonuses to the top 25% most convincing fabricators. The incentive structure was designed to get us the hardest possible adversarial data.

The result was stronger than we expected. Using only three data elements — age, gender, and MUAC (mid-upper arm circumference) measurements — a Random Forest model achieved an AUC of 0.91 ± 0.09. In practical terms, only 2.5% of real workers received fraud risk scores higher than even the lowest-scoring adversarial faker. Out of 33 fakers across three rounds, the algorithm detected essentially all of them on statistical pattern alone — before GPS verification, before photo verification, before biometric checks.

The implication for CHC is that fraud detection is not a claim made on a website. It is an empirically validated layer that has been tested against people we paid to defeat it. And because verification is stacked — biometric, GPS, photo, algorithmic — a fabricator has to beat four independent layers, not one. A fabricator who knows how to pass the algorithm still has to account for being in the right place at the right time with the right person. A fabricator who is actually on-site still has to pass pattern analysis. Stacking is the point.

What this means for a funder is that the cost-per-verified-visit number Connect reports is a real number. It is not an estimate, not a projection, and not a claim. It is the price of a visit that passed the stack. Undelivered work does not enter the denominator, because it does not get paid for.

The honest caveat is that our adversaries were paid testers, not real fraudsters, and adversarial testing is a continuous discipline. A real bad actor with months of context and a deep understanding of the model could plausibly do better than the testers did. Our response is to rotate which data elements the model weights most heavily, to re-run adversarial exercises on fresh campaigns periodically, and to never treat a single AUC as the final answer. The 0.91 is what the system catches today. Tomorrow's number is earned by continuing to attack it.

Source: Founders Pledge Final Report, Feb 2026 — adversarial fraud testing appendix; Connect insight 3.

Why did the cost per verified visit go down as the program scaled, when everything else in development gets more expensive?

This is the finding from CHC that has the broadest implication for global development funding, and it is also the most structurally surprising. Over the Founders Pledge grant period, the cost per verified visit dropped from $2.20 to $1.70 — a 22% reduction while the number of visits, LLOs, and countries were all increasing. This is not how most development programs scale. Most get less lean, not more.

Three architectural properties make the Connect CHC cost curve work in the direction it does. The first is that LLOs have inherently low overhead. They are local and lean by construction. They do not carry the org chart of an international NGO or the compliance infrastructure of a multinational. The marginal operational cost of adding a new LLO to the network is smaller than the cost of adding one unit of delivery capacity to a larger organization.

The second is that performance-based payment ensures costs track actual delivery rather than organizational size. A CHC LLO is paid per verified visit. If they do not deliver, they do not get paid. The grant's money does not flow to LLO overhead that underperforms; it flows to the fraction of the campaign that completed verification. This is the inverse of headcount-based funding, where expenses are fixed and output is variable.

The third is that digital technology is integrated into every step of the worker journey. The Learn app, the Deliver workflows, the verification stack, the payment rails, the supervisory AI coach in Kenya. The marginal cost of the 10,000th FLW is close to zero. The efficiencies compound as the network grows rather than decay.

The numbers underneath are country-specific. Nigeria campaigns with ORS/Zinc co-packs averaged $1.30 per visit across 14 LLO contracts. Outside Nigeria, lighter bundles (Vitamin A + deworming) averaged $0.78 per visit across 17 contracts. One Sierra Leone LLO delivered ORS/Zinc at $1.00/visit — near the practical floor of what this work can cost. Setup fees averaged $0.40 per visit during the grant, and at the scale we are building toward we expect that to fall to ~$0.10 per visit, because setup amortizes across more delivery.

The implication for funders is unusual. In most development portfolios, the first dollar is more cost-effective than the hundredth, because the low-hanging fruit gets picked first and overhead grows. In Connect CHC, the evidence across the grant period is that the next dollar of funding is expected to buy more verified impact than the first one did, because the structural efficiencies continue to compound. We do not claim this continues forever. We claim it is the observed direction over the two years we have the cleanest data for, and it is the right direction.

Source: Founders Pledge Final Report, Feb 2026 — cost structure appendix, Nigeria and non-Nigeria comparison; Connect insight 4.

What happens when the platform is not in the room? Does locally-led mean anything beyond branding?

The test case for this was a Connect Learning Program Stage 2 vaccine promotion campaign — seven LLOs operating largely without direct Dimagi support on the ground, using only the digital Learn app, the in-app assessment, the AI coach, and the standard documentation. We wanted to see what would happen when the platform was deliberately out of the room.

What happened was that five or six of the seven LLOs independently leveraged religious leaders, community leaders, or existing community relationships as part of their outreach. This was not in the curriculum. Our digital training covered vaccine myths and counter-messaging in clinical detail. It did not tell anyone to go talk to the imam before the door-to-door work began. The LLOs did it because they are locally led, and because they knew their communities well enough to know that the platform's content was the baseline, not the ceiling.

The specifics mattered. One FLW, countering a rumor about vaccine side effects, used a personal example: "We were immunized, we have children." Another LLO had FLWs who had been recommended by the community itself, which meant they arrived at each door with a pre-existing credibility that an external hire could not have manufactured. A third LLO arranged a meeting with the local religious authority before the campaign began, so that when the first household was visited, they had already heard the pastor endorse the campaign.

This is why CHC scales the way it does. If we had to centralize knowledge about community outreach — what the right signal is in each village, which authority figures matter, what the local framing should be — the program would not scale past the countries where Dimagi has heavy presence. Delegating that judgment to the LLO is not a decentralization move. It is a structural recognition that the platform cannot know what the LLO knows.

What CHC does operationally with this insight is quiet. We resist features that would standardize community outreach. We keep the counter-messaging content in Learn as a baseline and deliberately stop short of prescribing local tactics. We leave budget and schedule flexibility for LLOs to adapt. And we are experimenting with ways to let LLOs log outreach investments in the app — because the thing that matters most about the program is also the thing we are worst at measuring, and we would like to be less bad at measuring it.

Source: CLP Stage 2 Report, Dimagi, Feb 2026; Connect insight 6.
