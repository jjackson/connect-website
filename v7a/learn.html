How It Works/Learn

Training that has to earn the right to deliver.

Before a Frontline Worker begins service delivery through Connect, they must complete mandatory digital training and pass an in-app assessment. No classroom time. No placeholder credentials. Competence is verified before compensation. This page is the argument for why that is not enough — and what we added when we learned it wasn't.

01

A passing test score is not a competent worker. We had to learn that the hard way.

The simplest model for digital training is also the most tempting: put the content in an app, ask questions at the end, and let the score be the credential. If a worker passes, they are ready. That was our working assumption for the first year of Connect. It is intuitive, it is cheap to scale, and it turns out to be wrong.

What we found when we watched the work was that knowledge did not equal competence. Workers who aced the post-module test still struggled with the parts of the job that a multiple-choice question cannot capture — how to hold the room with a skeptical caregiver, how to recognize a subtle danger sign that the app has not explicitly flagged, how to practice motivational interviewing without leading the answer. The scores were high. The first-visit quality was not.

So we layered the model. A Connect worker now moves through five gates, not one. They start with self-paced digital learning in the Learn app. They take an in-app knowledge test and must pass to progress — that stays. But we added daily interaction with an AI coach bot built on Dimagi's Open Chat Studio, practice with peers, and one supervised observation visit rated by an LLO supervisor before any paid delivery is allowed.

The AI coach is where the ceiling moved. It does what a test cannot: scenario-based practice, motivational interviewing drills, and daily reflection on real visit experiences. A worker who has just come home from a visit can describe what happened in plain language, and the coach will find the gap in their thinking rather than mark a box. This is not auto-grading. It is a conversation that names specific things the worker missed and offers a structured way to practice them before the next visit.

The results are the reason this section exists. Across 400+ FLWs in ECD and CLP validation cohorts — drawn from the broader program of 5,000+ FLWs active across 12 countries — 85% now progress from training to service delivery, 88% score above 70% on their first observed visits, and 77% pass their supervised observation. Those are the live pass rates, not targets. They are also, notably, not 100% — which matters, because the whole point of the layered model is that it filters people out before the funder pays for work that would not have stood up in the field.

The 15% who don't pass the initial gate re-enter the learning cycle — no time limit, no penalty. 85% pass on first attempt; of the 15%, approximately half pass on their second cycle. No FLW is disqualified from the program for failing the gate. The model filters for competence, not for exclusion.

Live observation is the most expensive gate in the training pipeline. It requires a trained supervisor to travel, watch, and rate. Video-asynchronous variants — visits recorded and rated later — are in controlled pilot. Target deployment at broader scale: 2026.

Method

Five-gate model: self-paced learning → in-app test → AI coach daily interaction → peer practice → one LLO-supervised observation. Each gate is a pass-fail; failure loops the worker back to coaching, not to a dropped contract.

02

We started building an ECD program against a gap that, measurably, wasn't there.

When we designed the Early Childhood Development intervention in Malawi, the working theory was familiar. Caregivers needed to be convinced that talk-and-play mattered, that responsive caregiving built brains, that the first 1,000 days were real. An ECD program, we assumed, was in the business of shifting attitudes. We built the content around that.

Then we ran the baseline survey. Fifty caregivers. And the attitudes were already correct. Nearly every caregiver endorsed talk-and-play. Nearly every one agreed with responsive caregiving. The needle we had planned to move was already most of the way there — which is a pleasant surprise for a program officer and a disorienting one for a program designer.

What the data actually said was that the gap was somewhere else. Caregivers knew talk-and-play was important; they just did not know when. They did not know the specific timing of rapid brain development, or the concrete ways to support mental growth day to day. And crucially, when we watched them at home, the behaviors that flow from that knowledge — teaching, encouragement of autonomy — were weaker than the attitude scores suggested.

So we re-pointed the intervention. Not more attitude content; more knowledge and behavior content. The endline results, after eight sessions, were specific: +33% on timing of rapid child development, +41% on ways to support mental development, +21% on observed teaching behavior, +21% on observed encouragement of autonomy. Attitude scores stayed where they were, which was fine, because they had been fine to begin with.

This is why the Learn page, and not just the ECD page, tells this story. Digital training is only as good as the diagnosis behind it. If we had shipped the curriculum we originally planned, we would have moved the wrong metric and declared victory. Instead we built the curriculum the data asked for, and we kept going: the pilot's 3-visit structure is expanding to 8–10 visits over 12 months — not because we have to keep convincing caregivers ECD matters, but because behavior change takes sustained practice.

The 47% endline score on encouragement of autonomy (up from baseline, lowest of the four subdomains) tells us the 3-visit model is too short for this subdomain. The 8–10 visit curriculum over 12 months is designed specifically to address behavior change that requires sustained practice. The planned RCT will test whether the extended model reaches target thresholds.

03

When we step out of the room, does the training still work?

A digital training stack can look good in a pilot where Dimagi is sitting next to the LLO answering questions on the phone. The harder question is what happens when we are not there. CLP Stage 2 — the Connect Learning Program's second cohort of locally-led organizations — was the setup for finding out.

Seven LLOs operated a vaccine promotion campaign without direct Dimagi support, relying only on the digital Learn app, the in-app assessment, the AI coach, and the standard documentation. All seven completed their campaigns operating without direct Dimagi support — up from 2 of 7 at Stage 1, validating the self-service documentation layer. The content was good enough to stand on its own.

That number matters because it is the load-bearing test for one of Connect's structural claims — that digital technology integrated into every step of the worker journey creates efficiencies that compound as the network grows. If every new LLO needed a Dimagi representative on the ground, that claim would collapse. The CLP Stage 2 result is the closest we have come to falsifying it, and it did not.

But self-service has a limit. Of the seven LLOs, five or six independently leveraged religious leaders, community leaders, or prior relationships to counter vaccine hesitancy. We did not train them on that; they did it because they are locally led and they knew their communities. The platform's job, we concluded, is to make that kind of local adaptation possible — not to replace it. A digital curriculum that could cover every cultural context would not be a curriculum. It would be a novel.

The open problem is a kind of selection bias. CLP Stage 2's seven LLOs were, by that point, already filtered — they had survived Stage 1, they had made it through our EOI process, they had the organizational shape that uses a platform well. Saying digital training "works without direct support" is true for LLOs that are going to work with us at all. It is still an open question for the next tier of partners, and we are not yet claiming otherwise.
