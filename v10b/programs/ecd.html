the challenge // why ecd

The first 1,000 days, where knowledge doesn't equal practice.

The science of early childhood development is settled. The first 1,000 days set cognitive trajectories that are difficult to recover. Responsive caregiving, talking to infants, structured play, and developmentally-timed stimulation are among the highest-return interventions available in human development. The gap is delivery: 250 million children in LMICs are at risk of not achieving their potential, and at-home structured ECD support remains thin.

Most ECD program designs assume the binding constraint is caregiver attitudes. Train the FLWs to convince, build attitude-shift dashboards, watch the leading indicator move. We walked into Malawi with a version of this assumption. The pilot showed we were wrong about what the intervention had to do — and the redesign was a more honest piece of work because of it.

learning 01 // floor_effect

Caregivers' attitudes were already correct. The intervention had to move behavior, not belief.

scope=ecd.malawi The standard ECD intervention design assumes caregivers need to be convinced that early stimulation matters. Train the FLWs to make the case. Track attitude shifts as the leading indicator. Behavior change follows — eventually. We walked into Malawi with that template and ran a baseline survey to set the targets.

What the baseline showed

In our Malawi ECD pilot — 76 FLWs, 9,510+ visits, a 50-caregiver baseline/endline survey — nearly every caregiver already held positive ECD attitudes at baseline. Belief in the value of early stimulation, talking to infants, responsive caregiving. The intervention didn't need to convince them. It needed to close the gap between belief and routine practice. The "floor effect" — when the baseline is already so high that there is no room to move — was telling us the intervention design was solving the wrong problem.

Knowledge moved further than behavior

By endline: knowledge of rapid-development timing rose +33%; observed teaching behavior +21%; encouragement of autonomy +21% (still only 47% at endline). Knowledge moved further than behavior moved — exactly the pattern the layered Learn stack is designed to address. The observed teaching behavior number is the one that matters: caregivers were doing more of the work that early stimulation actually requires, not just describing it.

Why we report the gap

Reporting attitude shifts when attitudes were already at the ceiling would be flattering. It would also be useless to anyone who wanted to know whether the intervention was working. The headline metric for ECD on Connect is observed teaching behavior at endline, with knowledge as a leading indicator. Encouragement of autonomy at 47% endline is still the binding constraint — and we said so.

learning 02 // dosage_redesign

If practice is the binding constraint, more visits — not louder messaging — is the lever.

scope=ecd The Malawi pilot's behavior-vs-knowledge gap forced a design decision. The original ECD program was 3 visits over a defined window — sufficient to convey the content, modest enough to fit a tight budget. If the binding constraint had been belief, three visits would have been enough: belief moves on argument, and three structured arguments is meaningful exposure. Behavior is different. Behavior moves on practice, repetition, and feedback over time. Three visits is not enough to install a routine.

What we changed

We doubled the visit dosage from 3 visits to 8–10 visits across 12 months. The next cohort is the test of whether the increased dosage moves the metric the floor effect made the binding constraint: encouragement-of-autonomy at endline. The 47% endline figure is the hardest sub-domain — the practice that takes the most parental work to install — and we don't yet know whether 8-10 visits will move it, or whether it requires a different intervention design entirely.

What the dosage change costs

Doubling dosage roughly doubles the FLW-time and verified-visit cost per family enrolled — though it doesn't double commodity or training cost. ECD on Connect runs at $7–$18 per child fully-loaded, depending on country and LLO economics. The 8-10-visit redesign moves toward the upper end of that range. The bet is that behavior change is what justifies ECD spend; if it doesn't move on the higher dosage, the design needs to change again.

learning 03 // ecd_validates_layered_train

Layered FLW training was validated against ~400 ECD workers. The model holds.

scope=ecd+platform The layered training stack now used across the Connect platform — self-paced digital learning, in-app knowledge test, daily AI coach interaction (Open Chat Studio), peer practice, and one supervised observation visit — was developed in part on ECD. The ECD intervention is unusually demanding on FLW skill: the work is conversational, behavior-shaping, and depends on subtle observation of caregiver-infant interaction. A multiple-choice test catches almost none of what matters. We needed something else.

What the data shows

Validated across 400+ FLWs through iterative experiments: 85% of FLWs progress from training to service delivery. 88% score above 70% on their first observed visits. 77% pass the supervised observation gate. This isn't an ideal test environment — it's ECD, CHC, and KMC rollouts across multiple LLOs and countries, with the AI coach handling routine queries and supervisor time concentrated on the observation gate.

What we haven't decomposed yet

The 77% supervised-observation pass rate is the binding constraint of the whole training stack. It's also the figure we haven't yet decomposed cleanly: how much of the 77% comes from the AI coach, how much from peer practice, how much from supervised observation itself. The decomposition matters because each layer has a different cost and operational profile. We will publish it when we have clean comparison cohorts. Whether AI-coach contribution to the 77% generalizes outside ECD is open — we have not run the controlled comparison.
