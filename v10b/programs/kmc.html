the challenge // why kmc

The intervention works. The follow-up doesn't exist.

Kangaroo Mother Care is one of the most evidence-supported interventions in neonatal medicine. Continuous skin-to-skin contact, exclusive breastfeeding, and frequent monitoring reduce mortality in small and vulnerable newborns by up to 40%. The WHO has recommended it as standard of care for infants ≤2000g since 2022.

The bottleneck is post-discharge. Hospitals send families home within days of birth — sometimes hours — and the structured at-home follow-up that KMC requires rarely exists. Global KMC coverage for eligible infants remains below 5%. The gap isn't the science. It's the surface where the science meets the home.

Connect KMC is a verified-delivery program for that gap. 4–5 home visits in the first 60 days post-discharge. FLWs equipped with a portable scale, thermometer, pulse oximeter, and measuring tape. Danger-sign detection wired into the app. Performance-based payment to keep the LLO and FLW economics aligned with consistent delivery.

learning 01 // guardrails_vs_hard_stops

The 3-day window is a guardrail, not a payment criterion. We get real numbers because of it.

scope=kmc KMC has two kinds of quality check, and the difference between them is one of the most consequential design decisions on the platform. The 4 mandatory home visits per SVN is a payment criterion: below 4 visits, the visit cycle isn't paid. Hard. Binary. Enforced through the verification stack. The "first visit within 3 days of discharge" is a guardrail: audited, surfaced in dashboards, and addressed through coaching — but it does not block payment. Soft.

Why we drew the line that way

The first instinct, when an FLW misses a quality target, is to withhold payment. It feels fair. It seems like accountability. We considered it. We didn't do it, because punishing a guardrail corrupts the data. If missing the 3-day window costs an FLW money, they will falsify visit dates to hit it. The platform will show 100% compliance. We will have no idea what's actually happening in the field. The guardrail will have measured nothing, because the measurement itself changed the behavior. This is the fundamental problem with conflating a signal with a sanction.

The current gap, named honestly

The 3-day enrollment rate is currently at 50% against a 70% target. That is a real number — because we chose to get a real number instead of a fake 100%. The cause isn't FLW effort: it's that hospital discharge notifications aren't yet integrated into our enrollment workflow. We're working on it through LLO coaching, scheduling support, and discharge-notification integration with partner hospitals. Not through punishment.

A platform that doesn't punish missed guardrails gets the real data it needs to improve the program. A platform that does, doesn't.

learning 02 // equipped_visits

Every KMC home visit, the FLW arrives with the equipment to detect danger signs — and the app captures them.

scope=kmc Most home-visit programs run on observation alone. The FLW arrives, asks questions, eyeballs the infant, and reports back. That works for some interventions. It does not work for the small and vulnerable newborn, where danger signs can be subtle and the cost of missing them is mortality.

The equipment

Every Connect KMC home visit, the FLW arrives with a portable weighing scale, thermometer, pulse oximeter, and measuring tape. The app captures the readings — not memory after the fact. Danger signs — poor weight gain, hypothermia, infection markers — auto-trigger referrals through the platform, and the app prompts the FLW to follow up at the next visit. The visit becomes a structured assessment, not a check-in.

What this changes about the home visit

The shift is small in equipment cost and large in operational consequence. A visit without a scale is qualitative. A visit with one is a longitudinal weight curve. A visit without a pulse oximeter cannot detect early-stage hypoxia; with one, it can. The home visit becomes a continuation of clinical care rather than a community engagement activity. The verification stack catches FLWs who didn't actually take the readings — biometric ID, GPS, photo, and submission patterns all check out.

What's still uneven

Equipment availability across all 6 KMC LLO partners has been uneven during the rollout. We don't yet have a published audit of equipment uptime — how often a given LLO's FLWs arrive at a visit without a working pulse oximeter or scale. That number matters: a stockout looks like an FLW skipping a reading, and our system can't yet distinguish "skipped" from "no equipment available." It's open work.

learning 03 // enrollment_timing

We hit the 28-day target. We're missing the 3-day target. We publish both.

scope=kmc Across 4,005 KMC cases as of March 2026, the average age at enrollment is 12.8 days — comfortably inside our 28-day enrollment target. By that measure, the program is performing as designed. But the 28-day target is the outer bound. The narrower target — first visit within 3 days of hospital discharge — is currently being met for only 50% of cases against a 70% goal.

Why both numbers matter

The 28-day average tells you the program reaches families before the most acute neonatal risk window has closed. The 3-day rate tells you whether it reaches them in the high-risk first week. Both are real. Reporting only the 28-day number would tell a clean story; reporting only the 3-day rate would tell a misleadingly bleak one. We publish both.

The diagnosis

The gap isn't FLW effort. The binding constraint is information flow: hospitals don't yet have a structured channel to notify Connect KMC LLOs of qualifying discharges. By the time the LLO knows a family is home with a small newborn, several days have already passed. We're working on integration with discharge notifications at partner hospitals. Whether that closes the gap to 70%, or whether the 3-day target itself needs to be redesigned for the realities of post-discharge logistics, is open.

What's still scaling

Whether KMC FLWs sustain ≥4 visits per case at the 5,000+ case scale is also a live question. Current average is 3.4 against the 5-visit guardrail. The 4-visit floor is still hit by the payment criterion; the 5-visit guardrail is closer to the ideal pediatric follow-up cadence. As the program scales, drift from 5 toward the 4-visit floor would mean we are losing the second touchpoint where danger signs typically emerge. We monitor it explicitly.
