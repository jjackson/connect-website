CHC is Connect's most evidence-rich program. This page treats five questions the team had to answer to get CHC working — each with an evidence-tier badge and a deep link to the page that treats the underlying mechanism in full.

Q1 · LLO selection · I1 · N4 hard data ⟶ deliver#trial-run ⟶ insights#n4

How do we vet LLOs when past reputation doesn't predict performance?

The CHC LLO funnel is the cleanest published view of the Trial Run model1. 107 EOI applications. 37 LLOs contracted under Trial Run terms ($3,000 setup fee for 3,000 verified child visits, performance-based). 24 advanced to full Trial Runs. 14 graduated to larger campaigns. Three additional LLOs went straight to larger campaigns based on prior measured performance from earlier Connect programs.

The reason this funnel works as a procurement screen rather than a procurement document is that the data we collect during a Trial Run is real production data on the same Connect verification stack that a full campaign would use. There is no synthetic pilot signal — verified visits during a Trial Run are counted, paid, and verified the same way as visits in a campaign that has crossed the conversion gate. The 65% conversion rate is the output of a measurement, not an intent, and we publish it because the alternative (opaque vendor lists) is precisely what funder due diligence is designed to escape. The 35% that did not pass were paid the setup fee for the partial work they completed, not the per-visit pay; they were not continued, but they were not blamed either, and the published failure rate is part of how we hold ourselves accountable to the model.

Why this matters specifically in CHC: the program operates in 8 countries with very different LLO ecosystems. Nigeria has many candidate partners with prior CHV-campaign experience; Sierra Leone has fewer; Central African Republic has effectively none, as the failure documented on the Deliver page shows. Pre-vetting at scale across that variance was producing biased screens — partners we knew were systematically advantaged over partners we did not. Trial Run terms remove the bias by paying small for real and rewarding only delivery.

The honest gap on this CHC page: the selection criteria that drive the 65% conversion are program-manager judgment calls today, not codified into a model an outside funder could replicate. We have a checklist of red flags from the failed cohort (staffing concentration, single-point-of-failure dependence, cash-flow fragility) but it is descriptive, not predictive. A funder evaluating Connect for replication should know that the screening machinery is human, not algorithmic.

Source: FP Final Report (Feb 2026), CHC §LLO Activation Model — full funnel published. [1]
Q2 · coverage · I2 · N2 hard data ⟶ verify#microplans ⟶ insights#n2

How do we push verified delivery into the harder-to-reach areas without hand-routing each campaign?

Microplanning is the CHC innovation. The mechanism is portable — the same code path runs on every Connect program — but the published numbers are CHC-specific and the methodology was forged here.

Before microplans, FLWs working a CHC neighborhood averaged 0.4 visits per estimated eligible child1. They cherry-picked accessible households. The populations they missed were systematically those with lower baseline health coverage — convenience sampling at the exact moment equity was the goal.

The microplan layer overlays a grid onto the operational area using Google Open Buildings, OpenStreetMap, and Microsoft building data. Grid cells with at least one detected building become Delivery Units. DUs group into Service Areas sized for one to two weeks of work. The Connect app blocks an FLW from moving to the next Service Area until they have closed every Delivery Unit in the current one. After that change: 1.4 visits per estimated child in microplanned cells. Population coverage: 82–84% → 94%. A 3.5× thoroughness gain on the same FLW base.

Where the mechanism fails: in dispersed settlement patterns, FLWs walk long distances to DUs containing no buildings. Some DUs fall on inaccessible land — factory grounds, prison perimeters, restricted military zones. One CHC campaign in CAR removed microplan execution entirely because the partner could not work the layout. We do not yet have a published threshold for the settlement density at which microplanning becomes counterproductive — the operating rule today is that the partner has the final call. That rule is honest about what it does not encode: a quantitative density threshold that we could ship in the app and trust to flag a campaign before it starts. Producing that threshold from cross-campaign data is on the engineering roadmap, but it has not landed yet, and we would rather give partners the discretion than encode a threshold we have not validated.

Source: FP Final Report (Feb 2026), CHC §Coverage Results. [1]
Q3 · verification · I3 · N1 · N22 hard data ⟶ verify#fraud ⟶ insights#n1

Can the algorithmic verification layer detect fabrication when fakers are paid bonuses to defeat it?

The CHC fraud-detection adversarial test is the empirical foundation under Connect's verified-payment model. The test was designed and run inside CHC; the model applies platform-wide, but the AUC and bonus-test mechanics on this page are CHC-specific.

The protocol1: experienced CHC FLWs were paid to submit 100 fake visit records each, in three rounds. After each round, fakers were coached on what had given them away, so each round was harder than the last. Cash bonuses were offered to the top 25% most convincing fabricators — a structural incentive to actually try. We did not run a single round and call the result the upper bound; we kept paying people to defeat the system after they had been told why their previous attempts had failed.

The result on the algorithmic layer alone: using only three data elements (age of child, gender, MUAC measurement), a Random Forest classifier achieved AUC 0.91 ± 0.09. Out of 33 adversarial fakers across the three rounds, only 2.5% of real workers received fraud-risk scores higher than the lowest-scoring fabricator. The 97.5% framing — "97.5% of real workers scored cleaner than the worst-detected fake" — is the inverse of the same number.

Three features is a deliberately stressed choice; we could have built a 20-feature model with a higher AUC. We chose three because the question we wanted to answer was harder: can the algorithmic layer detect fabrication when the data has been engineered to look plausible? The other three layers in Connect's verification stack — biometric, GPS, photo — close the parts that age/gender/MUAC don't. A fabricated visit on Connect has to beat all four.

Where the CHC adversarial test does not generalize: we tested against motivated, paid, coached fabrication. The production false-positive rate against unmotivated fabrication — a tired worker who fudges one visit — is operationally more important and is not what the AUC 0.91 measures. We will publish the production false-positive rate when we have a clean cohort comparison.

Source: FP Final Report (Feb 2026), CHC §Fraud Detection (Adversarial Testing). [1]
Q4 · economics · I4 · N3 · N13 hard data ⟶ pay#cost-inversion ⟶ insights#n3

Does the verified-payment model actually invert the standard cost-with-scale curve?

CHC is the Connect program where the cost-inversion curve has been most cleanly measured. The standard NGO scaling pattern is that operational costs grow with output. CHC during the FP grant period was the inverse: as visit volume scaled to the 880,000+ figure, cost per verified visit dropped from $2.20 to $1.70 — a 22% reduction — without substantial growth in Dimagi's team1.

The architectural reasons are platform-level, not CHC-specific: LLOs have inherently low overhead (local, lean); performance-based payment ties operational cost to delivery rather than organizational size; integrated digital tooling produces compounding efficiency gains. The CHC numbers are the measurement of those mechanisms in one program; whether KMC, ECD, and Readers will show the same curve is an empirical question we will publish as data accumulates.

The bundle separation matters for procurement comparisons. Inside CHC, Nigeria visits include an ORS/Zinc co-pack and across 14 LLO contracts averaged $1.30 per verified visit fully loaded. Outside Nigeria, the bundle is VAS plus deworming with no ORS, and across 17 LLO contracts averaged $0.78. The $1.70 weighted average is the right number for "CHC during the FP grant period." It is the wrong number for "what would a similar campaign cost in country X." The right comparison depends on the bundle.

The setup-fee component, currently averaging about $0.40 per visit during the grant, is projected to converge toward $0.10 per visit at scale as the Trial Run model proves out and per-LLO activation cost amortizes across larger campaign volumes. That projection is forward-looking; we will publish the converged number when the next cohort closes. Treating projections as measurements is the failure mode the FP Final Report's methodology section is designed to prevent.

Source: FP Final Report (Feb 2026), CHC §Cost Reduction. [1]
Q5 · CLP Stage 2 · I6 · N7 hard data ⟶ deliver#religious-leaders ⟶ insights#n7

What does the L in LLO actually do that a centralized model cannot?

The CLP Stage 2 vaccine campaign within CHC produced the cleanest piece of evidence Connect has on what locally-led organizations actually do that a centralized model cannot. Of 7 LLOs in the campaign2, 5–6 independently leveraged religious leaders, community leaders, or existing community relationships as part of their vaccine-hesitancy outreach. They did this without prompting from Dimagi, without it being in the Learn modules, and without it being on the supervisor checklist.

Specific examples: one FLW countered hesitancy with a personal example — "We were immunized, we have children." Another LLO had FLWs who had been recommended by the community itself, lending immediate credibility before a single conversation about vaccines. The pattern is locally-grounded knowledge that does not survive being centralized; if Dimagi had written a "religious-leader engagement module" and pushed it through the Learn app, it would have been a cartoon of what those LLOs already understood about their own communities.

This is the mechanism behind Connect's L-in-LLO. The platform tilts capability into the local partner: FLW recruitment, supervision schedules, the framing of outreach, the local language and idiom, the relationships with health-facility staff. Connect controls the workflow logic, the verification stack, and the payment guardrails. The split is what makes 44 LLOs in 14 months tractable without 44 Dimagi field offices.

The honest gap on the CHC page, as noted on Deliver: we do not yet have a structured framework for capturing what individual LLOs invent locally. The 5-of-7 finding was surfaced by an outside evaluator going to the field, not by the platform. Building a feedback loop that surfaces those inventions without flattening them is on the roadmap.

Source: CLP Stage 2 Report (Feb 2026), CHC vaccine campaign — 7 LLOs evaluated. [2]

Open questions on CHC

  1. Whether the cost-per-verified-visit advantage holds outside Nigeria's commodity bundle — IPA RCT 2026 ($1.5M, PRO Impact) will test.
  2. The 65% Trial Run conversion criteria are program-manager judgment calls today, not codified into a model an outside funder could replicate.
  3. Microplan execution failed in CAR's dispersed settlement and on inaccessible land. We removed it from one campaign entirely.
