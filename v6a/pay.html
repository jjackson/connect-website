The Lede

Traditional grant funding pays organizations based on projected activities — then hopes those activities happen. The incentive is to secure the next grant, not to verify what was delivered. Connect's model pays only after delivery is verified. The effect isn't just accounting: it's an alignment of motivations across an entire delivery chain.

Workers have direct financial motivation to deliver quality services — because low-quality work fails verification. LLOs have motivation to recruit and supervise well — because poor performance means no revenue. Funders bear no financial risk for undelivered work — and see cost-per-verified-outcome instead of cost-per-activity-planned.

The Three Pillars

How the money moves.

01

Performance-based

Workers are paid per verified service delivered. Every action has a clear, known value. KMC: ~$60 per completed case. CHC: $0.78–$1.30 per visit depending on commodities. Readers: per verified distribution. Action and value are coupled.

02

Direct mobile money

Payments go directly to workers' mobile money accounts. No intermediaries. No skimming. No cash logistics. Workers see exactly what they've earned in real-time through the Connect app.

03

End-to-end transparency

From funder to frontline, every dollar is tracked. Workers see their earnings. LLOs see disbursements. Funders see cost-per-verified-outcome. The same data serves every stakeholder.

The Numbers

Costs drop as the network grows.

Most organizations become less lean as they scale. Connect's architecture inverts the pattern. During the Founders Pledge grant period, the platform scaled to a rate capable of supporting millions of child visits per year without substantial growth in Dimagi's team. Per-visit cost fell 22 percent — from $2.20 to $1.70 — across 37 LLO contracts.

What workers report

Income, skills, and no displacement.

A GiveWell displacement study with 20 CCC-CHC workers documented the on-the-ground effect of performance-based pay. Workers reported substantial income increases — for most, CCC-CHC became a primary income source. They gained technology skills (CommCare, Google Maps), improved communication abilities, and increased confidence.

Critically, the study found no disruption to routine community health services. Campaign work is normal and expected in Nigeria's Community Health Volunteer system; other volunteers covered reduced hours during campaigns as they typically do. The income was additive, not substitutional.

The Alignment Argument

Pay-for-outcomes makes quality a shared incentive.

Traditional grant funding pays organizations based on projected activities — then hopes those activities happen. The incentive is to secure the next grant, not to verify what was delivered. Everything sits in tension: funders want impact, grantees want sustainability, workers want income. Each optimizes for its own outcome.

Connect's model pays only after delivery is verified. Everyone's incentive points the same direction: quality that passes verification. Workers earn more by doing better work. LLOs grow by recruiting and supervising better workers. Funders see costs fall per verified outcome, not rise.

The incentive isn't to look like you're delivering. It's to actually deliver.
