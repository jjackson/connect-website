Restoring near vision — door to door.

Presbyopia — age-related near vision loss — affects billions. In underserved communities, the lack of affordable reading glasses limits work, education, and everyday participation. Connect Readers brings screening and correction directly to adults 35+, in partnership with RestoringVision. This is the platform's first vision-care deployment.

The challenge

Presbyopia is treatable. The distribution is the problem.

Presbyopia affects billions globally. The correction is cheap — a pair of reading glasses. The obstacle is access: traditional vision care requires people to travel to clinics, which for rural or low-income adults is often prohibitive in time and cost. For many in underserved communities, the first pair of corrective lenses they ever receive is the one brought to their door.

Connect Readers inverts the conventional vision-care model. Instead of asking people to come to clinics, FLWs go to households — screening adults 35+, fitting appropriate-strength glasses, and completing the distribution on-site. The same platform that runs CHC and KMC handles training, verification, and payment. For recipients, the result is immediate: restored ability to read medication labels, work with small objects, participate in market activity, manage household accounts.

How it works

The Readers delivery stack.

01

Standardized screening

Mobile-guided near vision screening protocol. FLWs follow a consistent process across all communities. The app determines appropriate lens strength from the screening result.

02

Digital training

Same Learn-app pattern as other Connect programs — self-paced, offline, with an in-app knowledge test gating service delivery. Content covers screening technique and lens-fitting.

03

Door-to-door distribution

FLWs conduct household visits in assigned areas. Screen adults 35+. Fit glasses of appropriate strength. Complete the distribution on-site.

04

Verified performance payment

Each distribution verified via GPS and biometric data. FLWs receive mobile money payments for each verified screening and distribution.

05

Donated commodity supply

Glasses donated by RestoringVision. LLOs (eHA, C3HD) manage supply chain — receiving, allocating, inventory tracking. Cost scales with delivery, not with overhead.

06

Same four-layer verification

Biometric, GPS, photo capture, algorithmic — the same verification stack used in CHC. No program-specific build-out required.

Learning 01 · What we expect to learn · Readers, first vision-care deployment

Does a platform designed for child health port cleanly to a vision-care intervention?

Connect was built around a specific cluster of assumptions: short structured visits, measurable clinical outputs, a commodity or service delivered per household, and a verifiable confirmation event. CHC fits that pattern tightly. KMC, ECD, and now Readers are tests of whether the pattern generalizes. Readers is the farthest from the original shape, which is exactly what makes it interesting.

The clinical action is narrower than CHC — a single screening and a single distribution per recipient, rather than a multi-component care visit. The population is adults 35+ rather than children under 5. The commodity is a donated physical good with its own supply chain constraints rather than a consumable the LLO can stock freely. The household interaction is shorter, and the success criteria — did the right person get the right-strength glasses, and did they fit correctly — is different from a danger-sign screen.

What stays the same: the Learn app's training model, the CommCare workflow structure, the Trial Run activation approach, the four-layer verification stack, and the mobile money payment rail1. If Readers delivers at target quality under that shared stack, it validates a claim the rest of the site has been making: that the platform's value is general, not CHC-specific. If the delivery stumbles, the stumble will tell us where program-agnostic ends and program-specific begins. Both outcomes are useful.

This is a deliberately new shape of program on Connect. The case for the platform's generality rests in part on the result.

The first campaign is the evidence-generating event. Contract signing is the remaining gate; LLOs are finalized (eHA, C3HD), campaign planning is complete. When that data arrives, it joins the public evidence base and updates this page.

Learning 02 · Partnership model · Readers, RestoringVision donation

How does a donation-funded commodity model change the LLO economics?

In CHC, the commodities (Vitamin A, deworming medication, ORS/Zinc, MUAC tapes) are procured and budgeted alongside the service delivery. In Readers, the commodity — 15,000 pairs of reading glasses — is donated at scale by RestoringVision1. That changes the economic shape of the program in ways worth tracing.

First, the LLO's scope shifts. Under the Readers model, LLOs manage the supply chain directly — receiving the donated glasses, allocating stock to FLWs, tracking inventory against distribution records, and handling any commodity-level issues in the field. In CHC the commodity procurement is more centralized; in Readers it's more distributed. Whether that distributes risk or concentrates it at the LLO is one of the things we'll learn from the first campaign.

Second, the cost story is different. Without commodity costs in the per-visit number, the FLW + LLO + platform layers are cleaner to measure. The donated good takes the cost variability of the commodity out of the equation. The platform can demonstrate what it costs Connect to deliver a distribution, separately from what the commodity cost. For funders evaluating Connect's platform value, this is a useful signal — and for other donated-commodity programs considering the platform, it's a template.

The donated-commodity model is a meaningfully different economic shape than what CHC and KMC run on. Readers is where we find out what breaks, what transfers, and what Connect needs to add.

Third, the verification shape is slightly different. Biometric identity still matters (confirming the right person received a pair), but the MUAC-style clinical pattern detection used to catch fraud in CHC doesn't have an analogue here. We'll need to validate that GPS, photo capture, and inventory reconciliation together produce the same level of fraud resistance the CHC stack did. That's active work.

For RestoringVision, the partnership is a test of whether a verified delivery platform can responsibly absorb a 15,000-unit donated supply and produce transparent, beneficiary-level confirmation of distribution — something donated-commodity programs struggle to do at scale. If it works, it's a template for how other donated commodities (therapeutic food, water treatment supplies, menstrual products) might flow through Connect.

Why it matters

Near-vision correction is one of the highest-leverage micro-interventions in global health.

For adults in rural and low-income communities, presbyopia removes access to reading labels, counting money, sewing, identifying seeds or medicines, and any work that depends on close-range detail. Traditional vision programs ask these same adults to travel to clinics — which many cannot afford in time or cost. Door-to-door distribution with performance-based payment inverts the equation: the FLW goes to the community, and their compensation is tied to verified delivery.

The first pair of corrective lenses many Readers recipients will ever own is the one fitted in their home by an FLW they were probably introduced to by a neighbor. The platform's job is to make the mechanics — training, screening, fitting, verification, payment — smooth enough that the LLO can spend its attention on the community work that determines whether doors open.
