Dispatch 1 of 4 · The gap

KMC reduces neonatal mortality up to 40%. Global coverage is below 5%. The gap is post-discharge.

Kangaroo Mother Care reduces neonatal mortality by up to 40% in small or vulnerable newborns. The WHO recommends it for all newborns weighing 2000 grams or less. The mortality benefit is among the most robust findings in MNCH. And yet global KMC coverage remains below five percent of eligible infants. This is not a knowledge problem at the top of the system; the WHO recommends it, ministries of health endorse it, training curricula include it. It is an implementation problem at the bottom — specifically, at the moment a small or vulnerable newborn is discharged from a hospital and the family is sent home.

Hospitals discharge families within days. Structured at-home follow-up rarely exists. The implicit theory of care relies on the family knowing what to do, having time to do it, and recognising danger signs early enough to return to a facility. Each of those assumptions fails at high rates in the populations where neonatal mortality is concentrated. The post-discharge gap — the period between the family leaving the hospital and the next routine well-child visit — is where most preventable neonatal deaths happen.

Connect KMC closes that gap with four to five structured home visits in the first sixty days. The visits are not advisory. They are scheduled, equipped, verified, and paid against verification. Each visit captures weight, temperature, oxygen saturation, MUAC, and the family's adherence to skin-to-skin contact and exclusive breastfeeding. Danger signs auto-trigger referrals back to a facility, and the app prompts the FLW to follow up at the next visit. The scope claim is narrow: Connect KMC is the post-discharge layer. Hospital-side KMC initiation is somebody else's mandate. We build, verify, and pay for the home leg.

Dispatch 2 of 4 · Equipment as service

Every visit, the FLW arrives with a scale, thermometer, pulse oximeter, and measuring tape.

Every Connect KMC home visit, the FLW arrives with a weighing scale, thermometer, pulse oximeter, and measuring tape. The Connect app captures the readings. Danger signs — poor weight gain, hypothermia, infection — auto-trigger referrals, and the app prompts follow-up at the next visit. None of this equipment is exotic. The pulse oximeter is roughly thirty dollars retail. The scale and thermometer are commodity items. What is not commodity is having the equipment present at every visit, calibrated, with an FLW trained to use it and a workflow that captures the readings into the same data layer that pays for the visit.

The reason we are explicit about this is that the textbook description of KMC home visits often omits the equipment-availability question. "The FLW takes vitals" is a sentence that does a lot of quiet work. In practice, equipment availability across our six KMC LLO partners has been uneven; we don't yet have a published audit of equipment uptime, and we suspect — based on supervisor reports — that there are weeks where particular partners have one or two FLWs working with incomplete kits. Building the equipment-presence rate into the dashboard, and making it visible to LLO supervisors and to us, is the next iteration on this dispatch.

The deeper point: the post-discharge layer is a set of physical artifacts, a set of digital workflows, a set of trained workers, and a payment model that compounds them. Connect's job on this dispatch is to make the artifacts visible to the workflow and the workflow visible to the payment. A pulse oximeter that exists but is not in the FLW's bag at visit four does not appear in the platform telemetry. We are working on closing that loop.

Dispatch 3 of 4 · Guardrails

Four mandatory home visits is the rule. "First visit within 3 days" is a guardrail — and we're at 50%.

KMC has four mandatory home visits per Service Visit per Newborn (SVN) as a payment criterion. Workers do not get paid for an SVN unless the four visits are completed. That is a hard rule and the platform enforces it. The "first visit within three days of discharge" target is a different kind of rule. It is a guardrail — audited and tracked, but not enforced as a payment block. Current performance: fifty percent of cases against a seventy-percent target. We are not hitting it. We publish the gap.

The reason for the split is that punishment trains workers to falsify dates, not to hit timelines they cannot actually hit. If the three-day-from-discharge window were a payment block, FLWs would learn very quickly which date field to enter to get paid. The guardrail design is the operational expression of a principle we are trying to hold: platforms that punish guardrails don't get honest data. The price of holding it is that some real targets are missed in the dataset, including this one. We coach. We work on integrating hospital discharge notifications. We track. We do not punish.

The honest read on the fifty-percent number is that the gap is structural. Hospital discharge notifications are not yet integrated into the Connect data layer at most of our six KMC LLO partners. FLWs learn about a discharge through a phone call from the family or the hospital — sometimes the same day, sometimes day three, sometimes day five. The integration work is the lever. We are also not certain whether discharge-notification integration alone closes the gap, or whether the three-day window itself needs to be redesigned for the realities of post-discharge logistics in low-resource settings. That is the open question we close this dispatch with.

Dispatch 4 of 4 · Enrollment age

Average enrollment age is 12.8 days — under our 28-day target. The 3-day target is the harder one.

Across 4,005 KMC cases as of March 2026, we are hitting our twenty-eight-day enrollment target — average age at enrollment is 12.8 days. That is a target we set when the program launched, and the early performance is meaningfully below it. Worth saying: meeting a twenty-eight-day target is not the same as meeting a three-day target, and the reason we publish both is that the difference between them is the part we are trying to close. The twenty-eight-day target is reachable through standard FLW outreach and family-network contact. The three-day target requires the discharge-notification integration described in the previous dispatch.

There is a related question we have not yet answered: whether KMC FLWs sustain four or more visits per case at the five-thousand-plus scale. Our current published average is 3.4 visits per case against the 4-visit guardrail. The drop-off is concentrated at the fourth visit, where families have often returned to routine and the FLW has to re-establish contact. We are still gathering data on whether this is a structural ceiling or whether incentive design and visit-spacing changes close it. Either way, the four-visit guardrail is what the program design says is the right number; if the data converges differently, we will redesign the guardrail rather than redefining what counts as a visit.

Most KMC programs report enrollment-from-birth instead of enrollment-from-discharge. Discharge timing is the part of the program Connect is responsible for; birth timing is upstream of us. We choose the harder target because it is the one our intervention can move. Reporting against a target our intervention does not move is the failure mode the methodology is designed to prevent.
