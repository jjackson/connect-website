The challenge

Coverage gaps at the exact households equity requires.

Child health campaigns — vitamin A supplementation, deworming, MUAC screening for acute malnutrition, ORS for dehydration, vaccine promotion — are among the most cost-effective interventions in global health. The science is unambiguous. The delivery, at scale, is where the gap opens.

Conventional door-to-door campaigns hand FLWs a catchment area and a quota. Predictably, workers reach easier households first. The populations most at risk — the remote, the mobile, the marginalized — get missed, exactly the populations the campaign was designed to reach. Monitoring catches this after the money is spent, too late to correct.

CHC on Connect exists to close that gap with mechanics: GPS-enforced microplanning, biometric verification, algorithmic fraud detection, and performance-based contracts with locally-led organizations. The combination produces coverage, thoroughness, and cost numbers that most door-to-door campaigns cannot.

How it works

The CHC delivery stack.

01

LLO activation via EOI

Simple Expression of Interest process: brief scope doc + Google Form + Dimagi network. A single EOI produced ~100 applications.

02

Trial Run model

New LLOs get $3K setup for 3K visits. Evaluated on actual delivery. Scaled up or not continued based on field performance.

03

Digital training + AI coach

FLWs complete self-paced Learn modules, pass an in-app assessment, then use WhatsApp-based AI coach for ongoing questions.

04

Microplanned delivery

Grid overlay from Google Open Buildings + OSM. FLWs close every Delivery Unit in a Service Area before unlocking the next.

05

Four-layer verification

Biometric identity, GPS on every visit, photo capture, algorithmic fraud detection on submission patterns.

06

Mobile money payment

FLWs paid per verified visit. $0.78-$1.30 depending on commodity bundle. Direct to worker's mobile money account.

Question 01 · Partner selection

If you can't predict which LLOs will deliver, how do you build a network of 44 partners without betting the grant on each one?

Standard development contracting assumes you can vet your way to good partner selection: track record, team size, past-funder references, organizational maturity. Under the Founders Pledge grant, Connect contracted 37 LLOs under a different assumption — that pre-selection is an unreliable predictor of field performance and the only trustworthy signal is actual delivery at a small scale.

The mechanism is the Trial Run. New LLOs receive a small initial contract — $3,000 setup fee for 3,000 verified child visits. Payment is tied to actual delivery. If they can execute, they roll into a larger campaign. If they can't, financial exposure is capped and the next partner gets a turn quickly. Of 37 contracted: 24 entered Trial Runs; 10 underperformed and weren't continued; 3 went straight to larger campaigns based on prior evidence from inside the network; 17 went on to complete full campaigns.

The number that matters is that 10-of-37 underperformance rate. Under conventional diligence, many of those organizations had strong paper credentials. Under actual delivery, they stumbled. The network is stronger precisely because the filter is downstream, where the real signal is.

The Trial Run model has since been reused, without modification, for KMC, Readers, and chlorine dispensers. It's sector-agnostic because it doesn't rely on any domain-specific features of CHC — it relies on the idea that small, performance-based contracts are the cheapest form of evidence about an organization's operational fit.

Pre-selection is an expensive way to be wrong. The Trial Run is a cheap way to find out.

What's still unresolved: The 10-of-37 dropout rate is the current noise floor under this activation model. Pushing it lower without adding back the pre-selection overhead we deliberately cut is an open design question.

Sources: Founders Pledge Final Report (Feb 2026). Insight 1 in the public insights log.

Question 02 · Geographic coverage

How do you make sure the households workers skip get visited — when the easy households are always closer?

Without geographic enforcement, FLWs naturally cluster. Given a neighborhood and a visit quota, they reach accessible households and skip the harder ones. Under baseline Trial Run conditions, average coverage sat at 0.4 visits per estimated eligible child — convenience sampling at the exact moment equity was the goal.

Microplanning is our answer. Before each campaign, Dimagi overlays a grid onto the operational area using Google Open Buildings, OpenStreetMap, and Microsoft building data. Cells containing at least one building become Delivery Units (DUs). DUs group into Service Areas sized for 1-2 weeks of work per FLW. A worker cannot advance to the next Service Area until every DU in the current one has been closed — either with a completed visit or a confirmed "no eligible children here." The app enforces the sequence.

The data under this structure: 1.4 visits per estimated child in microplanned cells vs 0.4 without — a 3.5× increase in thoroughness. Population coverage in visited cells rose from 82-84% (experienced LLO, no microplan) to 94%. Reaching 95% of the population required 91% of cells under microplanning vs 72% of cells without — revealing how much coverage had previously concentrated in the easy cells. The benchmark comparison: a well-run non-Connect CommCare campaign sits at ~82% population coverage. The Connect CHC microplanned number is meaningfully higher.

The microplan doesn't work in every context. In dispersed settlement patterns, workers walked long distances to DUs with no actual buildings — the grid imposed friction the terrain didn't justify. Some DUs fell on inaccessible land (factory grounds, prison grounds), requiring audit-based exceptions. One campaign removed microplan execution entirely due to partner constraints. The grid is a tool that suits density. It doesn't suit everything.

Traditional campaigns optimize for visits. Microplanned campaigns optimize for coverage of the hard households. The two numbers diverge most where equity matters most.

What's still unresolved: Making the microplan model robust to sparse and irregular terrain is open work. The current approach is optimized for dense settlements; other terrain shapes need different geometry.

Sources: Founders Pledge Final Report (Feb 2026) coverage tables. Insight 2 in the public insights log.

Question 03 · Fraud

If paying on verified visits creates incentive to fabricate, how do you know your verification layer actually catches fraud — not just claims to?

Most fraud-detection claims in development are aspirational. They describe an approach and point at a clean audit. They rarely publish an adversarial test. For CHC, we ran one — because fraud resistance is a funder-critical question and the answer deserves to be a number.

We paid experienced FLWs to submit 100 fake visit records each, across three rounds. After each round, we coached the fakers on what had given them away. We offered bonuses to the top 25% most convincing fabricators — so the test was against motivated insiders, not naive ones. The research question: could statistical patterns alone, with no GPS and no photos, discriminate fake visits from real ones?

Using only three data elements — age, gender, and MUAC measurements — a Random Forest model achieved an AUC of 0.91 ± 0.09. Only 2.5% of real workers received fraud risk scores higher than even the lowest-scoring fabricator. Out of 33 adversarial fakers across the three rounds, we detected them with near-perfect discrimination from patterns alone.

That's statistical detection before GPS or photo verification enter the picture. In production, a fabricated CHC visit has to defeat four independent layers: biometric identity (no reusing beneficiary records), GPS + microplan constraints (the visit must be inside the expected DU on the expected day), photo capture at the point of delivery, and algorithmic anomaly detection on submission patterns. Each layer uses different signal. Coordinating a fake that defeats all of them requires more effort than actually doing the visit honestly — which is the design intent.

For funders, this means fraud risk is empirically bounded, not promised. For LLOs, the same verification data serves as auditable proof of delivery for their next grant. For workers, it means the honest ones aren't paying a trust tax for the few who might cheat.

Fraud detection isn't a feature on a datasheet. It's a measurable discrimination capacity. We published ours.

What's still unresolved: The adversarial tests ran under a specific commodity mix and geography. The model's discrimination holds in the CHC context; extending it to materially different visit schemas (e.g., KMC clinical screens) requires its own validation.

Sources: Founders Pledge Final Report (Feb 2026) fraud testing section. Insight 3 in the public insights log.

Question 04 · Cost at scale

If everyone else gets less cost-effective as they scale, why did Connect CHC invert the pattern?

The orthodoxy in development finance is that operational costs grow with program scale. More partners to manage, more FLWs to supervise, more logistics. Every Dimagi program before Connect followed that pattern. We expected CHC to follow it too. It didn't.

Under the Founders Pledge grant, cost per verified visit started at $2.20 and fell to $1.70 — a 22% reduction over 14 months, while visit volume grew toward 880,000+. Dimagi's team size during the same period was flat. In Nigeria (ORS/Zinc co-packs included): average $1.30/visit across 14 LLO contracts. Outside Nigeria (VAS + deworming only, no ORS): average $0.78/visit across 17 contracts. One Sierra Leone LLO delivered ORS/Zinc for $1.00/visit.

Three structural reasons this works. (1) LLOs operate with inherently low overhead — they're local, lean, and not carrying the fixed costs of a Dimagi-style central organization. (2) Performance-based payment forces costs to track actual delivery, not organizational size; an LLO that hires three extra staff without delivering three staff worth of visits gets paid the same as one that didn't. (3) Digital technology is integrated into every step — worker guidance in CommCare, automated verification, mobile money disbursement — so adding campaign volume compounds efficiency instead of compounding coordination overhead.

No single factor inverts the pattern. Stacked, they produce a different economic shape: one where scale creates cost reduction instead of cost creep. Setup fees during the grant averaged $0.40/visit; at larger campaign scale they're expected to fall toward $0.10/visit, accelerating the trend further.

This matters to funders for a specific reason: most development programs have a structural ceiling on cost-effectiveness. The more they scale, the more ops burden grows, until marginal cost-per-outcome stabilizes at a level that's hard to reduce further. Connect CHC shows what the other architecture looks like in practice — one of the few examples we can point to where the inversion is not just a projection but a measured result.

Most cost-effectiveness ceilings are structural. Removing the ceiling requires redesigning the architecture, not renegotiating the budget.

What's still unresolved: Whether the inversion continues at 10× current scale is a question the next grant period is designed to answer. Nothing in the mechanism suggests it should stop, but scale reveals things the pilot period couldn't.

Sources: Founders Pledge Final Report (Feb 2026) cost tables. Insight 4 in the public insights log.

Question 05 · Community trust

How do LLOs navigate vaccine hesitancy in communities that don't trust outside health workers?

Our first design instinct was to handle vaccine hesitancy through content. The Learn app would cover common myths, medical facts, counseling scripts. Read the module, pass the test, you're equipped. That wasn't wrong — and it also wasn't what actually moved the needle in the field.

In the CLP Stage 2 vaccine campaign, across 7 LLOs, we observed that 5-6 of them independently leveraged religious leaders, community leaders, or existing community relationships as part of their outreach — before we suggested it, before the app prompted it. One FLW countered hesitancy with a personal example: "We were immunized, we have children." Another LLO had FLWs who had been recommended by the community itself, lending credibility before any door was knocked on.

The platform didn't teach this. The LLOs already knew it. They operate in these communities; they have relationships with imams, with pastors, with village elders, with traditional birth attendants. When we saw 5-6 out of 7 LLOs doing this independently, the signal was clear: the real intervention isn't in the app — it's what locally-led organizations do with the platform when they operate in their own context.

This is why the L is load-bearing in LLO. Dimagi cannot centralize what an imam knows about trust in his community. We can't code it into the Learn module. What we can do is design the platform so the LLO's relationship work is the constraining resource — not paperwork, not fraud management, not supervision overhead. The more we take those burdens off, the more attention the LLO can spend on the relationships that actually determine whether a family opens the door.

This reframes the funder pitch for CHC. The platform's value is not that it trains FLWs to counter hesitancy with better facts. The platform's value is that it lets locally-led organizations do the work they already know how to do, at larger scale, with tighter verification, and with sustainable funding attached.

The platform's job is to enable the LLO's local knowledge, not replace it.

Sources: CLP Stage 2 Report (Feb 2026). Insight 6 in the public insights log.
