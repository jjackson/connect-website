The Challenge

250 million children are at risk of not achieving their developmental potential.

The figure comes from low- and middle-income countries, and it's a function of a narrow biological window. Ninety percent of brain development happens by age five. Nurturing care in the first thousand days of life — responsive caregiving, age-appropriate play, language-rich interaction — has documented effects on cognitive, language, motor, and socioemotional outcomes. The interventions work. The reach doesn't.

In most of the world, caregivers have limited access to evidence-based parenting guidance. Not because the content doesn't exist — because nobody's at their door with it. Connect ECD tests a direct answer to that reach problem: train frontline workers digitally, pair them with an AI coach for ongoing support, and send them home-to-home through the same LLO network that runs the CHC program.

What FLWs deliver

A structured parenting intervention at home.

Frontline Workers visit the homes of pregnant women and caregivers of infants up to five months old. Each visit follows a structured protocol: caregiver registration, ECD counseling, practical support on responsive caregiving, guidance on talk-and-play activities that support brain development, age-appropriate developmental activities, and a structured observation of caregiver-child interaction.

The current pilot runs three visits per caregiver. The expansion plan extends to eight to ten visits over twelve months — long enough for behavior change to set. The goal: 250,000 caregivers reached over 24 months, with 1,000 trained FLWs and a growing LLO network in Malawi, Mozambique, and Nigeria.

What makes this program unique

The AI coach, interacting daily.

ECD is the first Connect program where an AI coach is a core part of the service — not a supervision add-on. The coach, built on Dimagi's Open Chat Studio platform, interacts with each FLW daily. Workers are paid for the interaction.

The bot offers example scenarios to identify knowledge gaps. It walks workers through motivational interviewing techniques — open-ended questions, affirmations, reflective listening, summarizing. It asks about the day's visits and provides tailored feedback based on self-reflection. It is, in a meaningful way, the training that never ends.

The Pilot · Malawi, April–July 2025

76 FLWs. 9,510 visits. Three domains measured.

The first formal evaluation ran in Malawi over four months with 76 FLWs across a pilot geography. Baseline and endline surveys with 50 caregivers measured three outcome domains: attitude, knowledge, and observed behavior.

The attitude result was instructive on what the program doesn't need to do. Nearly all caregivers already held positive attitudes at baseline — toward talk-and-play, responsive caregiving, child correction — and those attitudes remained strong at endline. The barrier isn't belief. It's knowledge and practice.

"+33% increase in understanding the timing of rapid child development."

ECD Pilot Report · Malawi · 2026

Knowledge gains were significant. A 33 percent increase in understanding the timing of rapid child development. A 41 percent increase in ways to support mental development. Remaining gaps appeared specifically around language and thinking skills, where only 35 percent of caregivers answered correctly at endline (up from 26 percent) — a place where future visit content can focus.

Behavior change showed up in observation, which is the harder thing to move. Surveyors watched caregivers interact with their child and scored four subdomains. Teaching behaviors rose 21 percent. Encouragement of autonomy also rose 21 percent, though still sat lowest at 47 percent at endline. Affection and responsiveness, both already strong at baseline, improved further.

Quality of delivery

Digital training produced a workforce ready on day one.

The hardest question for any self-service training model is whether the workers it produces can actually do the job. The ECD pilot answered that question publicly.

Eighty-five percent of FLWs successfully progressed through digital learning into service delivery. Of those who reached the field, 88 percent scored above 70 percent on their first observed visits. Seventy-seven percent passed the supervised observation visits.

The Autonomous Learning process was validated through iterative experiments with roughly 400 FLWs. The model — digital-only training, in-app assessment, AI coach support, single supervised observation — generalizes to other Connect programs.

The economics

$7 to $18 per child, for an 8-session intervention.

Total cost for an 8-session ECD intervention sits at $7 per child in Nigeria and $18 in Mozambique. Cost assumptions are explicit: fair hourly rates at country standards (1.25 hours per visit), 40 percent LLO oversight top-up, 20 percent Dimagi platform top-up.

This is a full delivery cost, not a marginal one. It includes recruitment, training, supervision, payment, and platform infrastructure. For a parenting intervention with documented behavior change at endline, these are economics that scale.

Theory of change

Three research questions, in order.

01. Upskilling workers. Can FLWs effectively deliver a new intervention through digital-only training, validated by observed practical tests?

02. Improving caregiver skills. Can FLW-delivered behavior change improve responsive caregiving?

03. Improving child outcomes. Can FLW-delivered ECD improve cognitive, language, motor, and socioemotional development?

The first question has an answer (yes). The second has early answers (yes, on knowledge and observed teaching behavior). The third is the frontier — the one that scale and duration will answer.
