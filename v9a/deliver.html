Delivery is locally led. The platform gets out of the way.

Trained workers deliver proven services using an app that guides every interaction step-by-step — registration, counselling, screening, treatment. Locally-led organisations manage the people and permissions. The platform handles verification, payment, and scale.

Three pillars

How the delivery surface actually works.

01 / Guided workflows

Every step structured and captured.

CommCare guides FLWs through registration, counselling, screening, treatment. GPS, timestamps, and images captured at the moment of interaction. No paper forms. No end-of-day data entry.

02 / Locally-led implementation

LLOs run operations; the platform doesn't.

Government approvals, smartphone procurement, localisation, FLW recruitment and onboarding — all owned by LLOs. Connect provides the platform; they provide the relationships.

03 / Performance-based contracts

Paid per delivery, not per headcount.

LLOs are paid per verified service. This inverts the traditional model. Connect scaled to 44 LLOs across 11 countries with minimal growth in Dimagi's team because costs track delivery, not organisation size.

Field notes · Deliver

What the scaling model taught us.

Field note 01 · Multi-country, 2024–2637 LLOs contracted

LLO performance can't be pre-vetted. Only proven.

Standard procurement practice: vet potential partners before contracting. Check their track record. Assess team capacity. Evaluate organisational maturity. We did all of this before the Founders Pledge grant began. It turned out to be a poor predictor.

Past reputation, team size, organisational maturity — none of these reliably forecast whether a locally-led organisation would actually deliver once a campaign started. We had LLOs with strong résumés that faltered, and LLOs with thin track records that exceeded expectations.

What we thought

Careful pre-selection — résumés, references, capacity assessments — would filter to high performers.

What we learned

Pre-selection was a poor predictor. Only actual delivery, against live performance criteria, tells you whether an LLO works.

What we changed We invented the Trial Run model. New LLOs receive a small initial contract — $3,000 setup for 3,000 verified child visits. Because payment is performance-based, we bear minimal financial risk if the LLO fails. Of 37 LLOs contracted under this model: 24 entered Trial Runs; 10 underperformed and were not continued; 3 went straight to larger campaigns based on prior track record. The model is now sector-agnostic — reused for reading glasses distribution, KMC, and chlorine dispensers without modification.

Source: Founders Pledge Final Report, Feb 2026.

Field note 02 · Nigeria vaccine campaign, 20257 LLOs, Stage 2

LLOs navigate vaccine hesitancy with religious leaders. We didn't teach them this.

Connect's Learn module covers vaccine myths, counter-messaging, and counselling technique. We assumed that when FLWs read the content, they were equipped. They are. But what actually happened in the field was richer than what the training prescribed.

Of 7 LLOs in our CLP Stage 2 vaccine campaign, 5–6 independently leveraged religious leaders, community leaders, or existing community relationships as part of their outreach strategy. They did this before we asked. One FLW countered vaccine hesitancy with a personal example: "We were immunised, we have children." Another LLO had FLWs who had been recommended by the community itself, lending immediate credibility.

What we thought

Digital training covers the content. FLWs read it and they are equipped.

What we learned

The real intervention is not what's in the app. It's what locally-led organisations do with the platform in their own communities.

What we changed We stopped thinking of the app as the intervention. The app is infrastructure. The intervention is the relationship between a community and the people who serve it — which is exactly why the L in LLO is load-bearing. We cannot centralise what an LLO knows about its own community. Our job is to enable them, not replace them.

Source: CLP Stage 2 Report, Feb 2026.

Field note 03 · Central African Republic, 20252 LLOs dropped out

We documented a failure. Publishing it is part of the practice.

Connect's rapid partner activation model worked well across 10 countries. We assumed it was portable anywhere. In the Central African Republic, it wasn't.

Both contracted LLOs dropped out due to staffing turnover and connectivity issues. Remote coordination alone wasn't enough. Activating partners in fragile contexts — where infrastructure, political stability and talent availability are all stressed — likely requires dedicated in-country presence, not just a digital platform and a performance-based contract.

What we thought

The model travels. If it works in Nigeria and Kenya, it works anywhere.

What we learned

Fragile contexts require more than a platform. They require people on the ground.

What we changed We do not repeat the same activation approach in similarly difficult geographies without in-country presence. We publish the failure because real platforms publish what didn't work. The CAR experience is now shaping how we scope partners in fragile contexts.

Source: Founders Pledge Final Report, Feb 2026 — section on fragile contexts.
