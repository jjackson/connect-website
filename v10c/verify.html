Step 03 of 04 The verification layer

Every service confirmed. Not tracked. Not monitored. Verified.

Verification is how the system defines a delivery in the first place — not a checkpoint applied after the fact.

Four independent layers: biometric identity, geographic coverage, photo capture, and algorithmic fraud detection. Payment only triggers once the layers agree. The story below is how that came to be — including the day we paid people to defeat us.

Learning · 01 · Coverage geometry

Workers cluster. Microplans force them outward.

The hardest problem in field verification wasn't fraud — it was the invisible choice of which households to skip. The answer was to take that choice out of the worker's hands and put it in a grid.

The case for microplanning didn't begin with a technical decision; it began with a coverage table. We were looking at door-to-door CHC results across LLOs and seeing a pattern that wouldn't go away. Workers were hitting their visit quotas, the app was logging every interaction, the supervisors were green across the board — and yet population coverage was sitting at numbers that didn't reach where the program was designed to reach. Something was being skipped, and nothing in the data caught it because the data was a record of what got done, not a record of what got missed.

Early 2024 · The baseline approach"Assign a catchment. Set a quota. Trust the process."

Give an FLW a neighborhood and a visit target. They'll figure out who to reach. Operationally simple, widely used, and the default across community health programs — and a reasonable default for a program where the bottleneck is supply (workers, time, commodities) rather than allocation. We started with that frame, and waited to see what the field showed.

Mid-2024 · What the data showed0.4 visits per eligible child.

Without geographic enforcement, FLWs averaged 0.4 visits per estimated eligible child — convenience sampling at the exact moment equity was the goal. The populations systematically missed were those with lower baseline health coverage: more remote settlements, more mobile families, the harder-to-reach end of every catchment. Nobody was misbehaving; they were doing exactly what the system rewarded. Reach a target, log a visit, move on.

At the exact moment equity was the goal, we were producing convenience sampling.

Convenience-sampling coverage: 0.4 visits / child

Late 2024 · The microplan interventionBuild the grid. Force saturation.

Before a campaign, we overlay a grid onto the operational area using Google Open Buildings, OpenStreetMap, and Microsoft building data. Cells with at least one building become Delivery Units (DUs). DUs group into Service Areas sized for 1–2 weeks of work per FLW. A worker cannot advance to the next Service Area until every DU in the current one is closed — either with a completed visit or a confirmed "no eligible children present." The app enforces the sequence.

The choice this removes is the one that mattered: the worker can no longer pick which households are visited and which are skipped. That choice is now made by geometry, not preference, and the geometry was built before anyone arrived in the field.

2025–2026 · What the grid produced1.4 visits per child. 94% population coverage.

1.4 visits per estimated child in microplanned cells, vs 0.4 without — a 3.5× increase in thoroughness. Population coverage in visited cells jumped from 82–84% (experienced LLO, no microplan) to 94%. To reach 95% of the population, microplanned campaigns needed 91% of cells; non-microplanned campaigns needed 72% — revealing how much coverage had previously concentrated in the easy cells. The benchmark comparison: a well-run non-Connect CommCare campaign sits at ~82% population coverage. The Connect CHC microplanned number is meaningfully higher.

Coverage in visited cells: 82–84% → 94% · thoroughness: 3.5×

What didn't workDispersed settlements and inaccessible land.

Microplans failed in dispersed settlement patterns — workers walked long distances to DUs with no actual buildings; the grid imposed friction the terrain didn't justify. Some DUs fell on inaccessible land (factory grounds, prison grounds), requiring audit-based exceptions. One campaign removed microplan execution entirely due to partner constraints. The grid is a tool that suits density; it doesn't suit everything. Open work: making the approach robust to sparse and irregular terrain.

Learning · 02 · Fraud as a test case

We paid people to defeat our fraud detection. We watched them fail.

Most fraud-detection claims in the sector are aspirational. We ran the adversarial study — paid experienced workers to fabricate convincingly — so we could report a number instead of a promise.

0.91
AUC ± 0.09

A Random Forest model on three data elements — age, gender, and MUAC measurements — separated paid fabricators from real workers at AUC 0.91 ± 0.09. Only 2.5% of real workers received fraud risk scores higher than even the lowest-scoring fabricator.

Fraud-detection claims in development are usually presented as a feature list. Biometrics. GPS. Photo capture. Anomaly detection. Each item suggests a defense, but none of them tells you how strong the defense is. We had been making this kind of presentation ourselves, and a question kept coming back: what happens if a motivated insider tries to fake data? Could we catch them with statistics alone — before GPS or photos even mattered? We did not have a number we could quote. So we went and got one.

Pre-test · The comfortable assumption"Our verification stack handles fraud."

We had biometrics, GPS, photo capture. On paper, the stack looked like more than enough. The question we hadn't answered honestly was the only one a careful funder would ask: show me the test that broke it. We didn't have one. Most of the sector doesn't, and the ones that do are usually qualitative.

The adversarial studyThree rounds. 33 fakers. Escalating coaching.

We paid experienced FLWs to submit 100 fake visit records each and ran three rounds. After each round, we coached the fakers on what had given them away — meaning the next round of fabrication started from a better-informed adversary. We offered bonuses to the top 25% most convincing fabricators, so the test was against motivated insiders, not naive ones. The research question: could statistical patterns alone, with no GPS and no photos, discriminate fake visits from real ones?

Coached rounds: 3 · adversarial fakers: 33 · bonus on top 25%

The resultAUC 0.91 ± 0.09 from three fields alone.

A Random Forest model trained on three data elements — age, gender, and MUAC measurements — achieved an AUC of 0.91 ± 0.09. Only 2.5% of real workers received fraud risk scores higher than even the lowest-scoring fabricator. Said the other way: 97.5% of real workers scored cleaner than even the most convincing fabricator we hired. This is statistical discrimination alone, before GPS and photo verification kick in.

97.5% of real workers scored cleaner than even the lowest-scoring fabricator we paid.

What it meansFraud detection as an empirical layer, not a promise.

A fabricated visit on Connect has to defeat all four independent layers — biometric identity, GPS plus microplan constraints, photo capture, and algorithmic anomaly detection. Each layer uses different signal. Coordinating a fake that defeats all of them requires more effort than delivering the visit honestly, which is the design intent.

The point of running the adversarial study was not to prove the model perfect. It was to give funders a number they could reason about, instead of an architecture they had to take on faith. We have it. We publish it. The number does not improve the verification stack on its own — it just reduces the asymmetry between what the platform can demonstrate and what its critics can suspect.

Learning · 03 · Design philosophy

Guardrails are not hard stops. The data changes when you confuse them.

Platforms that punish missed guardrails don't get honest data. Separating payment criteria from audit signals was one of our most important architectural decisions — and it took a while to see why.

Almost every platform that pays for outcomes has the same instinct on day one: if a worker misses a quality target, withhold payment. It feels like accountability. It pattern-matches with how every other procurement and audit system in the world works. We had it too. The decision to resist that instinct, and to draw a sharper distinction between what triggers a non-payment and what triggers a conversation, took a while to land — and it lands harder once you watch what happens when you don't draw it.

The initial instinct"If they miss the target, don't pay."

The first version of the rule looks fair. Visit must happen within three days of discharge. Miss the window, no payment. The FLW knows the rule, the system enforces it, the funder gets crisp numbers. We considered exactly this design.

Why we didn'tPunishing a guardrail corrupts the data.

If missing the 3-day window costs an FLW money, the FLW will falsify the visit date. The platform will then show 100% compliance. We will have no idea what is actually happening, because the measurement itself changed the behavior. The guardrail will have measured nothing — and worse, it will have produced a confident number that is wrong in a direction we can no longer see. This is the fundamental problem with conflating a signal with a sanction.

A platform that doesn't punish missed guardrails gets the real data it needs to improve the program.

The distinction we drewPayment criteria vs guardrails.

Payment criteria are hard. A visit must meet these or it isn't paid: identity verified, GPS on-site, minimum visit duration, the right type of interaction logged. Guardrails are soft. They trigger audits and performance conversations. They don't block payment. The distinction is small on paper and large in behavior — it determines whether the platform sees what actually happens, or only what workers want us to see.

Two classes of check: payment-blocking vs audit signal

2026 · Live exampleKMC first-visit-within-3-days: audited, not enforced.

In the KMC program, four mandatory home visits per SVN is a payment criterion. The guardrail "first visit within 3 days of discharge" is currently at 50% compliance against a 70% target. That's a real number — because we chose to get it instead of getting a fake 100%. We're working on it through LLO coaching, scheduling support, and discharge-notification integration. Not through punishment.

The 50% number is uncomfortable. It looks like a failure on a marketing site. It is on a marketing site precisely because the alternative — moving it to 100% by changing the incentive shape — would tell us less, not more. The whole point of the architecture is to keep the measurement honest enough that the program manager can act on it.

What we don't know yet.

  1. The 97.5% adversarial detection is from paid-bonus testing in CHC. Production false-positive rates against unmotivated fabrication aren't yet published — we will when we have a clean cohort comparison.
  2. Microplans failed in dispersed settlement patterns. We don't yet have a clean threshold for when settlement density makes microplanning counterproductive.
  3. Photo verification quality degrades on low bandwidth. False-positive rate on the photo layer in low-bandwidth deployments isn't yet published.
