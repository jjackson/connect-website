layer 01

Biometric identity

PersonalID confirms that real individuals received real services. No duplicates. No ghosts. The same record can't be claimed twice under different names.

layer 02

GPS + microplanning

Geolocation confirms visits happened in the right places. Microplan execution forces coverage — workers must close every Delivery Unit in their Service Area before moving on.

layer 03

Photo + algorithmic

Image capture at visit time. Statistical models analyze submission patterns to flag anomalies. Fabrication has to beat all four layers, not just one.

Workers cluster. Microplans force them outward.

The hardest problem in field verification wasn't fraud — it was the invisible choice of which households to skip. The answer was to take that choice out of the worker's hands and put it in a grid.

early 2024 · the baseline approach

"Assign a catchment. Set a quota. Trust the process."

Give an FLW a neighborhood and a visit target. They'll figure out who to reach. Operationally simple, widely used, and the default across community health programs. The model assumes coverage is a property of effort. It isn't.

mid-2024 · what the data showed

0.4 visits per eligible child

Without geographic enforcement, FLWs averaged 0.4 visits per estimated eligible child — cherry-picking accessible households and skipping the harder ones. The populations systematically missed were those with lower baseline health coverage. At the exact moment equity was the goal, we were producing convenience sampling.

visits_per_child=0.4 · mode=convenience_sampling
late 2024 · the microplan intervention

Build the grid. Force saturation.

Before a campaign, we overlay a grid onto the operational area using Google Open Buildings, OpenStreetMap, and Microsoft data. Cells with at least one building become Delivery Units. DUs group into Service Areas sized for 1-2 weeks of work. A worker cannot advance to the next Service Area until every DU in the current one is closed — either completing the visits or confirming no eligible children are present.

2025-2026 · what the grid produced

1.4 visits per child. 94% population coverage.

1.4 visits per estimated child in microplanned cells, vs 0.4 without — a 3.5× increase in thoroughness. Population coverage in visited cells jumped from 82-84% to 94%. To reach 95% of the population, microplanned campaigns needed 91% of cells; non-microplanned needed 72% — revealing how concentrated coverage was in the easy cells.

coverage=82-84% → 94% · cells_for_95%=91% vs 72%
what didn't work

Dispersed settlements and inaccessible land

Microplans failed in dispersed settlement patterns — workers walked long distances to DUs with no actual buildings. Some DUs fell on inaccessible land (factory grounds, prison grounds). One campaign removed microplan execution entirely due to partner constraints. The grid is not universal; it's a tool that suits density. Open work: making the approach robust to sparse or irregular terrain.

pre-test · the comfortable assumption

"Our verification stack handles fraud."

We had biometrics, GPS, photo capture. On paper, the stack looked like more than enough. The question we hadn't answered honestly: what if a motivated insider tried to fake data? Could we catch them with statistics alone — before GPS or photos even mattered?

the adversarial study

Three rounds. 33 fakers. Escalating coaching.

We paid experienced FLWs to submit 100 fake visit records each. Ran three rounds. After each round, we coached the fakers on what had given them away. We offered bonuses to the top 25% most convincing fabricators. The escalating coaching is the part that matters: we stress-tested against motivated, learning adversaries, not naïve ones.

coached_rounds=3 · fabricators=33 · records_each=100
the result

AUC 0.91 ± 0.09 from three fields alone

A Random Forest model trained on three data elements — age, gender, and MUAC measurements — achieved an AUC of 0.91 ± 0.09. Only 2.5% of real workers received fraud risk scores higher than even the lowest-scoring fabricator. 97.5% of real workers scored cleaner than the worst paid faker. This is statistical discrimination alone, before GPS and photo verification kick in.

what it means

Fraud detection as an empirical layer, not a promise

A fabricated visit on Connect has to beat all four independent layers — biometric identity, GPS + microplan constraints, photo capture, and statistical pattern detection. Each layer uses different signal. Coordinating a fake that defeats all of them requires more effort than delivering the visit honestly. That's the design intent: make honest delivery the cheapest path.

progression_of_thinking // design_philosophy

Guardrails are not hard stops. The data changes when you confuse them.

Platforms that punish missed guardrails don't get honest data. Separating payment criteria from audit signals was one of our most important architectural decisions — and it took a while to see why.

the initial instinct

"If they miss the target, don't pay."

The first instinct when a quality target isn't met — first visit within 3 days of discharge, for example — is to withhold payment. It feels fair. It seems like accountability. We considered it.

why we didn't

Punishing a guardrail corrupts the data

If missing the 3-day window costs an FLW money, they'll falsify visit dates to hit it. The platform will show 100% compliance. We'll have no idea what's actually happening. The guardrail will have measured nothing, because the measurement itself changed the behavior. This is the fundamental problem with conflating a signal with a sanction.

the distinction we drew

Payment criteria vs guardrails

Payment criteria are hard — a visit must meet these or it isn't paid (identity verified, GPS on-site, minimum visit duration). Guardrails are soft — they trigger audits and performance conversations, but don't block payment. The distinction is small on paper and large in behavior: it determines whether the platform sees what actually happens or only what workers want us to see.

classes=[payment, audit] · role=enforce vs observe
2026 · live example

KMC first-visit-within-3-days: audited, not enforced

In the KMC program, 4 mandatory home visits per SVN is a payment criterion. The guardrail "first visit within 3 days of discharge" is currently at 50% compliance against a 70% target. That's a real number — because we chose to get it instead of getting a fake 100%. We're working on it through LLO coaching, scheduling support, and discharge notification integration. Not through punishment.

A platform that doesn't punish missed guardrails gets the real data it needs to improve the program.

what we don't know yet // page=verify
  • The 97.5% adversarial detection is from paid-bonus testing in CHC. Production false-positive rates against unmotivated fabrication aren't yet published.
  • Microplans failed in dispersed settlement patterns. We don't yet have a clean threshold for when settlement density makes microplanning counterproductive.
  • Photo verification quality degrades on low bandwidth. False-positive rate on the photo layer in low-bandwidth deployments isn't yet published.
