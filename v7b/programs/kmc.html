Kangaroo Mother Care

Closing the post-discharge gap for small and vulnerable newborns. Structured home visits by trained frontline workers, aligned with WHO 2022 KMC guidance.

3,600+SVNs receiving home-visit follow-up (March 2026)

The challenge

Every year, an estimated 2.3 million newborns die during the first 28 days of life, accounting for nearly half of all deaths in children under five. Most of these are small and vulnerable newborns — 80% with low birth weight, two-thirds preterm.

Kangaroo Mother Care — continuous skin-to-skin contact, exclusive breastfeeding, and close follow-up — reduces neonatal mortality by up to 40%. The WHO recommends KMC for all newborns weighing ≤2000g at birth.

Yet KMC coverage remains below 5% for eligible infants globally. Hospitals discharge families within days, returning them home with little structured follow-up. This is the post-discharge gap. Connect KMC exists to close it.

How it works

Five components. Four visits minimum. Sixty days.

01

Identifying SVNs

LLOs partner with health facilities. FLWs meet caregivers before discharge, explain KMC, invite them to enroll. Early identification is the precondition for everything else.

02

Home visits

FLWs conduct follow-up visits at home, eliminating travel for caregivers. Minimum 4 visits per SVN; program pays for up to 5; FLWs may visit more for high-need cases. All within 60 days; at least one at 28+ days.

03

Counseling

FLWs help caregivers practice sustained skin-to-skin contact with a KMC wrap the program provides. They counsel on exclusive breastfeeding, troubleshoot feeding, and provide hygiene guidance to prevent infection.

04

Screening for danger signs

FLWs bring a weighing scale, measuring tape, pulse oximeter, and thermometer to every visit. Danger signs flag in the app and trigger referrals. The app prompts follow-up on referrals at the next visit.

05

Emotional & social support

Many SVN mothers experience stigma and isolation. FLWs offer encouragement and practical advice, building confidence and trust — an explicit, paid-for activity rather than a side effect.

06

Payment

$60 per verified completed case, split among LLO, FLW, and a 20% Connect platform fee. Payment is triggered by digitally-verified home-visit completion — no separate reporting cycle.

Learning 01 · Program design

Why the 4-visit minimum is a payment criterion, but "first visit within 3 days" is a guardrail.

KMC has a long evidence base and a specific clinical rhythm. WHO guidance points toward early, frequent post-discharge contact — the first visit ideally within 3 days of hospital discharge, the full schedule spanning at least 28 days to cover the neonatal period. This is well under the 28-day neonatal window target, meaning most SVNs are captured while program support can still have clinical impact., enrollment within 3 days of discharge at 50% against a 70% targetKMC Conceptual Model · March 2026 (guardrail metric)Missed discharge notification from facilities is the largest single cause of 3+ day enrollment delay. Connect is working with partner hospitals on discharge-notification integration rather than penalising FLWs for misses caused upstream., and average 3.4 FLW visits per case against the 5-visit guardrailKMC Conceptual Model · March 2026Note: the program pays for up to 5 visits, but the minimum to be paid at all is 4. 3.4 is the current average across all active cases. High-need cases regularly receive more than 5 visits; the average includes cases closed early for clinical reasons.. Not every metric is a pass. The ones that aren't — the guardrails — are the ones we can learn from.

The program's payment criterion is specific: four mandatory home visits per SVN, each with completed screening, each verified via GPS and biometric ID. Miss any of those, and the case does not pay out. No exceptions. The guardrail, by contrast, is the "first visit within 3 days of discharge" metric. It is audited, not enforced. The distinction matters because enforcing it would produce exactly the data corruption we are trying to avoid: workers falsifying visit dates to hit the 3-day window would give us a cleaner-looking number and destroy the signal we need to improve discharge-notification integration. Early discharge rate, currently at 7.6% against a 5% targetKMC Conceptual Model · March 2026Early discharge means the case closed before the 4-visit minimum was met — usually due to re-hospitalisation, death, or loss-to-follow-up. Treated as a guardrail because its causes are mostly outside FLW control and we need honest reporting on them., sits in the same category — it is an honest number precisely because workers are not punished for producing it. The program-design consequence is that Connect KMC runs with public guardrail misses and no incentive to hide them.

Learning 02 · Coverage

A 40%-mortality-reduction intervention with sub-5% coverage is not a science problem.

The clinical evidence for Kangaroo Mother Care is settled. The WHO 2022 guidance, which Connect KMC is aligned with, is unambiguous: KMC should be provided to all newborns weighing ≤2000g at birth. Continuous skin-to-skin contact, exclusive breastfeeding, and structured early follow-up can reduce neonatal mortality by up to 40%WHO KMC Guidance · 2022 · Meta-analysisPooled estimate across multiple randomized and observational studies. The 40% figure is widely cited in WHO and Every Newborn Action Plan documentation and represents the upper bound of clinically-observed effect., lower infection rates, and increase exclusive breastfeeding. Yet KMC coverage remains below 5%Every Newborn Action Plan coverage reviewSub-5% coverage is a global estimate. Facility-based KMC initiation has higher rates in some tertiary centers, but continuation of KMC after discharge — the step that actually determines outcomes — collapses coverage figures in nearly every context we've reviewed. for eligible infants globally. Hospitals initiate KMC and then discharge families within days. The intervention that saves lives happens at home, and the system for ensuring it happens at home rarely exists.

This is a delivery architecture problem, not a medical one. A program that wants to close the coverage gap has to do three things: identify eligible SVNs (usually at facility), make continuation affordable for families (home visits, not clinic return), and pay someone competent to deliver each visit with fidelity. Connect KMC operates across Uganda, Kenya, India, and Nigeria in partnership with six LLOs — PIPNU (Uganda), Nama Wellness, Global Health Innovations, Ansh, BERI, and eHealth AfricaConnect KMC program page · ConfluenceThese partners were selected based on prior experience with newborn health programs and existing facility partnerships. PIPNU is the most publicly-visible — their executive director gave a Ugandan National TV interview in August 2025 explaining the program (see Resources below). — each managing facility partnerships and FLW recruitment locally. The program's scale to 5,000+ enrolled cases and 3,600+ SVNs in active follow-up is modest against the global gap, but the unit economics — $60 per verified completed case — make the scale question answerable rather than aspirational.

Early performance is consistent with the program's design intent. Average age at enrollment is comfortably under the 28-day neonatal window. The 4-visit minimum is being met for cases that complete. The guardrail misses — timing of first visit, occasional early discharge — are where the operational work now sits.

Learning 03 · Delivery mechanics

What an FLW carries to a KMC home visit, and why each piece is there.

A Connect KMC visit is not a conversation. It is a structured clinical encounter conducted in a home. An FLW arrives with a portable weighing scale, thermometer, pulse oximeter, and measuring tapeConnect KMC Overview · FLW EquipmentThe equipment set is standardised across all partner LLOs. Commodities are procured by LLOs using program funds — Connect does not centrally ship equipment, which would add logistics cost and reduce LLO ownership., along with a KMC wrap supplied by the program and a smartphone running the Connect Deliver app. Each item is there for a specific measurement in the screening protocol: weight gain trajectory (the strongest single signal of post-discharge thriving), temperature (hypothermia risk is elevated in SVNs), oxygen saturation (infection screening), and head circumference.

The app's role is not to replace the FLW's judgement but to structure it. Each visit walks through the same sequence: weight and vitals, KMC positioning observation, breastfeeding technique check, maternal mood and support screening, danger-sign review, and any referrals. GPS and timestamps are captured automaticallyVerification layer · standard across ConnectThis is the same verification stack documented on the Verify page: biometric ID, GPS, photo where applicable, plus algorithmic pattern detection. KMC visits additionally capture the full screening-rubric payload which is more data-rich than typical CHC visits.; images where appropriate; and the full screening payload submits as the structured record that triggers verification and payment. If the FLW identifies a danger sign they cannot address at home — severe illness, feeding failure, suspected sepsis — the app prompts a referral to the nearest facility and then prompts follow-up at the next visit to close the referral loop.

The combination is what makes the scale possible. Equipment costs per FLW are modest and front-loaded. The app makes a trained-but-not-clinical worker reliably execute a clinical rubric. The verification stack makes the work payable without requiring a separate audit. The FLW is compensated directly via mobile money for each verified visit. Nothing in this description is novel individually; the novelty is the composition.
