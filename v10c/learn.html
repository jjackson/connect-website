Step 01 of 04 The training layer

Workers train on a phone, then prove competence — before delivery.

The training stack evolved in public. Version one was a knowledge test. It wasn't enough.

What shipped is a five-layer model — digital learning, assessment gate, daily AI coaching, peer practice, and supervised observation — validated against ~400 FLWs across iterative experiments. The story below is how that came to be, and what's still open.

Learning · 01 · Layered training

The knowledge test wasn't enough. We had to build around it.

Our first training version assumed that if a worker could pass an in-app test, they could deliver the visit. They couldn't. The gap between knowledge and competence turned out to be the hardest problem in the Learn stack — and the most interesting.

The original Learn stack was deliberately spare. A worker logged in to the CommCare app, completed self-paced video and text modules, then took a multiple-choice knowledge assessment. If they scored above the threshold, they were cleared to deliver. That was the whole gate. We trusted the test because the test was easy to score and easy to scale, and because the alternative — embedded skill assessment — sounded like the kind of thing that would never survive contact with a thousand FLWs across eleven countries.

Early 2024 · The starting assumption"Pass the test, start the work."

We built a self-paced digital course inside the Learn app, followed by a multiple-choice knowledge assessment. If the FLW scored above threshold, they were cleared to deliver. Simple, scalable, measurable — and it produced clean, defensible numbers that funders could read at a glance. Worker certified, system green, money flows.

Late 2024 · What the field showedCompetence doesn't equal knowledge.

Workers who aced the test still struggled with the nuances of in-person counseling. Handling a skeptical caregiver. Recognizing subtle danger signs. Practicing motivational interviewing instead of reciting a script. Supervised observations revealed an entire category of skill that the multiple-choice format couldn't reach — and didn't even pretend to.

We were selecting for people who could memorize facts, not for people who could make a hesitant mother feel heard.From an early supervisor debrief, CHC pilot

The shape of the problem became clearer once we pulled apart what the test was actually measuring. It captured recall well: dosing schedules, danger signs, eligibility criteria. It captured nothing about the encounter — the rhythm of conversation, the right moment to ask a follow-up, the willingness to slow down when a caregiver got quiet. The gap between the certified worker and the competent worker was, in retrospect, exactly the gap a written test cannot close.

Observed gap on every early cohort: knowledge > behavior

2025 · What we changedA five-layer training stack.

Instead of strengthening the test, we added layers around it. Workers now complete self-paced digital learning, pass the in-app knowledge test, interact daily with an AI coach bot (built on Dimagi's Open Chat Studio and accessed via WhatsApp), practice with peers in structured sessions, and finally complete one supervised observation visit rated by an LLO supervisor. Each layer addresses something the previous one missed — the test confirms recall, the AI coach builds judgment, peer practice rehearses the encounter, and the supervised visit is the actual gate.

The AI coach specifically fills the space between text-based knowledge and real encounters: scenario-based practice, motivational-interviewing drills, daily reflection on real visit experiences, and conversational identification of knowledge gaps. In the Kenya CHC pilot, that coach handled 500 of 511 FLW queries — 97.8% — without human escalation, with a five-second average response time. Independent evaluation pegged accuracy at 88.7% and usefulness at 81.5%. Those are not perfect numbers; they are publishable ones.

Layers added to the gate: 4 new ones · AI coach handle rate: 97.8%

2026 · What the data says nowValidated against ~400 FLWs across programs.

85% of FLWs progress from training to service delivery. 88% score above 70% on their first observed visits. 77% pass the supervised observation gate. This isn't an ideal test environment — it's ECD, CHC, and KMC rollouts across multiple LLOs and countries. The stack holds. We now treat the knowledge test as one of five signals, not the signal.

The deeper architectural shift is harder to capture in a number. The platform stopped pretending it could verify competence cheaply. Once we accepted that competence has a cost, we could design a stack that actually pays it — and the resulting model has held across three programs and four countries without redesign.

Sources: ECD Overview (Feb 2026), CLP Stage 2 Report (Feb 2026), Founders Pledge Final Report. Insight 8 in the public insights log.

Learning · 02 · Target the right gap

Caregivers already believed. Training had to move knowledge and behavior instead.

A good training program addresses the actual gap, not the one in the proposal. For ECD, baseline data told us we were aiming at the wrong thing — and reallocating the intervention mattered more than expanding it.

The proposal we wrote in 2023 followed the standard playbook for an early-childhood-development home-visiting intervention: build caregiver attitudes about responsive caregiving, build caregiver knowledge of child development, and build caregiver behaviors that act on both. The literature supports targeting all three. The funders who reviewed the proposal expected to see all three. The Learn app we built had modules for all three.

Q2 2025 · Malawi baseline50 caregivers already scored at ceiling on attitudes.

In the Malawi pilot baseline survey of 50 caregivers across 76 FLWs, nearly all caregivers already agreed that talk-and-play supports brain development, endorsed responsive caregiving, and rejected harsh correction. Attitude scores were high before the intervention started — and stayed high after it finished. We were planning to convince people of something they already believed, and the intervention was sized to produce a movement that the data said wasn't available.

We were planning to convince people of something they already believed.

Q3 2025 · Endline resultsThe movement was in knowledge and observed behavior.

Where the intervention actually moved the needle: +33% on knowledge of rapid child development timing, +41% on ways to support mental development, +21% observed improvement in teaching, +21% observed improvement in encouraging autonomy. Attitudes didn't need to move. Behavior did. The intervention was producing the right kind of change — but the headline metric we were resourced to chase wasn't the one carrying the work.

Knowledge gains: +33% to +41% · observed behavior gains: +21% across two domains

2026 · What we changedFrom 3 visits to 8–10, focused on practice.

We're expanding the pilot from 3 visits to 8–10 visits across 12 months — not because caregivers need more convincing, but because behavior change requires sustained practice and feedback. Digital training content shifted weight away from attitude modules toward knowledge scaffolding and behavior rehearsal. The program is now evidence-guided, not just evidence-based.

The hardest sub-domain remains encouragement of autonomy: the proportion of caregivers we observe actively letting a young child make small choices, attempt small tasks, and lead small interactions. It sat at 47% at endline. Whether 8–10 visits will move it, or whether it requires a different intervention design entirely, is the open question for the next cohort.

Sources: ECD Overview (Feb 2026), Malawi pilot data (April–July 2025, 76 FLWs, 9,510+ visits, 50 caregivers). Insight 7 in the public insights log.

What we don't know yet.

  1. Whether the AI coach or peer practice contributes more to the 77% supervised-observation pass rate — we have not yet published the decomposition.
  2. Whether the 97.8% AI-coach handle rate from the Kenya CHC pilot holds in non-English-language deployments.
  3. Encouragement-of-autonomy sits at 47% endline — the hardest ECD sub-domain. Whether 8–10 visits will move it, or it needs a different design entirely, is open.
