The question · 1

Why does ECD fit Connect's model at all?

Early childhood development interventions depend on sustained, structured home visits during the first few years of life. The per-visit content is behavioral — talk-and-play routines, responsive-caregiving coaching, observed practice of teaching behaviors — and the intervention only works if the caregiver actually changes what they do with the child every day. Measuring that is hard. Paying for it without fraud or rote compliance is harder. Connect's LDVP architecture is a fit for ECD specifically because the platform was built to handle intervention types where quality is as load-bearing as quantity.

The question · 2

What happens when the intervention aims at the wrong variable?

◆ Scope · ECD, Malawi pilot

You get the Malawi pilot. The intervention as designed was aimed at building positive caregiver attitudes about responsive caregiving and talk-and-play. The 50-caregiver baseline showed that caregivers already held positive ECD attitudes. They agreed talk-and-play supports brain development. They endorsed responsive caregiving. The attitude scales were near ceiling before we started. The intervention was ready to move a variable that did not need moving.

Where the pilot actually produced results was in knowledge and observed behavior. Knowledge of timing of rapid child development moved +33%. Knowledge of ways to support mental development moved +41%. Observed teaching behavior and observed encouragement of autonomy each moved +21%. Attitudes did not move, because they did not need to.

What we changed. The 3-visit pilot is expanding to 8-10 visits across 12 months — not because we need more time to convince caregivers ECD matters (they know), but because behavior change takes sustained practice. Learn content for ECD now weights knowledge reinforcement and behavioral rehearsal over attitudinal framing. The AI coach drills the caregiver-facing behaviors directly. The intervention is evidence-guided, not merely evidence-based.

One behavior we haven't moved as much as we want to: encouragement of autonomy, 47% at endline. It's up from baseline but short of target. Caregivers readily took up teaching behaviors. They were slower to adopt behaviors that require stepping back — letting a toddler struggle, tolerating frustration, resisting the impulse to rescue. This is the hardest subdomain; we're not done with it.

The question · 3

How does Learn's layered model run for a behavior-change program?

◆ Platform · ECD implementation

ECD FLWs go through the standard Connect Learn sequence: self-paced digital learning, knowledge test, daily AI coach with ECD-specific scenarios (how to demo a talk-and-play routine, how to respond when a caregiver says "I don't have time"), peer practice, supervised observation rated by an LLO supervisor. The platform numbers — 85% progressing to delivery, 88% scoring above 70% on first observation, 77% passing observation — apply here as they do across programs.

The AI coach's role for ECD matters more than it does for commodity-oriented programs. ECD is built on conversations and observable behaviors, not throughput, and the coach is what drills those patterns. For ECD specifically, the coach has been loaded with scenario cards that drill talk-and-play demos, responsive-caregiving responses, and autonomy-support conversations — the behaviors we know (from the Malawi evidence) are the ones that actually need practice.

Details.

  • Region (pilot): Malawi.
  • Pilot design: 3 visits (initial). Expanding to 8-10 visits across 12 months.
  • Measurement: 50 caregiver surveys (baseline, endline) plus observed visits for behavioral assessment.
