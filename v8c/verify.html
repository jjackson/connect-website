TopicAdversarial fraud test
Scope · Platform
Essay III.1

We paid people to defeat our fraud detection. Then we watched them fail.

Every platform claims fraud detection. Most don't test theirs adversarially. We did — and we designed the test so that "cheating worked" would have been a finding we'd have had to publish. Before the test, Connect's verification story was biometric match at the door, GPS fix on the visit, photo of the commodity or client1 — three independent signals, difficult to fake all three simultaneously, probably sufficient. "Probably sufficient" is the phrase you use when you haven't actually tried to break the system.

The question we wanted to answer was whether we needed a fourth layer that did not depend on GPS, timestamps, or photos at all — a statistical layer that could catch fabrication even if the other three were gamed. To know whether that layer would work, we had to build fakers good enough to push back on it.

The setup. We paid experienced frontline workers2 to submit 100 fake visit records each. We ran three rounds. After each round we coached the fakers on what had given them away, improving their ability to fabricate plausible data. We offered bonuses to the top 25% most convincing fabricators. This is the kind of adversarial testing most organizations only do when a regulator makes them.

The finding. Using three data elements — age, gender, and MUAC measurements — a Random Forest model achieved an AUC of 0.91 ± 0.093. Only 2.5% of real workers received fraud-risk scores as high as the lowest-scoring fabricator. Out of 33 adversarial fakers across the three rounds, we detected them with near-perfect discrimination using data patterns alone — before GPS and photo verification even kicked in.

What this means in production is that Connect's verification is empirically validated rather than assumed. Because verification has four independent layers — biometric, GPS, photo, algorithmic4 — a fabricated visit has to beat all four simultaneously. The failure modes of the four layers are different, which is the point. They do not correlate. Stacking them multiplies difficulty rather than adding it.

Marginalia

¹Pre-adversarial-test verification stack: 3 layers. Four layers became the standard after the test.
²All fakers were experienced CHC FLWs recruited specifically for the test, with informed consent and payment regardless of outcome.
³Random Forest classifier, 5-fold cross-validation. Input features: child age, child gender, MUAC measurement. Full methodology in FP report appendix.
⁴The four layers: biometric match (face/fingerprint vs enrolled FLW), GPS (fix at visit time), photo (of commodity / client / caregiver), algorithmic (statistical pattern detection).

Insight02
TopicEquitable coverage
Scope · CHC
Essay III.2

Workers naturally cluster. Microplans force them outward.

Verify is not only about whether a visit happened. It is about which visits happened. A platform can verify 100% of a cherry-picked sample and still fail at equity. Microplanning is how Connect gets coverage that isn't convenience-sampled — and the finding below is specifically from the CHC program, where the evaluation was run.

Early CHC campaigns gave FLWs a defined operational area and a target number of visits. The assumption was that workers, incentivized per verified visit, would spread evenly across the area to maximize yield. They didn't. Given a neighborhood, workers averaged 0.4 visits per estimated eligible child — visiting accessible households and skipping the harder ones, whether harder meant farther from the road, complicated language, or households known to resist5. The populations missed were systematically the ones with lower baseline health coverage. We were convenience-sampling at the exact moment equity was the goal.

The fix was to overlay a microplan layer. Before a campaign we generate a grid using Google Open Buildings, OpenStreetMap, and Microsoft building-footprint data6. Grid cells containing at least one building become Delivery Units. DUs group into Service Areas sized for one to two weeks of work. A worker cannot move to the next Service Area until they have closed every DU in the current one — either by completing visits or by confirming no eligible children are present.

In the CHC program, microplanning produced 1.4 visits per estimated child in microplanned cells versus 0.4 without — a 3.5× increase in coverage thoroughness. Population coverage rose from 82-84% to 94%7. The per-visit payment did not change. What changed was that progress through the grid became the gate, not visit count alone.

Where the microplan didn't work. Dispersed settlement patterns broke the grid. Workers walked long distances to DUs that contained no actual buildings. Some DUs fell on inaccessible land — factory grounds, prison grounds, private estates. One campaign removed microplan execution entirely due to partner constraints. The grid is powerful where density supports it; the platform falls back to area-based assignment where it does not8.

Marginalia

⁵The 0.4 figure is from pre-microplan CHC campaigns. Same FLW cohorts, same areas, same payment structure — only the coverage mechanism differed.
⁶Building-footprint data sources are merged and de-duplicated at grid-generation time. Cells are typically 100m × 100m.
⁷Coverage is measured as (children visited) / (estimated eligible children in area), using census + building-footprint population estimation.
⁸Microplanning is a configurable layer in Connect, not a hard requirement.
Source · Founders Pledge Final Report, Feb 2026 — microplanning evaluation within CHC.

TopicDesign philosophy
Scope · Platform framework
Essay III.3

Guardrails are not hard stops.

The Verify surface has to decide what counts as a valid visit. The intuitive design is to make every quality target a blocker: miss the target, reject the visit, withhold payment. It is clean, punitive, and legible to funders. It is also the design that produces the worst data.

Platforms that punish missed targets do not get honest data. If first visit must be within 3 days of discharge for payment to clear, FLWs learn to falsify visit dates to hit the window9. We would not know they were late. We would not know which LLOs were running into which kinds of delays. We would have the appearance of good performance and lose the signal we need to improve the program.

The distinction we drew is between payment criteria — hard, must be met to be paid — and guardrails — audited, trigger performance conversations and LLO coaching, but do not block payment. The distinction is a platform configuration framework; every program inherits it.

The live example is KMC. KMC's payment criterion is 4 mandatory home visits per small-and-vulnerable newborn. The key guardrail is "first visit within 3 days of discharge". We audit the guardrail. We do not enforce it. Current performance on the 3-day guardrail is 50% against a 70% target10. We know it is 50% precisely because we are not punishing misses — FLWs and LLOs report accurately. The 20-point gap is driven by three causes we are actively working on: facility-to-FLW discharge-notification lag, LLO scheduling bottlenecks, and FLW availability constraints.

The gain from keeping the guardrail soft is the real data to improve the program. We see every 3-day miss as it happens. We can diagnose why. We can fix the cause. A hard-stop design would have erased the signal.

⁹Gaming is not hypothetical. We've observed falsified timestamps in comparable commodity-distribution programs (not on Connect) that used hard-stop designs.
