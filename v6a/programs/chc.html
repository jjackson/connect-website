The Challenge

A well-run campaign can cover 82 percent of its population — and miss exactly the children it needs to find.

Every year, millions of children under five in sub-Saharan Africa miss out on basic preventive health services — vitamin A supplementation, deworming, oral rehydration salts, malnutrition screening, vaccination. The gap is not always where it looks. A well-organized campaign in a familiar area can deliver to 82 percent of the population and still leave behind the families with the lowest baseline access, because those are also the families hardest to find.

The Child Health Campaign (CHC) is Connect's flagship program and, by volume, the single largest. It runs the same LDVP architecture as every Connect program — train, deliver, verify, pay — and delivers Vitamin A, deworming, ORS/Zinc co-packs, MUAC malnutrition screening, and vaccine promotion at the door. The innovation isn't any single service. It's the discipline of geographic coverage, driven by microplanning, that makes sure the hardest 12 percent of children get reached.

What gets delivered

Five core services. Children 0–59 months.

—

Vitamin A supplementation

Door-to-door administration of Vitamin A to children 6–59 months. A WHO-recommended, evidence-backed preventive for young child mortality.

—

Deworming

Treatment for soil-transmitted helminths according to national eligibility protocols. Workers are trained on age and symptom thresholds.

—

ORS + Zinc co-packs

Oral rehydration salts plus zinc for treatment and prevention of diarrheal illness. Nigeria deployments bundle ORS/Zinc at household level.

—

Malnutrition screening

MUAC (mid-upper arm circumference) measurement. FLWs flag moderate and severe cases for referral through the app.

—

Vaccine promotion

Counseling for routine immunization and targeted campaign support in hesitancy-affected communities.

—

Data capture

Every visit captured in CommCare — structured, GPS-tagged, time-stamped. Verification runs on the data the workers create.

The Innovation

Microplanning: a grid across the target area.

Before each CHC campaign, Dimagi overlays a grid onto the operational area using open-source satellite data — Google Open Buildings, OpenStreetMap, Microsoft. Grid cells with buildings in them become Delivery Units. Delivery Units group into Service Areas, each sized for one to two weeks of a single worker's time. Workers cannot unlock a new Service Area until they have closed every Delivery Unit in the current one.

"This pushes workers into areas they'd otherwise skip."

Founders Pledge Final Report · 2026

The operational effect is quiet and large. Coverage rises — from 82 percent in a well-run comparable non-Connect campaign to 94 percent with microplans. Thoroughness rises even more dramatically: 1.4 visits per estimated child in microplanned cells, versus 0.4 in Trial Run campaigns without the saturation requirement. The discipline of the grid is what makes the coverage numbers real, not aspirational.

Scaling the network

44 LLOs, recruited through an Expression of Interest.

The LLO activation model is deliberately light. A brief scope doc and a Google Form, circulated through Dimagi's professional network, produced roughly 100 applications for CHC. From those, a Trial Run: a $3,000 initial contract to deliver 3,000 verified visits. Performance on the Trial Run — actual speed, actual quality — decides whether an LLO gets larger campaigns.

Of 37 LLOs contracted through the Founders Pledge grant: 24 entered Trial Runs and were offered larger campaigns. 10 underperformed and were not continued. Three went straight to larger campaigns on the strength of prior track records. 17 completed campaigns following successful trials. The model scales participation without the funder having to guess, up front, who can deliver.

The economics

Cost dropped 22% as the program scaled.

Over the Founders Pledge grant period, the per-visit cost fell from $2.20 to $1.70 — a 22 percent reduction. This is the opposite of the typical pattern, where organizations become less lean as they grow. The architecture explains why.

LLOs have inherently low overhead because they're local and lean. Performance-based payment ensures costs track actual delivery, not organizational size. And the digital infrastructure — training, guidance, verification — creates compounding efficiencies as the network adds workers and campaigns. Setup fees averaged $0.40 per visit during the grant; at scale, Dimagi projects them falling to roughly $0.10.

The best-case efficiency is striking. Outside Nigeria, where the commodity bundle is simpler (VAS + deworming, no ORS/Zinc), the average across 17 contracts came in at $0.78 per verified visit. One Sierra Leone LLO delivered ORS/Zinc at $1.00 per visit. These are not projections. They are what the platform actually costs once an LLO is up to speed.

For the workers

No displacement. Meaningful income.

A GiveWell displacement study interviewed 20 CCC-CHC workers to document the on-the-ground effect. Workers reported substantial income increases — for most, CCC-CHC became a primary income source. They also reported gaining technology skills (CommCare app, Google Maps), improving communication abilities, and increased confidence.

Critically, the study found no disruption to routine community health services. Campaign work is normal and expected in Nigeria's Community Health Volunteer system; other volunteers covered reduced hours during campaign periods as they typically do. The income was additive. That is the question any responsible funder asks, and the answer is on the record.

Under evaluation

An RCT is underway.

Dimagi is running a randomized controlled trial of CHC in 2026 with Innovations for Poverty Action (IPA) as the evaluation partner, funded through PRO Impact ($1.5M). The RCT is intended to produce causal evidence for the program's health impact, complementing the operational evidence already on the record.
