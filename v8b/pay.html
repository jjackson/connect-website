Insight 04 · Unit economics · CHC

Connect's architecture produced a cost reduction as CHC scaled.

◆ Scope · CHC (Founders Pledge grant period)

This is the finding we have to be precise about — the one earlier versions of this site bungled, and the one the current scoping audit corrects. In the CHC program, cost per verified visit fell 22%. That number is CHC's. The mechanism that produced it is architectural and may generalize to other programs — but we measure it per program, and we name it per program.

Going into the Founders Pledge grant period, our internal expectation — and the expectation reflected in the grant model — was that cost per verified visit would be roughly flat. The operational assumption across the development sector is that coordination overhead grows as you scale: more partners, more places, more operational burden per delivered unit. Every prior Dimagi program had reflected that ceiling.

What we measured in CHC across the grant period was different. CHC scaled to a rate capable of supporting millions of child visits per year without substantial growth in Dimagi's team. Cost per verified visit in CHC dropped from $2.20 to $1.70 — a 22% reduction as the program grew. That is a CHC number. It is not a Connect-wide number. We are measuring the same curve for KMC, ECD, and Readers and will publish those numbers separately when we have them.

Three structural reasons, all of them platform-level, explain why this architecture is built to produce cost-reducing scale. First, LLO economics: LLOs have inherently low overhead because they are local and lean, and Connect does not build a parallel in-country ops structure, it contracts one that already exists. Second, performance-based payment: costs track actual delivery, not organizational size, so there is no fixed coordination layer that grows linearly with LLO count. Third, integrated digital tooling: Learn, Deliver, Verify, and Pay sit on a single platform, which produces efficiency gains that compound as the network grows rather than diluting them into a bigger ops team.

The counterintuitive implication is that most development organizations face a structural ceiling on cost-effectiveness — the more they scale, the more ops burden grows. CHC under the Connect architecture is one of the few measured examples where scale produced cost reduction rather than cost creep. Whether the same curve holds for every new program is an empirical question. We do not claim "Connect got leaner." We claim "CHC got leaner under the Connect architecture, and here's why we think the architecture is built to repeat that." Watch this page as we publish per-program numbers from KMC and Readers.

Insight 05 · Displacement survey · CHC (Nigeria)

Campaign work doesn't displace routine health services in Nigeria's CHV system.

◆ Scope · CHC, Gombe & Sokoto, Nigeria

Funders raise the question regularly: that paying FLWs for campaign work will pull them away from their volunteer duties, improving campaign delivery at the cost of the baseline system. It is a reasonable concern. We took it seriously, commissioned the qualitative study it deserved, and found the premise was built on the wrong model of the system.

The concern assumes a clear line between "volunteer routine care" and "campaign work," with the first being the norm and the second being an intrusion. That is not the structure in Nigeria. What we commissioned was 24 in-depth interviews across Gombe and Sokoto — 20 FLWs, 2 LLO staff, and 2 local health representatives — all within the CHC program.

What we learned was that campaign work is normal and expected in Nigeria's community health volunteer system. CHVs routinely do campaigns; it is their main income source. When our CHC FLWs reduced volunteer hours, other volunteers at the facility covered the work, as they typically do during any campaign. In Gombe, FLWs maintained volunteer hours by shifting schedules within Connect's flexible 7am-to-7pm window. There was no observable displacement of routine care. The routine system absorbed the shift because the routine system expected it.

The deeper point is that the displacement concern was built on Western assumptions about what CHVs do. In the actual context, campaigns are not a disruption of the system. They are the system. The volunteer layer runs continuously; campaigns are one of the main coordinated activities through which it operates.

A word on scope. The finding is from Gombe and Sokoto, inside the CHC program. We expect the underlying cultural fact — that campaign work is integrated with CHV systems — to hold in similar community-health settings in other countries. We have not measured it elsewhere yet. We would re-interview before repeating this as a finding in a new geography. Naming the scope is part of the practice — the same practice we apply to the cost-inversion finding on the previous article, and to every program-scoped number on this site.
