§1 · Learn · how digital training produces competent FLWs

Knowledge tests didn't produce competent FLWs. We had to layer.

Workers who aced the digital knowledge test still struggled in real visits. We stopped treating the test as a release gate and built four additional layers around it. Across roughly 400 FLWs of iterative experimentation: 88% score above 70% on first observed visits and 85% progress from training to delivery. This page is the engineering retrospective: the question we had, what we tried, what we found, what changed, and what's still open.

Learning 1 of 3 · Platform · I8 · N5 hard data ⟶ insights#n5

The test passed. The visit failed.

The question we had

If a frontline worker can answer 70% of multiple-choice questions about a protocol — danger signs, dosage, when to refer — does that mean they can deliver the visit competently in a real home? Our first Learn pipeline implicitly said yes. The test was the release gate; passing it was the credential.

What we tried

Self-paced modules in a Connect Learn app, one per program. An in-app multiple-choice test at the end with a 70% pass-mark. Workers who passed went to service delivery. Workers who failed re-took the modules. Clean, auditable, and — for the FLWs we were sending into homes — insufficient. Our supervised-observation visit, the last step in the pipeline, kept catching workers the test had passed.

What we found

Knowledge is not competence. Workers who aced the digital test still struggled with the parts of frontline work that are not test-shaped — handling a skeptical caregiver, recognising subtle danger signs in a quiet baby, motivational interviewing during a five-minute visit while another child cries on a hip. The pattern was consistent across ECD, CHC, and KMC pilots. Test scores predicted exam-shaped recall; they did not predict in-home performance.

What we did measure: in our Kenya CHC pilot, an AI coach we built on Dimagi's Open Chat Studio — accessed by FLWs through WhatsApp — handled 500 of 511 queries (97.8%) without escalating to a human supervisor, with a 5-second average response time. Independent evaluation: 88.7% accuracy and 81.5% usefulness1. The handle rate was the empirical evidence that an AI coach could carry the routine middle of supervision; the 2.2% escalation stream is the dataset we now mine to extend the bot.

What changed

The test was demoted from release gate to midpoint. The pipeline now runs in five stages: self-paced learning, in-app knowledge test, daily AI-coach interaction, structured peer practice, and one supervised-observation visit rated by an LLO supervisor. The test gates progress to the AI coach; the AI coach is what closes the gap to competence. Across roughly 400 FLWs of iterative experimentation2: 85% of workers progress from training to service delivery, 88% score above 70% on their first observed visit, and 77% pass the supervised observation. The 23% who don't pass get coaching, not termination — and the AI coach captures the patterns of where they failed for the next iteration of the Learn content.

What's still open

We have not yet published the failure-mode analysis on the eleven Kenya escalations, nor whether the 97.8% handle rate holds in non-English-language deployments. The Open Chat Studio platform supports multilingual content, but the FLW prompt corpus we use is English-heavy. We also have not decomposed the AI-coach contribution from peer-practice contribution to the 77% supervised-observation pass rate; we will publish the decomposition when the next pilot closes.

Source: Founders Pledge Final Report (Feb 2026), CHC §AI-Powered Supervision; Connect Learn pipeline metrics validated across ~400 FLWs spanning ECD, CHC, KMC. [1] [2]
Learning 2 of 3 · ECD Malawi · I7 · N6 hard data ⟶ insights#n6

In ECD, attitudes were already correct. Knowledge and behavior were the gap.

The question we had

The standard ECD intervention thesis says caregivers undervalue talk-and-play and need attitude change. Was that true in our population, and was the curriculum we shipped to FLWs designed against the right gap?

What we tried

A three-visit pilot built around attitude shift — modules and FLW prompts emphasizing why responsive caregiving matters, why the first 1,000 days matter, why language exposure matters. Then a baseline survey across 50 caregivers in Malawi3 to measure both attitudes and knowledge before the intervention started.

What we found

The baseline scrambled the thesis. Nearly all 50 caregivers already held positive ECD attitudes. They agreed talk-and-play supports brain development. They endorsed responsive caregiving. The attitude scores were a ceiling, not a floor — high before we started and high after we finished. Where we actually moved the needle, in the endline panel: knowledge of the timing of rapid child development rose +33 percentage points; knowledge of ways to support mental development rose +41; observed teaching behavior rose +21; observed encouragement-of-autonomy rose +21 but reached only 47% at endline. The pattern was consistent: knowledge moved sharply when given, behavior moved more slowly because behavior is not a quiz answer.

What changed

We rebuilt the dosage. The 3-visit pilot is being expanded to 8–10 visits across 12 months — not because we need more time to convince caregivers ECD matters (they know it matters) but because behavior change takes sustained practice. The extra visits are not more talk; they are more observed practice with feedback. The Learn content for ECD now opens with a frank acknowledgement of the floor effect — "you already know this matters" — and routes the curriculum into practical sequencing of rapid-development milestones.

What's still open

Encouragement-of-autonomy at 47% endline is the hardest sub-domain. We do not yet know whether 8–10 visits will move it, or whether it requires a different intervention design entirely. The Malawi panel is N=50; whether the attitude-already-correct finding holds in Mozambique and Nigeria contexts is open.

Source: ECD Overview (Feb 2026); Malawi pilot, 76 FLWs, 9,510+ visits, 50-caregiver baseline/endline panel. [3]
Learning 3 of 3 · CHC Kenya · N14 hard data ⟶ insights#n14

The AI coach handles 97.8% of queries. The 2.2% is the data we use most.

The question we had

One human supervisor for ten FLWs is expensive and slow; one for fifty is cheap and unsupervised. Could an AI coach occupy the middle layer well enough that human supervisors only see the genuinely novel cases — and could we prove it with numbers a foundation officer would accept?

What we tried

A chatbot built on Dimagi's Open Chat Studio, trained on the CHC protocol corpus, deployed via WhatsApp because that's the surface FLWs already used daily. We logged every query and every response. We set up an independent rater process to score responses on accuracy and usefulness, blind to the bot's internal confidence.

What we found

In the Kenya CHC pilot1, the chatbot handled 500 of 511 FLW queries — 97.8% — without escalating to a human supervisor, at a 5-second average response time. Independent rating: 88.7% accuracy, 81.5% usefulness. The architectural reason this matters is leverage: the AI coach as middle layer answers the routine 97.8% — protocol questions, dosage clarifications, "what do I do if the caregiver refuses" scenarios — and routes only genuinely novel queries to human supervisors. That 2.2% escalation stream is also the dataset we mine to extend the bot. Every escalation is a flagged gap in either the Learn content, the protocol, or the bot's prompt; we triage them weekly.

What changed

The AI coach is now a permanent middle layer in the Learn pipeline, not a pilot artifact. We standardized the eval — handle rate, accuracy, usefulness, response time — and run it per-program on rollout. The CHC numbers above are the calibration; KMC and ECD are running the same eval now.

What's still open

Whether the 97.8% handle rate holds in non-English-language deployments. The Open Chat Studio platform is multilingual; the FLW prompt corpus we have used to refine prompts is English-heavy. A clean apples-to-apples comparison across languages is something we expect to publish when the next pilot closes. We also have not run a controlled comparison of AI coach contribution vs peer-practice contribution to the supervised-observation pass rate.

Source: Founders Pledge Final Report (Feb 2026), CHC §AI-Powered Supervision — Kenya pilot, 500/511 queries, independent eval 88.7% accuracy / 81.5% usefulness / 5-second response time. [1]

Open questions on Learn

  1. Whether the AI coach or peer practice contributes more to the 77% supervised-observation pass rate — we have not yet published the decomposition.
  2. Whether the 97.8% AI-coach handle rate from the Kenya CHC pilot holds in non-English-language deployments.
  3. Encouragement-of-autonomy is the hardest ECD sub-domain (47% endline). Whether 8–10 visits will move it, or it needs a different design entirely.
