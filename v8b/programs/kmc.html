The question · 1

How do you close a 95-point coverage gap on an intervention this cheap and this effective?

KMC is cheap, clinically established, and chronically under-delivered. The 2022 WHO guideline update formalized immediate-KMC: skin-to-skin contact initiated before the newborn is clinically stable, rather than after. Facility-based KMC wards are half the answer. The other half is what happens after discharge, when small and vulnerable newborns — low-birthweight, preterm, or born with complications — need continued KMC support, breastfeeding counseling, weight monitoring, and early warning for danger signs over the weeks following discharge. That's where home visits come in. That's where Connect comes in.

The Connect KMC program is a 5-visit structured protocol per SVN. Trained FLWs carry a standard equipment bundle (digital scale, thermometer, pulse oximeter) and deliver a scripted sequence of visits over the post-discharge window. The platform layer handles FLW training (Learn + AI coach with KMC-specific drills), LLO coordination, visit verification, and performance-based payment tied to completing the visit sequence. The program is active in India.

The question · 2

Why isn't "first visit within 3 days of discharge" a payment requirement?

◆ Platform framework · KMC live example

Clinically, first-visit-within-3-days is the right target. That's when the newborn is most vulnerable and when caregiver behaviors are most mutable. The temptation was to require it for payment. That would have been the wrong choice.

If payment depends on the 3-day window, FLWs learn to falsify the visit date. We lose the signal we need — which discharges got supported on time and which didn't, and why. So we made a different choice. KMC's payment criterion is 4 mandatory home visits per SVN (with the 5th as a program-wide quality target). The 3-day-post-discharge target is a guardrail — audited, reported, surfaced to LLO coaching, but not payment-blocking. This is the platform framework applied to a KMC-specific target.

The live number: current performance on the 3-day guardrail is 50% against a 70% target. We know it's 50% precisely because we're not punishing misses; FLWs and LLOs report accurately. The gap is driven by three causes we're actively working on: facility-to-FLW discharge-notification lag, LLO scheduling bottlenecks, and FLW availability constraints. Coaching, scheduling support, and discharge-notification integration are the current levers. If we turned the guardrail into a hard stop tomorrow, the reported number would jump to ~90% and most of the improvement would be fabrication. We'd rather know the truth.

The question · 3

What does a 5-visit KMC sequence actually cover?

Each FLW carries a digital weighing scale, a thermometer, and a pulse oximeter. The 5-visit sequence covers post-discharge assessment, KMC-practice coaching, breastfeeding support, danger-sign surveillance, and developmental follow-up. Each visit is scripted in the Deliver app and backed by the Learn app's KMC content plus AI coach drilling. Verification runs the standard four layers — biometric match, GPS, photo, algorithmic — with the photo element particularly load-bearing, because KMC depends on observable caregiver behavior, not just commodity delivery.

The 5-visit sequence

  • Visit 1 (within 3 days of discharge, guardrail) — post-discharge weight, feeding assessment, skin-to-skin observation, danger-sign counseling
  • Visit 2 (week 1) — weight trajectory, EBF support, temperature stability
  • Visit 3 (week 2) — SpO2 spot check, feeding adequacy, caregiver confidence
  • Visit 4 (week 4) — weight, feeding, developmental milestones
  • Visit 5 (week 6) — program exit, referral triage, handoff to routine care
