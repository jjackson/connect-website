Three pillars

What pay-for-outcomes actually means.

01 / Performance-based

Per verified service, not per activity plan.

KMC: ~$60 per verified completed case (4–5 home visits per SVN). CHC: $0.78–$1.30 per verified visit depending on commodity bundle. Complete a visit, get paid.

02 / Direct mobile money

No intermediaries. No cash logistics.

Payments flow to workers' mobile money accounts directly from the platform. No skimming, no delayed disbursement. Earnings visible in the Connect app in real time.

03 / End-to-end transparency

One ledger, three stakeholders.

Workers see their earnings. LLOs see disbursements. Funders see cost-per-verified-outcome. The same data serves every stakeholder.

Field note 01 · Multi-country, 2024–26Founders Pledge grant period

Most organisations get less lean as they scale. Connect got leaner.

As campaigns grow, operational costs grow. That's how scaling works. Every other Dimagi program follows this pattern — add staff, add overhead, add coordination costs. We assumed Connect would too. We were wrong.

Connect inverts the pattern. During the Founders Pledge grant period, the platform scaled to a rate capable of supporting millions of child visits per year without substantial growth in Dimagi's team. Cost per verified visit dropped from $2.20 to $1.70 — a 22% reduction as we grew.

Why this works (I)

LLOs have inherently low overhead because they are local and lean. They don't need to build global infrastructure; they already have a community.

Why this works (II)

Performance-based payment ensures costs track actual delivery, not organisational size. The platform absorbs growth as visits scale, not as staff scales.

What we changed We stopped hiring for the next campaign. Instead we codified the platform's repeatable operations, so that each new LLO onboarding uses the same infrastructure. Digital integration at every step — worker guidance, verification, payment — creates efficiencies that compound as the network grows. The counterintuitive implication: Connect's architecture is one of the few examples we know of where scale creates cost reduction instead of cost creep.

Source: Founders Pledge Final Report, Feb 2026 — cost-trajectory analysis.

Field note 02 · GiveWell survey, Nigeria 202520 FLWs, 2 LLOs, 2 health reps

Campaign work doesn't displace routine health services. We thought it might. It doesn't.

"If we pay FLWs to do campaign visits, they'll neglect their regular volunteer health work." This is the displacement concern funders raise. We raised it internally. GiveWell raised it. It's the right question to ask.

We commissioned 24 in-depth interviews with 20 FLWs, 2 LLO staff, and 2 local health representatives across Gombe and Sokoto in Nigeria. The picture that emerged did not match Western assumptions about what community health volunteers do.

The system as it exists

Campaign work is normal and expected in Nigeria's community health volunteer system. CHVs routinely do campaigns — it's their main income source.

What actually happens

When our FLWs reduced volunteer hours, other volunteers at the facility covered the work, as they typically do during any campaign. In Gombe, FLWs maintained volunteer hours by shifting schedules within Connect's flexible 7am–7pm window.

What we changed We are more specific now in addressing the displacement concern with funders. The answer is not "we hope it doesn't displace." The answer is "we commissioned qualitative research, and in the actual context the concern does not materialise." FLWs report substantial income increases, technology skills, communication ability, and confidence. Connect-CHC has become a primary income source for most respondents. This is income, not displacement.

Source: GiveWell FLW Displacement Survey, Oct 2025.
