The three layers

How a service gets verified.

01 / Biometric & identity

Real people received real services.

Unique identification confirms recipients. PersonalID prevents duplicate records, ghost beneficiaries and re-submission under different names.

02 / GPS & coverage

Services delivered in the right places.

Geolocation confirms where a visit happened. Microplan execution pushes workers door-to-door instead of convenience sampling. A Service Area can't be closed until every Delivery Unit in it is addressed.

03 / Algorithmic

Patterns detect what GPS can't.

AI-powered analysis across submissions flags anomalies, statistical irregularities, and signals of fabrication. Independent of GPS and photo layers — a service has to beat all four.

Field notes · Verify

What verification actually catches.

Field note 01 · CHC microplanning, 2024–26Founders Pledge grant period

Workers naturally cluster. Microplans force them outward.

Give FLWs a target area and a visit quota. They'll figure out the rest. That was the assumption. In practice, workers cherry-pick. Given a neighbourhood, they average 0.4 visits per estimated eligible child — visiting accessible households and skipping the harder ones. The populations missed were systematically those with the lowest baseline coverage. Convenience sampling at the exact moment equity is the goal.

Microplanning closes this. Before a campaign, Connect overlays a grid onto the operational area using Google Open Buildings + OpenStreetMap + Microsoft data. Grid cells with at least one building become Delivery Units. DUs group into Service Areas sized for 1–2 weeks of work. A worker cannot move to the next Service Area until every DU in the current one is closed — either by completing visits or by confirming no eligible children are present.

The result

1.4 visits per estimated child in microplanned cells vs 0.4 without. A 3.5× increase in thoroughness. Population coverage jumped from 82–84% to 94%.

What didn't work

Microplans failed in dispersed settlement patterns (workers walking to DUs with no buildings) and on inaccessible land (factory or prison grounds). One campaign removed microplan execution entirely due to partner constraints.

What we changed Microplanning is now a default for CHC campaigns. It is not just a monitoring tool — it is how verification drives equitable coverage. The visitors funders care about most are the ones most likely to be skipped without a grid.

Source: Founders Pledge Final Report, Feb 2026 — geographic coverage analysis.

Field note 02 · Adversarial testing, 202533 fabricators, 3 rounds

We paid people to defeat our fraud detection. Then we watched them fail.

Our verification stack — biometrics, GPS, photos — handles fraud risk. Good enough. That was the assumption. But what if a motivated insider tried to fake data? Could statistical patterns alone — no GPS, no timestamps, no photos — tell us?

We paid experienced FLWs to submit batches of 100 fake visit records each. We ran three rounds. After each round, we coached the fakers on what gave them away. We offered bonuses to the top 25% most convincing fabricators. The kind of adversarial testing most organisations only do when a regulator makes them.

What we found

Using only three data elements — age, gender, MUAC measurements — a Random Forest model achieved an AUC of 0.91 ± 0.09. Only 2.5% of real workers had fraud risk scores higher than even the lowest-scoring fabricator.

Why it matters

Fraud detection isn't a promise. It is an empirically validated layer. And because verification has four independent layers, a fabricated visit has to beat all four to pass.

What we changed The statistical layer runs on every submission before GPS or photo verification even fires. It is not the last line of defence — it is the first. We publish the method because real verification needs to withstand adversarial scrutiny, not claim to.

Source: Founders Pledge Final Report, Feb 2026 — adversarial testing section.

Field note 03 · KMC · Design philosophyA principle we refuse to budge on

Guardrails are not hard stops. This is a design philosophy.

When a FLW misses a target — say, first visit within 3 days of discharge — the simple response is to reject the visit or withhold payment. This is what most platforms do. We don't.

We distinguish payment criteria (hard — must be met to be paid) from guardrails (soft — trigger audits and performance conversations, but don't block payment). The KMC program requires 4 mandatory home visits per SVN as a payment criterion. But the guardrail "first visit within 3 days of discharge" is audited, not enforced. Current performance is 50% against a 70% target. We're working on it — through LLO coaching, scheduling support, and discharge notification integration — not through punishment.

Why not enforce

Platforms that punish missed guardrails don't get honest data. FLWs learn to game the system — falsifying visit dates to hit the 3-day window, for example. A platform that separates audits from payment gets the real data it needs to improve.

The deeper point

Verification exists to serve the program, not to discipline workers. We built the separation so we can keep learning.

What we changed Payment criteria and guardrails are now distinct fields on every program spec. Audits happen monthly. Performance conversations happen in person, with the LLO. Nothing blocks mobile-money payouts for a guardrail miss — only for a payment-criterion miss.

Source: KMC Conceptual Model, March 2026; program-design working memo.
