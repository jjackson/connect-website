Dispatch 1 of 3 · The floor effect

The Malawi baseline survey came back wrong. The attitudes were already there.

Almost every ECD pitch we had read leads with "we shift attitudes." The implicit theory is that caregivers in low-income settings under-prioritise early-childhood development — that they need to be convinced talk-and-play matters, that responsive caregiving matters, that the first three years matter. The intervention's job is to change those attitudes. We designed our Malawi ECD pilot around that theory: a three-visit sequence focused on building positive attitudes and shifting beliefs about responsive caregiving.

Then we ran the baseline survey across fifty caregivers. Nearly all caregivers already held positive ECD attitudes at baseline. They agreed talk-and-play supports brain development. They endorsed responsive caregiving. The attitude scores were high before we started and high after we finished. The intervention did not need to convince them. It needed to close the gap between what they already believed and what they were doing in a typical day. Where we actually moved the needle, in the Malawi pilot endline at the same fifty-caregiver panel: knowledge of the timing of rapid child development rose by 33 percentage points; knowledge of ways to support mental development rose 41 points; observed teaching behavior rose 21 points; observed encouragement-of-autonomy rose 21 points but reached only 47% at endline.

The pattern is consistent: attitudes were a ceiling, not a floor. Knowledge moved sharply when it was given. Behavior moved more slowly and unevenly because behavior is not a quiz answer. The Western framing of "shift attitudes" is structurally optimistic — it assumes the audience is starting from a low point and the intervention's job is to raise them. In our Malawi panel, the audience was starting near the top of the attitude axis and the intervention's job was something different: to move the practice closer to the belief.

Dispatch 2 of 3 · The redesign

So we doubled the dosage. 3 visits became 8–10. Not more talk; more practice.

The dosage of the intervention is the variable we control most directly. The original three-visit pilot was designed for an attitude-shift hypothesis. When the data made clear that attitude was already at the ceiling and behavior was the gap, three visits was no longer the right dose. Behavior change takes sustained practice; you cannot lecture someone into changing how they hold their child during play across three half-hour conversations spaced a few weeks apart.

So we rebuilt the dosage. The three-visit pilot is being expanded to eight to ten visits across twelve months — not because we need more time to convince caregivers ECD matters (they know it matters) but because behavior change takes sustained practice. The extra visits are not more talk; they are more observed practice with feedback. The Learn app for ECD now opens with a frank acknowledgement of the floor effect — "you already know this matters" — and routes the curriculum into practical sequencing of rapid-development milestones. The visits look different too: more time on the floor with the child, less time at the table. Less test-shaped, more practice-shaped.

Per-child cost falls in the seven-to-eighteen-dollar range across the pilot, depending on visit count and commodity bundle. We mark this as preliminary because we are still mid-iteration on dosage; the cost number will move as the eight-to-ten-visit design replaces the three-visit one. The point of publishing it now is not as a final cost-effectiveness claim but to give a funder a working number to compare against alternatives. We will publish the final per-child number when the dosage redesign closes the data loop.

Dispatch 3 of 3 · FLW competence

Workers who aced our digital test still struggled with counseling nuance. So we layered.

The training pipeline that runs Connect ECD is the same pipeline described on the Learn dispatch. The pattern is general: workers who aced our digital knowledge test still struggled with the nuances of in-person counseling — handling a skeptical caregiver, recognising subtle danger signs, motivational interviewing during a five-minute visit while a child cries. So we layered the model. Self-paced learning. In-app test. Daily AI coach interaction (Open Chat Studio). Peer practice. One supervised observation visit. The test gates progress to the AI coach. The AI coach is what closes the gap to competence.

In ECD specifically, the AI coach has a content shape that other programs do not require: it drills caregiver-conversation scenarios with the floor-effect framing built in. "The caregiver agrees that talk-and-play matters. They are not yet doing it. What do you say?" is a different prompt than "The caregiver thinks talk-and-play is not important. Convince them." The first prompt matches reality; the second prompt does not. Building the right prompt set is itself part of the curriculum, and the AI coach surfaces gaps where the prompt set is wrong.

Across the pipeline, validated across roughly four hundred FLWs spanning ECD, CHC, and KMC, eighty-five percent of workers progress from training to service delivery and seventy-seven percent pass the supervised observation. The seventy-seven-percent supervised-observation pass rate is the binding constraint we optimise on, not the eighty-eight-percent first-visit score. We do not yet know whether the AI coach or peer practice contributes more to the seventy-seven-percent number — that decomposition is the next iteration on the Learn dispatch.

What we don't know yet

  1. The 47% endline on encouragement-of-autonomy is the hardest sub-domain. Whether 8–10 visits will move it is open.
  2. The Malawi pilot is N=50 caregivers. Whether the attitude-already-correct finding holds in Mozambique and Nigeria contexts is open.
  3. Whether AI coach contribution to the 77% pass rate generalizes outside ECD — we have not run the controlled comparison.
