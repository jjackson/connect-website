Learning 1 of 3 · CHC adversarial test · I3 · N1 · N22 hard data ⟶ insights#n1

We paid CHC FLWs to defeat our fraud detection. They couldn't.

The question we had

Verified-payment platforms can be defeated by sophisticated fabrication: a worker who fakes the GPS, fakes the photo, fakes the biometric, and submits visits that never happened. If the algorithmic layer of our verification stack is the last line of defense against motivated fakers, can we stress-test it honestly — not against random noise, but against people we are actively paying to beat us?

What we tried

Adversarial paid testing, three rounds1. Experienced CHC FLWs were paid to submit 100 fake visit records each. After each round, fakers were coached on what had given them away — so each round was harder than the last. Cash bonuses were offered to the top 25% most convincing fabricators, a structural incentive to actually try. We did not run a single round and call the result the upper bound; we kept paying people to defeat the system after they had been told why their previous attempts had failed.

What we found

Using only three data elements — child age, gender, and MUAC measurement — a Random Forest classifier achieved AUC 0.91 ± 0.09. Out of 33 adversarial fakers across the three rounds, only 2.5% of real workers received fraud-risk scores higher than the lowest-scoring fabricator. The 97.5% framing — "97.5% of real workers scored cleaner than the worst-detected fake" — is the inverse of the same number, and it is the framing the FP report uses.

Three features is a deliberately stressed choice; we could have built a 20-feature model with a higher AUC. We chose three because the question we wanted to answer was harder: can the algorithmic layer detect fabrication when the data has been engineered to look plausible? The other three layers in Connect's verification stack — biometric (FLW identity), GPS (location), photo (visit context) — close the parts that age/gender/MUAC don't. A fabricated visit on Connect has to beat all four. Our adversarial test only stresses the algorithmic layer; even there, fabricators couldn't beat real workers.

What changed

The adversarial testing protocol is now part of how we evaluate the algorithmic layer for new program domains. The AUC 0.91 is a CHC number from CHC commodity bundles; we will re-run the same protocol per program rather than transfer the score. Naming the test as adversarial paid red-teaming — and publishing the bonus structure — is also a deliberate framing choice. Most platforms claim fraud detection without naming the test.

What's still open

The 97.5% figure is from adversarial paid-bonus testing in CHC. The production false-positive rate against unmotivated fabrication — a tired worker who fudges one visit — has not been published; we will when we have a clean cohort comparison. Photo verification quality also degrades on low bandwidth; we have not yet published the false-positive rate on the photo layer in low-bandwidth deployments.

Source: Founders Pledge Final Report (Feb 2026), CHC §Fraud Detection (Adversarial Testing) — 3 rounds, 33 fakers, AUC 0.91 ± 0.09 from age/gender/MUAC. [1]
Learning 2 of 3 · CHC coverage · I2 · N2 hard data ⟶ insights#n2

Without microplans, FLWs averaged 0.4 visits per child. With them, 1.4 — and coverage hit 94%.

The question we had

FLWs working a CHC neighborhood will, left to their own routing, work the houses that are easiest to reach. The houses they don't reach are systematically those with lower baseline health coverage. How do you push verified delivery into the harder areas — at scale, without hand-routing each campaign?

What we tried

The microplan layer overlays a grid onto the operational area using Google Open Buildings, OpenStreetMap, and Microsoft building data1. Grid cells with at least one detected building become Delivery Units. Delivery Units group into Service Areas sized for one to two weeks of work. The Connect app blocks an FLW from moving to the next Service Area until they have closed every Delivery Unit in the current one. The constraint is enforced at the workflow level, not at the supervisor level.

What we found

Before microplans, FLWs in CHC averaged 0.4 visits per estimated eligible child. They cherry-picked accessible households — convenience sampling at the exact moment equity was the goal. After microplans, in the same CHC cohort: 1.4 visits per estimated child in microplanned cells. Population coverage rose from 82–84% to 94%. A 3.5× thoroughness gain on the same FLW base, achieved by changing what the app would let them do next, not by changing who they were.

What changed

Microplanning is now the default mode for CHC campaigns where the partner can support the layout. The mechanism is platform-portable — the same code path runs on every Connect program — but the published numbers are CHC-specific. We do not transfer the 3.5× gain to other program domains. We measure and publish per program.

What's still open

Where the mechanism fails: in dispersed settlement patterns, FLWs walk long distances to DUs containing no buildings. Some DUs fall on inaccessible land — factory grounds, prison perimeters, restricted military zones. One CHC campaign in CAR removed microplan execution entirely because the partner could not work the layout. We do not yet have a published threshold for the settlement density at which microplanning becomes counterproductive. The operating rule today is that the partner has the final call; that rule is honest about what it does not encode.

Source: Founders Pledge Final Report (Feb 2026), CHC §Coverage Results — visits-per-child 0.4 → 1.4; coverage 82–84% → 94%. [1]
Learning 3 of 3 · Platform philosophy · I10 · N10 triangulated ⟶ insights#n10

Guardrails are not hard stops — and KMC is currently failing one.

The question we had

A verified-payment platform must be honest about the difference between a payment criterion (you don't get paid unless this is true) and a guardrail (you should hit this, we'll coach you if you don't). Treat them the same and you train workers to falsify whatever the system enforces. Treat them differently — and explain the difference publicly — and you can keep collecting honest signal on what isn't yet working.

What we tried

In KMC, the live example2: 4 mandatory home visits per Small or Vulnerable Newborn (SVN) is a payment criterion — auditable, enforced, the gate for FLW pay. The "first visit within 3 days of discharge" target is a guardrail — audited, not enforced. Punishment for missing the 3-day window would train workers to falsify the discharge date. We chose not to.

What we found

Current KMC performance on the 3-day guardrail: 50% against a 70% target. Across 4,005 KMC cases as of March 2026, average age at first contact is 12.8 days — well under the 28-day enrollment guardrail — but for half of cases, FLWs are missing the first 3 days. The cause is not FLW effort. The cause is that hospital discharge notifications aren't yet integrated into the platform, so the FLW often does not learn a baby has been discharged until days later. Punishing FLWs for that gap would produce falsified timestamps, not faster contact.

What changed

We are integrating discharge notifications and coaching FLWs on faster contact, rather than penalizing the missed window. We publish the gap. Platforms that punish guardrails don't get honest data; we'd rather report the 50% and have it move than report 90% and have it be a fiction.

What's still open

Whether 70% is achievable through coaching plus discharge-notification integration alone, or whether the 3-day window itself needs to be redesigned for the realities of post-discharge logistics. If integration ships and the number doesn't move, we'll redesign the target rather than punish the gap.

Source: KMC Conceptual Model (March 2026) — 4,005 cases; 50% within-3-days vs 70% target; 12.8-day average age at first contact. [2]
