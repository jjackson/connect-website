Learning 01 · Coverage geometry · CHC

Workers cluster. Microplans force them outward.

The hardest problem in field verification, it turned out, wasn't fraud — it was the invisible choice of which households to skip. Without geographic enforcement, FLWs in the CHC program averaged 0.4 visits per estimated eligible child1. Given a neighborhood and a quota, workers reached accessible households and skipped the harder ones. The populations missed were systematically those with lower baseline health coverage. We were producing convenience sampling at the exact moment equity was the goal.

The intervention was to take the choice out of the worker's hands and put it in a grid. Before each campaign, we overlay a grid onto the operational area using Google Open Buildings, OpenStreetMap, and Microsoft building data1. Cells with at least one building become Delivery Units. DUs group into Service Areas sized for one to two weeks of work per FLW. A worker cannot advance to the next Service Area until every DU in the current one has been closed — either with a completed visit or a confirmed "no eligible children here." The CommCare app enforces the sequence; cherry-picking is no longer an option.

In CHC, microplanning produced 1.4 visits per eligible child versus 0.4 without — a 3.5× gain in thoroughness, and population coverage rose from 82–84% to 94%.

The data under this structure was stark. 1.4 visits per estimated child in microplanned cells versus 0.4 without — a 3.5× increase in thoroughness1. Population coverage in visited cells jumped from 82–84% (experienced LLO, no microplan) to 94%. To reach 95% of the population, microplanned campaigns needed 91% of cells; non-microplanned needed only 72% — revealing how concentrated coverage had been in the easy cells. The benchmark comparison: a well-run non-Connect CommCare campaign sits at ~82% population coverage. The microplanned CHC number is meaningfully higher, and the gain is in exactly the cells that matter most.

The grid does not work everywhere. In dispersed settlement patterns, workers walked long distances to DUs with no actual buildings — the structure imposed friction the terrain didn't justify. Some DUs fell on inaccessible land — factory grounds, prison grounds, restricted military areas — requiring audit-based exceptions. One campaign removed microplan execution entirely due to partner-side constraints. The grid is a tool that suits density. Making it robust to sparse and irregular terrain is open work; the current shape is optimized for what we have, not for everything we'll need.

Learning 02 · Fraud as a test case · Platform mechanism, tested in CHC

We paid people to defeat our fraud detection. Then we watched them fail.

Most fraud-detection claims in the development sector are aspirational. They describe an approach and point at a clean audit. They rarely publish an adversarial test, because adversarial testing is the kind of investigation that produces a number you might not like. We ran one anyway. Funder confidence in performance-based payment depends on a real answer to "could a motivated insider beat your verification?" — and the answer deserves to be a measurement, not a promise.

We paid experienced FLWs to submit 100 fake visit records each, across three rounds1. After each round, we coached the fakers on what had given them away. We offered bonuses to the top 25% most convincing fabricators — so the test was against motivated insiders, not naive ones. The research question was narrow: could statistical patterns alone, with no GPS and no photos, discriminate fake visits from real ones?

Out of 33 adversarial fakers across three coached rounds, only 2.5% of real workers received fraud risk scores higher than even the lowest-scoring fabricator.

Using only three data elements — age, gender, and MUAC measurements — a Random Forest model achieved an AUC of 0.91 ± 0.091. Only 2.5% of real workers received fraud risk scores higher than even the lowest-scoring fabricator. Out of 33 adversarial fakers across the three coached rounds, the model discriminated them with near-perfect accuracy from data patterns alone — before GPS or photo verification entered the picture.

That is statistical detection on one layer of a four-layer stack. In production, a fabricated CHC visit has to beat biometric identity (no reusing beneficiary records), GPS + microplan constraints (the visit must be inside the expected DU on the expected day), photo capture at the point of delivery, and the algorithmic anomaly detection we just described. Each layer uses different signal. Coordinating a fake that defeats all four requires more effort than actually doing the visit honestly — which is the design intent.

For funders, this means fraud risk on Connect is empirically bounded, not promised. For LLOs, the same verification data serves as auditable proof of delivery for their next grant application. For workers, it means the honest ones aren't paying a trust tax for the few who might cheat. The four-layer model is a deliberate architectural choice; the adversarial test is what lets us name the bound.

Learning 03 · Design philosophy · Platform, live example KMC

Guardrails are not hard stops. The data changes when you confuse them.

The first instinct when a quality target isn't met — first visit within 3 days of discharge, for example — is to withhold payment. It feels fair. It seems like accountability. We considered it. The reason we didn't is the most consequential design choice in how Connect treats verification.

If missing the 3-day window costs an FLW money, they will falsify visit dates to hit it. The platform will then show 100% compliance. We will have no idea what's actually happening. The guardrail will have measured nothing, because the measurement itself changed the behavior1. This is the fundamental problem with conflating a signal with a sanction — well-known in measurement theory, easy to forget when the program is your own.

So we drew a line. Payment criteria are hard: a visit is paid only when identity is verified, GPS confirms the visit was on-site, minimum visit duration was met, and required data was captured. Guardrails are soft: they trigger audits and performance conversations but don't block payment. The distinction is small on paper and large in behavior — it determines whether the platform sees what actually happens or only what workers want us to see.

A platform that punishes missed guardrails doesn't get the data it needs to improve the program.

The live example is in KMC. The program requires a minimum of 4 mandatory home visits per SVN as a payment criterion — enforced. The guardrail "first visit within 3 days of discharge" is currently at 50% compliance against a 70% target1. That is a real number. We have it because we chose to get it instead of getting a fake 100%. We're working on it through LLO coaching, scheduling support, and discharge-notification integration with facility partners. Not through punishment.

The same architecture supports a different outcome we publish honestly: KMC's average number of visits per case is 3.4 against a 5-visit guardrail; early discharge sits at 7.6% against a 5% target. Uncomfortable. Also real. The reward for honesty in measurement is the ability to actually move the metrics — coordinated work across facility partners and LLOs, rather than a coverage of the gap with rules that train workers to lie.

What's open

What we don't know yet.

01
The 97.5% adversarial detection is from paid-bonus testing in CHC. Production false-positive rates against unmotivated fabrication aren't yet published.
02
Microplans failed in dispersed settlement patterns. We don't yet have a clean threshold for when settlement density makes microplanning counterproductive.
03
Photo verification quality degrades on low bandwidth. False-positive rate on the photo layer in low-bandwidth deployments isn't yet published.
