Insight 04 · Unit economics

Connect's architecture produced a cost reduction as CHC scaled.

◆ Scope: CHC · Founders Pledge grant period

This is the finding we have to be precise about. In the CHC program, cost per verified visit fell 22%. That number is CHC's. The mechanism that produced it is architectural and may generalize — but we measure it per program, and we name it per program.

Phase 1 — sector baseline expectation

As campaigns grow, operational costs grow. Every other Dimagi program had followed this pattern.

Going into the Founders Pledge grant period, our internal expectation — and the expectation reflected in the grant model — was that cost per verified visit would be roughly flat. The operational assumption across development programs is that coordination overhead grows as you scale: more partners, more places, more ops burden per delivered unit. Every prior Dimagi program had reflected that ceiling.

Phase 2 — what we measured in CHC

CHC cost-per-verified-visit fell from $2.20 to $1.70 across the grant period. A 22% reduction.

In the CHC program, across the Founders Pledge grant period, we scaled to a rate capable of supporting millions of child visits per year without substantial growth in Dimagi's team. Cost per verified visit in CHC dropped from $2.20 to $1.70 — a 22% reduction as the program grew. This is a CHC number. Not a Connect-wide number. We're measuring the same curve for KMC, ECD, and Readers and will publish those separately.

Phase 3 — the structural reasons (platform-level)

LLO economics. Performance-based payment. Integrated digital tooling.

Three reasons that are architectural — they sit at the platform level, independent of which program is running on top:

  • LLO economics. LLOs have inherently low overhead because they're local and lean. Connect doesn't build a parallel in-country ops structure; it contracts one that already exists.
  • Performance-based payment. Costs track actual delivery, not organizational size. There's no fixed coordination layer that grows linearly with LLO count.
  • Integrated digital tooling. Worker guidance, verification, and payment are integrated into every step of the LDVP cycle — producing efficiency gains that compound as the network grows.
Phase 4 — how to read this for other programs

The mechanism generalizes; the number is per-program evidence.

CHC is the program where we've measured cost inversion. The platform architecture — LLO economics plus performance-based payment plus integrated tooling — is designed to produce cost-reducing scale. Whether the curve holds for KMC (which has more clinical per-visit work), for ECD (which has longer visit cadences), or for Readers (a different sector entirely) is an empirical question we measure per program. We don't claim "Connect got leaner." We claim "CHC got leaner under the Connect architecture, and here's why we think the architecture is built to repeat that."

The counterintuitive implication

Most development organizations have a structural ceiling on cost-effectiveness. This architecture is one of the few that doesn't.

Most development programs face a structural ceiling: the more they scale, the more ops burden grows. CHC under the Connect architecture is one of the few measured examples where scale produced cost reduction rather than cost creep. If the platform-level mechanism holds up in the other programs we're measuring, this becomes a much stronger claim. Until then, it's a CHC claim with a platform-level hypothesis attached.

Source: Founders Pledge Final Report, Feb 2026 — cost data from CHC program under FP grant.
Insight 05 · Displacement concern

Campaign work doesn't displace routine health services in Nigeria's CHV system.

◆ Scope: CHC · Gombe and Sokoto, Nigeria

Funders ask about "crowd-out" — that paying FLWs for campaign work will pull them away from their volunteer duties. We took the question seriously, commissioned the interviews, and found the premise was built on the wrong model.

Phase 1 — the concern

Paying FLWs for campaign visits will make them neglect their regular volunteer health work.

This is a standard funder concern. The implicit model: CHVs do routine volunteer work; campaigns are a disruption; paying them for campaigns will pull their time away from routine care. If true, it would mean Connect improves campaign delivery at the cost of the baseline system.

Phase 2 — what we actually did

24 in-depth interviews across Gombe and Sokoto in Nigeria, inside the CHC program.

We commissioned 24 in-depth interviews: 20 FLWs, 2 LLO staff, 2 local health representatives, across Gombe and Sokoto in Nigeria. All within the CHC program. The goal was to hear what displacement, if any, the workers themselves reported — and what local health system representatives observed.

Phase 3 — what we learned

Campaigns are normal. CHV systems already expect them. Other volunteers cover.

Campaign work is normal and expected in Nigeria's community health volunteer system. CHVs routinely do campaigns — it's their main income source. When our CHC FLWs reduced volunteer hours, other volunteers at the facility covered the work, as they typically do during any campaign. In Gombe, FLWs maintained volunteer hours by shifting schedules within Connect's flexible 7am-7pm window.

Phase 4 — the deeper point

The displacement concern was built on Western assumptions about CHVs.

The concern assumes a clear line between "volunteer routine care" and "campaign work," with the first being the norm and the second being the intrusion. That's not the structure in Nigeria. In the actual context, campaigns are not a disruption of the system. They are the system. The volunteer layer runs continuously; campaigns are one of the main coordinated activities through which it operates.

Scope caveat

This is a CHC-in-Nigeria finding. We'd re-survey before claiming it elsewhere.

The finding comes from Gombe and Sokoto inside the CHC program. We expect the underlying cultural fact — that campaign work is integrated with CHV systems — to hold in similar community-health settings elsewhere. We haven't measured it elsewhere yet. We'd re-interview before repeating this as a finding in other geographies. Naming the scope is part of the practice.

Source: FLW Displacement Survey, Oct 2025 — 24 in-depth interviews in Gombe and Sokoto, CHC program.
