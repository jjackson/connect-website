The challenge

250 million children at risk of not achieving developmental potential.

Early childhood development sits in a frustrating position in global health. The intervention is well-evidenced — responsive caregiving, talk-and-play, exposure to language, opportunities for autonomy. The catchment is enormous: roughly 250 million children globally at risk of not achieving their developmental potential. The delivery model is hard: it is fundamentally about behavior change in homes, conducted by FLWs who themselves need training that goes beyond a knowledge check.

Connect ECD is the platform applied to that delivery problem. The Malawi pilot — 76 FLWs, 9,510+ visits, 50 caregivers in baseline and endline — is the cohort that has produced most of the public learnings. The Mozambique and Nigeria expansions are the next test of whether those findings hold outside the Malawi context, which is an open question we don't yet have an answer to.

Learning · 01 · Target the right gap

Attitudes were at ceiling at baseline. The intervention had to move knowledge and behavior.

The proposal we wrote in 2023 followed the standard playbook for an early-childhood-development home-visiting intervention: build caregiver attitudes about responsive caregiving, build caregiver knowledge of child development, and build caregiver behaviors that act on both. The literature supports targeting all three. The funders who reviewed the proposal expected to see all three.

The Malawi baseline survey — 50 caregivers across 76 FLWs — found something different. Nearly all caregivers already agreed that talk-and-play supports brain development, endorsed responsive caregiving, and rejected harsh correction. Attitude scores were high before the intervention started, and stayed high after it finished. The headline outcome we were resourced to chase wasn't the one carrying the work.

We were planning to convince people of something they already believed.

Where the intervention actually moved the needle: +33% on knowledge of rapid child development timing, +41% on ways to support mental development, +21% on observed teaching behavior, +21% on observed encouragement of autonomy. The pilot was producing the right kind of change — just not the change we'd been measuring most carefully.

The reframe matters because of where it sends the next dollar. If the gap is in attitudes, you spend on persuasion content. If the gap is in knowledge and observed behavior, you spend on visit cadence and behavior rehearsal. We expanded the pilot from 3 visits to 8–10 visits across 12 months — not because caregivers need more convincing, but because behavior change requires sustained practice and feedback.

Learning · 02 · The FLW competence problem

Workers who aced the digital test still struggled with the encounter.

The FLW-facing version of the same problem turned out to be larger than the caregiver-facing version. Our first ECD training stack was a self-paced digital course followed by a multiple-choice knowledge assessment. Workers who passed the test still struggled with the nuances of in-person counseling — handling a skeptical caregiver, recognizing subtle developmental concerns, practicing motivational interviewing instead of reading a script.

We built a layered model around the test: self-paced digital learning, in-app knowledge gate, daily AI coach interaction (Open Chat Studio via WhatsApp), peer practice, and one supervised observation visit rated by an LLO supervisor. The numbers under that stack: 85% of FLWs progress from training to service delivery, 88% score above 70% on first observed visits, 77% pass the supervised observation gate. Validated across ~400 FLWs through iterative experiments.

The architectural shift is harder to capture in a number. The platform stopped pretending it could verify competence cheaply. Once we accepted that competence has a cost, we could design a stack that actually pays it. The model has held across CHC, KMC, and ECD without redesign, which is the strongest evidence we have that the layering — not any single layer — is what works.

Learning · 03 · The hardest sub-domain

Encouragement of autonomy is at 47%. We don't know yet whether more visits will move it.

Inside the +21% observed-behavior gain on encouragement of autonomy is a subtler number: the absolute endline rate sat at 47%. That is the share of caregivers we observed actively letting a young child make small choices, attempt small tasks, and lead small interactions. The relative gain is the headline; the absolute level is the open question.

Encouragement of autonomy is the hardest of the four ECD sub-domains for reasons we partly understand and partly don't. It runs against everyday parenting habits more than the other domains do. It requires the caregiver to slow down at exactly the moments when slowing down is least convenient. It is harder to model in a single visit because it is, by definition, a pattern across many small interactions.

The 8–10 visit cadence is partly a bet on this sub-domain — that more touchpoints with practice and feedback will move it. We don't know yet whether that's right. It might be that 8–10 visits move it from 47% to something materially higher; it might be that the sub-domain needs a different intervention design altogether (different content, different format, different point in the family's day). The Mozambique and Nigeria cohorts will tell us more.

The headline gain is the relative number. The open question is the absolute level.

What we don't know yet.

  1. The 47% endline on encouragement-of-autonomy is the hardest sub-domain. Whether 8–10 visits will move it is open.
  2. The Malawi pilot is N=50 caregivers. Whether the attitude-already-correct finding holds in Mozambique and Nigeria contexts is open.
  3. Whether AI coach contribution to the 77% pass rate generalizes outside ECD — we have not run the controlled comparison.
