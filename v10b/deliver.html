pillar 01

LLO autonomy by design

LLOs recruit FLWs, schedule campaigns, run supervision. Connect provides infrastructure — training app, verification stack, payment rails — not a top-down program manager. Local context decisions stay local.

pillar 02

App-guided every visit

CommCare-derived workflows guide each interaction step-by-step. GPS, timestamps, photos, and biometric checks capture as the visit happens — not from memory after the fact.

pillar 03

Performance-based contracts

LLOs are paid per verified visit, not per FLW enrolled. Scaling means delivering more verified work, not winning more grants. The contract structure mirrors the funder's incentive.

progression_of_thinking // llo_activation

Pre-vetting didn't predict performance. The Trial Run did.

Conventional procurement says vet partners thoroughly, then contract the winners. We tried it. Past reputation, team size, and organizational maturity didn't predict whether an LLO would actually deliver. So we built a different filter.

early 2024 · the conventional approach

"Vet thoroughly. Contract the winners."

Capability statements. References. Past portfolios. Site visits. Standard procurement. We built rank-ordered shortlists from the EOI applications and contracted the top names. It produced a list that looked rigorous on paper.

mid-2024 · what the field showed

Past reputation didn't predict delivery

Some highly-vetted partners under-delivered or stalled out. Some less obvious organizations — smaller, newer, less polished — turned in clean campaigns. The reputational signal was noisy at best, anti-correlated with delivery in some cases. The pre-qualification stack was sorting for proposal-writing capacity, not field execution.

late 2024 · the trial run model

$3,000 setup ↔ 3,000 verified visits

We invented the Trial Run: a small contract — $3,000 setup payment for 3,000 verified child visits — that lets the LLO show, not tell. The Trial is sized to be small enough to underwrite without conviction, but large enough that you cannot fake your way through it. The decision to continue is based on measured field performance, not on the proposal.

trial_size=$3K / 3K visits · decision_basis=field_perf
2025-2026 · what we measured

107 EOIs → 37 contracted → 24 trials → 14 passed

The full conversion funnel from the CHC cohort: 107 EOI applications, 37 contracted under Trial Run terms, 24 advanced to full Trials, 14 passed and ran larger campaigns, 10 underperformed and were not continued, and 3 went straight to larger campaigns based on prior measured performance. We publish this funnel because diligence readers ask — and because the alternative (opaque vendor lists) is what funders are trying to escape.

eoi_to_trial=37/107 · trial_pass=14/24 = ~65%
what we don't yet know

The 65% holds in CHC. Other programs are too small to publish.

Whether 65% conversion holds outside CHC is open. KMC, ECD, and Readers are running on too small a contract base to publish their own conversion rates yet. The selection criteria that drive the 65% are also not yet codified into a model an outside funder could replicate — today they're judgment calls by program managers, calibrated by the Trial outcomes.

progression_of_thinking // local_intervention

Five of seven LLOs invented vaccine-hesitancy outreach — before we asked.

In CLP Stage 2, LLOs solved a problem we hadn't designed for. We named the L in LLO honestly and built around what they brought, instead of overwriting it.

2025 · what we noticed

Vaccine hesitancy was the unannounced barrier

In our CLP Stage 2 vaccine campaign, the explicit work was vaccine delivery and microplanning. The implicit work — the gating constraint on coverage in many communities — was vaccine hesitancy. Households declining the visit. Caregivers asking why a stranger was offering vaccines. The standard playbook says: build a comms campaign, get celebrity endorsements, hire community engagement officers.

what 5-6 of 7 LLOs did instead

They leveraged community and religious leaders

Of 7 LLOs in the campaign, 5-6 independently leveraged religious leaders, community leaders, or existing community relationships to navigate the hesitancy. They did this before we asked. One FLW countered hesitancy with a personal example: "We were immunized, we have children." Another LLO sourced FLWs the community itself recommended — selection by social legitimacy rather than HR criteria.

independent_invention=5-6 of 7 LLOs
what we changed

The platform's job is to enable, not to centralize

The temptation was to write down what the LLOs were doing, package it as a "best practice," and ship it back to them as a curriculum. We didn't. The reason these tactics worked is precisely that they came from inside the community. Centralizing them would have stripped out the social capital that made them effective. Instead, we shifted what the platform optimizes for: not directing LLOs but resourcing them — providing training tooling, verification infrastructure, payment rails, and getting out of the way of the local intervention design.

what's still open

We don't have a structured way to share what LLOs invent

The "real intervention" often lives in LLO heads, not in our app. We have not built a structured framework for capturing or sharing what individual LLOs invent locally. A naive solution (write it down centrally, redistribute) would centralize what is by design distributed. A better answer might be peer-to-peer LLO networking — but we don't have it yet.

progression_of_thinking // fragile_context_failure

Both LLOs in CAR dropped out. We changed the activation rule.

Publishing failure is rare. We're publishing this one because the lesson is operationally sharp: remote coordination doesn't work in fragile contexts, and we've changed how we activate them.

2024 · the assumption

"The platform is portable. Connectivity bridges everything."

Connect's platform is intentionally light-touch. Workers download an app. Supervisors review remotely. Payment is automated. We assumed the same model would work in fragile contexts — why wouldn't it? — and contracted two LLOs in the Central African Republic for vaccine campaign work.

2025 · what happened

Both LLOs dropped out

In the Central African Republic, both contracted LLOs in our CHC vaccine work dropped out. Staffing turnover. Connectivity issues. Coordination friction we couldn't resolve over WhatsApp. The platform's lightweight model assumed an operational floor that wasn't there. Remote coordination wasn't enough.

car_dropout=2/2 LLOs · scope=chc.car.vaccine
what we changed

Fragile contexts now require dedicated in-country presence

We've flagged fragile contexts as requiring dedicated in-country presence before activation. We don't repeat the same model in similarly difficult geographies — the activation cost is higher, the timeline is longer, and the platform alone is not sufficient. This isn't a graceful retreat; it's a gating rule that adds friction we previously avoided.

what's still open

"In-country presence" is a coarse rule

We don't yet have a sharper predictive model for which fragile-context features make remote coordination fail. Connectivity stability? LLO staffing depth? Conflict proximity? They're all candidates; we haven't tested them as separable predictors. The rule today is binary and qualitative — and it costs us campaigns we might otherwise run.
