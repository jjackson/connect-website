Learning · 01 · Economics of scale

Most organizations get less lean as they scale. Connect got leaner.

Every other Dimagi program follows the standard pattern: more partners, more supervision, more ops burden. Connect inverted it — and understanding why took three distinct observations stacked on top of each other.

Most platforms that report cost-effectiveness publish cost-per-FLW-trained, cost-per-app-installed, or cost-per-enrolled-LLO. Each of these numbers is technically true and operationally beside the point — the question that actually matters is whether the work was done. Connect publishes cost per verified visit because the asymmetry between activity-cost and outcome-cost is exactly where most development funding gets eaten. The story below is why that metric matters, and what happened to it.

The orthodoxy"Scale means bigger teams."

The standard development-finance assumption: as a program grows, operational costs grow with it. More LLOs to manage, more workers to supervise, more logistics. Every Dimagi program before Connect showed this pattern. We assumed it would continue. We budgeted for it. We told funders to expect it.

Founders Pledge grant · EarlyThe starting cost: $2.20 per verified visit.

Early in the grant period, cost per verified CHC visit sat at $2.20. Consistent with expectations. We budgeted assuming that number would stay roughly flat as we scaled — and we treated flat as a win, because the orthodoxy says scale eats efficiency. Our internal forecasts didn't seriously consider the case where the number would fall.

Grant start: $2.20 / visit

Founders Pledge grant · End$1.70 per visit. 880K+ visits. Team size: flat.

By the end of the 14-month grant period, cost per verified visit had fallen to $1.70 — a 22% reduction. The platform had scaled to a rate capable of supporting millions of child visits per year without substantial growth in Dimagi's team. In Nigeria (with ORS/Zinc co-packs) the average settled at $1.30/visit across 14 contracts. Outside Nigeria (VAS + deworming only) it averaged $0.78/visit across 17 contracts. One Sierra Leone LLO delivered ORS/Zinc for $1.00/visit. The weighted-average headline ($1.70) hides those bundle differences — we report the breakdown so the average can't be read as monolithic.

Most cost-effectiveness ceilings are structural. Removing the ceiling requires redesigning the architecture, not renegotiating the budget.

Why it worked — three structural reasonsLean partners, performance contracts, integrated tech.

1. LLOs are inherently low-overhead because they're local and lean — they're not carrying the fixed costs of a Dimagi-style central organization. 2. Performance-based payment forces costs to track actual delivery, not organizational size; an LLO that hires three extra staff without delivering three staff worth of visits gets paid the same as one that didn't. 3. Digital technology is integrated into every step — worker guidance, verification, payment — so adding campaign volume compounds efficiency instead of compounding coordination overhead.

Each reason on its own doesn't flip the orthodoxy. Stacked, they produce a different shape: one where scale creates cost reduction instead of cost creep. Setup fees during the grant averaged $0.40/visit; at larger campaign scale they're expected to fall toward $0.10/visit, accelerating the trend.

The counterintuitive implicationMost cost ceilings are structural, not negotiable.

Most development organizations have a structural ceiling on cost-effectiveness because their architecture ties cost to organizational size. Connect's architecture breaks that tie. Whether it continues to break it at 10× current scale is something the next grant period will tell us. Nothing in the mechanism suggests it should stop, but scale reveals things the pilot period couldn't — which is why we publish the curve and let funders judge whether the next data point is consistent.

Source: Founders Pledge Final Report (Feb 2026). Insight 4 in the public insights log.

Learning · 02 · The displacement question

Campaign work doesn't displace routine health services. The concern was real. The finding was different.

A common funder worry: paying FLWs for campaign visits will crowd out their unpaid routine health work. We took the worry seriously and went looking for evidence. The evidence was shaped by a Western assumption we didn't know we were making.

This is the question that comes up in nearly every funder meeting. Some version of: "If you pay FLWs to do campaigns, won't they neglect the volunteer routine work that holds the community-health system together?" It is a serious question. The literature on extrinsic vs intrinsic motivation supports it. The instinct to ask it is right. Our instinct, when it became a recurring concern, was to commission an actual study rather than wave at the question with reassuring language.

The funder concern"Won't paid work crowd out volunteer work?"

A recurring question, raised by multiple funders: if we pay FLWs to do campaign visits, won't they neglect their regular volunteer health work? The concern assumes the two activities are in zero-sum competition for the worker's time. We took it seriously enough to go measure it.

What we commissioned24 in-depth interviews in Nigeria.

GiveWell funded an FLW Displacement Survey: 20 FLWs, 2 LLO staff, 2 local health representatives, across Gombe and Sokoto states in Nigeria, October 2025. Interviewed independently. The objective was to measure whether and how much routine work had been displaced by CHC campaign time.

Interview subjects: 24 · across 2 states · independent fielding

What the data saidCoverage held because that's how the system works.

Campaign work is normal and expected in Nigeria's community health volunteer system. CHVs routinely run campaigns — it's their main income source, not a departure from their role. When FLWs reduced volunteer hours during campaign periods, other volunteers at the facility covered, as they typically do for any campaign. In Gombe specifically, FLWs maintained volunteer hours by shifting their schedules within Connect's flexible 7am–7pm window.

Campaigns are not a disruption of Nigeria's CHV system. They are the system.

The deeper findingThe question was based on a Western assumption.

The displacement concern was built on an assumption about what community health volunteers do that doesn't apply in the Nigerian context. In the actual system, campaigns are not a disruption — they are the system. FLWs reported substantial income increases. Most said CHC had become a primary income source. The reported secondary effects were overwhelmingly positive: technology skills (CommCare app, Google Maps), improved communication, increased confidence.

We now present this finding to every new funder raising the displacement question. The context matters, and the context here is that we were worried about something the local system had already solved. The N=24 sample is small enough that we'd re-survey before claiming the finding holds in CHV systems with materially different structures — but inside the Nigerian frame, it is what the data says.

Source: GiveWell FLW Displacement Survey (Oct 2025). Insight 5 in the public insights log.

What we don't know yet.

  1. The $1.70 weighted average masks bundle differences ($1.30 Nigeria with ORS, $0.78 outside). Whether the 22% year-over-year curve holds in non-CHC programs is open.
  2. The N=24 displacement survey is in two Nigerian states. Whether "campaigns are routine" holds in CHV systems with different structures — re-survey first before claiming it elsewhere.
  3. Setup-fee projection ($0.40 → ~$0.10/visit at scale) is forward-looking. We will publish the converged number when the next cohort closes.
