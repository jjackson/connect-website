Memo / Programs / KMC

Program · Kangaroo Mother Care · 4 countries · 6 LLOs · Eleanor Crook Foundation

KMC reduces neonatal mortality up to 40%. Global coverage is below 5%. We close the post-discharge gap.

Kangaroo Mother Care is one of the most evidence-backed interventions in maternal and child health, and one of the least delivered. The WHO recommends KMC for all newborns ≤ 2,000g; global coverage remains below 5% because hospitals discharge families within days and structured at-home follow-up rarely exists. Connect KMC fills the gap with 4–5 structured home visits in the first 60 days. This is the working memo on what we have measured and where we are currently failing.

Learning 1 of 4 · why KMC

The intervention works. The delivery model is what's missing globally.

Kangaroo Mother Care reduces neonatal mortality by up to 40% for small or vulnerable newborns2. The mechanism is well-understood: skin-to-skin contact between caregiver and newborn, supported by frequent breastfeeding and home monitoring of weight, temperature, and danger signs. WHO's 2022 KMC guidance recommends it for all newborns weighing 2,000g or less. The clinical evidence is not in dispute.

The delivery problem is. Global coverage of structured KMC remains below 5% for eligible infants2. The reason is operational rather than clinical: hospitals discharge families within days of birth — sometimes hours — and what was supposed to be an extended care model becomes a brief inpatient procedure. Structured at-home follow-up rarely exists. The 40% mortality-reduction figure assumes the home component happens; in most settings it does not.

Connect KMC is built around closing that gap. Each enrolled SVN gets four to five structured home visits in the first 60 days post-discharge1. The FLW arrives with portable equipment — weighing scale, thermometer, pulse oximeter, measuring tape — and the Connect KMC app captures readings at each visit. Danger signs (poor weight gain, hypothermia, infection) auto-trigger referrals back to a facility, and the app prompts follow-up at the next visit. The structure is designed so that the at-home component is verified, paid, and supervised, not a hopeful afterword to inpatient care.

The honest scope: 4,005 cases as of March 2026 across 6 LLO partners in Uganda, Kenya, India, and Nigeria. The mortality-reduction outcome is not something we yet measure directly — that is the IPA-style RCT work. What we measure is coverage of the structured visit schedule and whether the danger-sign-to-referral chain functions. Both are necessary for the 40% figure to translate into lives; neither is sufficient on its own.

Learning 2 of 4 · what an FLW carries

Every visit: scale, thermometer, pulse oximeter, measuring tape. Readings into the app. Danger signs auto-refer.

The KMC visit is the most clinically dense visit shape Connect runs. It also has the most concrete equipment list of any program in the catalog. Every Connect KMC home visit, the FLW arrives with a portable weighing scale, a digital thermometer, a pulse oximeter, and a measuring tape1. The Connect KMC app records the readings — weight, temperature, oxygen saturation, head circumference — at each visit and tracks them against guideline thresholds.

The point of the equipment is the auto-referral chain. A weight stagnant or declining over two visits triggers an in-app prompt for the FLW to refer to the nearest facility and schedules a follow-up visit at higher frequency. A temperature reading below 36.5°C is a hypothermia flag, also auto-refer. An oxygen-saturation reading below threshold is a respiratory-distress flag. The FLW does not have to remember the thresholds; the app does. The FLW's job is to take the measurement honestly and to follow the referral the app produces. Connect's verification stack closes the loop on whether the measurement was actually taken.

This is also where the integration with hospital discharge notifications matters most. The Connect KMC enrollment flow requires that an SVN be identified at discharge — ideally with a notification pushed from the facility EMR into the Connect partner's queue. When that integration is live, the LLO can claim the case within minutes, dispatch an FLW to the home within 72 hours, and start the structured visit schedule on the clinical clock the WHO guidance assumes. When it is not live, the case has to be found through community channels, and the 3-day window slips.

The honest gap: equipment availability across all 6 KMC LLO partners has been uneven. Pulse oximeters are imported and have failed at higher rates than thermometers; a partner-level audit of equipment uptime is something we are doing for the next reporting cycle. We will publish the audit; the headline equipment list at the top of this section is what is supposed to be on every visit, not what is on every visit today.

Learning 3 of 4 · payment design

4 visits is a payment criterion. 3-day enrollment is a guardrail. We are failing the guardrail, openly.

KMC is the live example for Connect's distinction between payment criteria and guardrails — a platform-level configuration described on the Verify page but visible most clearly here.

The KMC payment criterion: 4 mandatory home visits per SVN. Hard. Must be met to be paid. The number is calibrated to the WHO guidance and to the clinical density required for the danger-sign auto-referral chain to function. An LLO that completes 3 visits per SVN is not paid for that SVN regardless of the visit quality. Current performance: an average of 3.4 visits per case against a 5-visit guardrail1. The 4-visit minimum is the floor; the 5-visit number is the soft target.

The KMC guardrail: "first visit within 3 days of discharge." Soft. Audited and coached, not enforced through payment withholding. Current KMC performance on that guardrail is 50% against a 70% target1. We are visibly failing it. The reason we publish the gap rather than hide it is the design philosophy: platforms that punish guardrails train workers to falsify timestamps, and we then lose the ability to see whether the underlying problem (delayed discharge notification, FLW staffing gap, household-finding latency) is improving.

What we are doing instead. Three workstreams. First, LLO coaching on scheduling, focused on the partners with the largest 3-day-window misses. Second, integration with hospital discharge notifications, so an FLW is alerted within an hour of discharge rather than learning later through community channels. Third, a rebuild of the FLW assignment flow so a discharge notification can be claimed within minutes of being pushed. Whether the combination closes the gap to 70% is what the next two cohorts will tell us. If integration ships and the number does not move, we will redesign the target rather than push harder on the timestamp.

The other open quantitative question is the 3.4-visit average against the 5-visit guardrail. The 4-visit floor is being met for paid SVNs; the 5-visit average is not. We do not yet know whether KMC FLWs will sustain ≥4 visits per case at 5,000+ scale; the current average suggests the 5-visit aspiration may have to become a stretch goal rather than a default.

Learning 4 of 4 · enrollment

Average age at first contact is 12.8 days — under the 28-day target, missing the 3-day target for half.

Across 4,005 KMC cases as of March 20261, the average age at first home visit was 12.8 days. The 28-day enrollment target — derived from the WHO post-discharge follow-up window for SVNs — is met. The 70%-within-3-days target, the more ambitious clinical goal, is not.

The two numbers tell a structural story. Connect KMC is finding the cases. The community channels through which LLOs identify discharged SVNs work — by 12.8 days post-birth, on average, an FLW is in the home. But the 12.8-day average masks a long tail. About half the cases are reached within 3 days; the other half are reached later, sometimes much later, because the FLW learned about the discharge through informal channels rather than a hospital push.

The clinical relevance of the 3-day window is that the highest-risk period for SVN mortality is immediate post-discharge. The first 72 hours is when hypothermia, feeding failure, and undiagnosed infection are most likely to escalate. A first visit on day 12 catches the cases that have already stabilized; a first visit on day 2 catches the cases that have not. The 50% gap is therefore a clinical gap, not just an operational one.

The honest gap: we do not yet know whether discharge-notification integration alone closes the 50% → 70% within-3-days gap, or whether the 3-day target itself needs to be redesigned for the realities of post-discharge logistics in lower-resource settings. The Eleanor Crook Foundation's funding for the next phase is partially allocated to building and testing the integration, and we have committed to publishing the resulting numbers — including the path that the integration does not change the average. A redesigned target is a better outcome than an unmet old one being quietly massaged.
