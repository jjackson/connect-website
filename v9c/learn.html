I.
The method

Three pillars of the Learn module.

01 / Mobile-first

Training on the worker's own phone.

Self-paced. Offline-capable. Interactive lessons, practice scenarios, assessment-driven progression. No classroom time, no travel cost.

02 / Locally relevant

Content approved by program SMEs.

Training materials localised per intervention and region. Approved by subject-matter experts — typically the LLO or a program-specific SME.

03 / Assessment-gated

Pass or don't deliver.

Workers must pass the in-app assessment before they are cleared. Fail means retake. The pass bar is standard across LLOs and FLWs for any program.

II.
Chapter one · Malawi · 2025

Knowledge tests alone don't produce competent FLWs. We tried. They didn't.

Our initial assumption was simple: if a Frontline Worker passes an in-app knowledge test, they are ready to deliver. This is how most digital training operates. It is also how our first pilots were structured.

Iteration after iteration told us something different. Workers who aced the test still struggled with the nuances of in-person counselling — how to handle a sceptical caregiver, how to recognise the subtle danger signs that don't appear in a multiple-choice question, how to practice motivational interviewing instead of lecturing.

"Knowledge and competence are not the same thing. The test measures recall. The field demands judgement, posture, patience."

We layered the model. Self-paced digital learning in Learn; an in-app knowledge test (must pass); daily interaction with an AI coach built on Open Chat Studio; practice with peers; one supervised observation visit rated by an LLO supervisor. Five components, not one.

The AI coach adds what the test can't: scenario-based practice, motivational interviewing drills, daily reflection on real visit experiences, identifying knowledge gaps through conversation rather than multiple choice.

What we changed

Daily AI coach interaction is now a paid activity in the ECD program. The Learn module's job is not just to gate progression — it's to build, with the coach alongside it, the kind of competence the field actually demands.

III.
Chapter two · Malawi pilot · 50 caregiver surveys

We learned caregiver attitudes were already correct. The gap was knowledge.

An ECD intervention, by textbook design, should build positive attitudes about responsive caregiving, talk-and-play, developmental support. That's the frame we brought into the Malawi pilot. It turned out to be wrong.

Baseline surveys with 50 caregivers showed that nearly all already held positive attitudes. They agreed that talk-and-play supports brain development. They endorsed responsive caregiving. Attitude surveys scored high before we started and high after we finished.

"Positive attitudes about ECD were already established. That's not where the intervention needed to go."

Where we moved the needle was knowledge and observed behaviour: +33% on timing of rapid child development, +41% on ways to support mental development, +21% observed teaching behaviour, +21% observed encouragement of autonomy.

Where we didn't: encouragement of autonomy still sits at 47% at endline. Language/thinking skills, 35% correct at endline up from 26%. Work to do.

What we changed

The 3-visit pilot is expanding to 8–10 visits over 12 months. Not because caregivers need more convincing that ECD matters — they know — but because behaviour change takes sustained practice. The program is now evidence-guided, not just evidence-based.

IV.
Chapter three · Kenya pilot · 511 queries

An AI coach turned out to be the part of training that compounds.

The initial Learn module was a closed loop: course, test, pass. Useful, but finite. Once a worker passed, the training ended. Real learning, of course, happens on the job — when something surprises a worker and they need guidance, fast, in context.

We built an AI coach on Dimagi's Open Chat Studio platform. It sits between Learn and Deliver. FLWs interact with the coach daily, paid for their interaction. It handles dosing questions, eligibility clarifications, procedural specifics — the things a supervisor used to answer on a crackly phone call, if at all.

"500 of 511 queries handled without human escalation. Independent evaluation: 88.7% accuracy, 81.5% usefulness."

The coach adds what the test doesn't: scenario practice, motivational interviewing drills, daily reflection on visit experiences, knowledge gap identification through conversation. It's also how ongoing support scales without adding supervisors — a structural answer to the oldest problem in community health.
