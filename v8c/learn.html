Essay I · Learn

Training that survives the doorstep.

Knowledge tests are the easy part. Competence — holding a conversation with a skeptical caregiver, noticing a subtle danger sign, running a motivational-interviewing drill — is the hard part. This is the essay of how we discovered the difference.

Essay I.1

Knowledge tests alone don't produce competent FLWs. We tried. They didn't.

The first version of the Learn app shipped on the assumption that knowledge was the gate. The logic was clean: if a frontline worker can name the MUAC thresholds, describe the danger signs, and identify the right referral pathway, they know what they need to know. Build the curriculum, gate progression on a knowledge test1, and you have a trained worker. For a while, the knowledge-test scores looked the part — cohort after cohort passed.

What changed the picture was supervised observation. When LLO supervisors sat in on actual home visits and rated worker performance, the correlation with test scores was not what we had expected. Workers who had aced the test still struggled with the real thing. Not on recall — on judgment. How do you redirect a caregiver who has already decided not to vaccinate? How do you notice the subtle danger sign in a child who otherwise looks fine? How do you run a motivational-interviewing technique on a noisy porch, with three siblings watching and a half-hour of daylight left? None of these are multiple-choice questions. Our test was grading the wrong thing2.

The redesign was to stop treating Learn as a test-gated linear curriculum and start treating it as a sequence of layers. Content first — self-paced, in the app. Knowledge test still gated, but no longer the final word. Then a daily AI coach built on Dimagi's Open Chat Studio3, which drills scenario-based practice and motivational-interviewing patterns in conversation. Then peer practice. Then a supervised observation visit, scored by an LLO supervisor, as the actual progression gate.

The AI coach is the piece that changed the ceiling. It runs drills a test cannot. "A caregiver tells you she doesn't want the vaccine; what do you say?" — and grades the answer on reasoning, not keyword match. It prompts daily reflection on real visit experiences and surfaces knowledge gaps through conversation. In deployment, the coach handles roughly 97.8% of worker queries without human escalation4. The edges — the 2.2% that do escalate — become training data for the next iteration.

The numbers, across roughly 400 FLWs in iterative experiments spanning CHC, ECD, and KMC5, are the ones we can sleep on. 85% of FLWs progress from training into service delivery. 88% score above 70% on their first observed visit. 77% pass the supervised observation. Workers who do not pass are not "failed"; they get targeted coaching on the specific drill they underperformed on, and most clear the bar on the next attempt.

It is worth saying what we haven't yet established. We don't know, experimentally, the marginal value of each layer. Could we drop peer practice and keep the coach? Could we ship Learn without the supervised observation in very-low-supervision contexts? We're designing the next round of experiments. Until then, the layered model is the system — and what we won't do is collapse it back to a test because the test is easier to measure.

Essay I.2

Caregiver attitudes were already correct. The gap was knowledge and behavior.

The second essay in Learn is not about the worker. It is about what the trained worker is trained to deliver. In the ECD pilot, the intervention as designed was aimed at building positive caregiver attitudes about responsive caregiving and talk-and-play. We assumed caregivers in low-income settings would need to be taught why early childhood development matters. We built visit scripts around that assumption.

The baseline survey6 — 50 caregivers in Malawi — told us we had aimed at the wrong target. Caregivers already held positive ECD attitudes. They agreed talk-and-play supports brain development. They endorsed responsive caregiving. The attitude scales were near ceiling before we started. The intervention was ready to move a variable that did not need moving.

Where the pilot actually produced results was in knowledge and observed behavior. Knowledge of the timing of rapid child development moved +33%. Knowledge of ways to support mental development moved +41%. Observed teaching behavior and observed encouragement of autonomy each moved +21%. Attitudes did not move. They did not need to.

What we changed. The 3-visit pilot is expanding to 8-10 visits across 12 months — not because more time is needed to convince caregivers ECD matters (they know), but because behavior change takes sustained practice7. Learn content for ECD now weights concrete knowledge reinforcement and behavioral rehearsal over attitudinal framing. The AI coach drills caregiver-facing behaviors directly. The intervention is evidence-guided, not merely evidence-based.

One behavior we haven't moved the way we want to: encouragement of autonomy, 47% at endline. It is up from baseline but short of target. Caregivers readily took up teaching behaviors. They were slower to adopt behaviors that require stepping back — letting a toddler struggle, tolerating frustration, resisting the impulse to rescue8. This is the hardest subdomain; we are not done with it.

Marginalia

⁶Baseline and endline caregiver surveys: 50 caregivers, Malawi, ECD pilot 2025-2026. Detailed methodology in ECD Overview.
⁷The 8-10 visit expansion is based on the behavior-change literature and on specific failure modes observed in the 3-visit pilot (knowledge acquired but behavior not sustained).
⁸The autonomy subdomain is recognized in the ECD literature as one of the most resistant to brief-intervention designs.
