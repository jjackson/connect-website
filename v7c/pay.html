Performance-based payment

Workers paid per verified service. KMC: ~$60 per completed case. CHC: $0.78-$1.30 per verified visit. The value and the action are coupled.

PILLAR 02

Direct mobile money

Payments flow to workers' mobile money accounts. No intermediaries. No skimming. No cash logistics. Earnings visible in the Connect app in real time.

PILLAR 03

End-to-end transparency

From funder to frontline, every dollar is tracked. Workers see earnings. LLOs see disbursements. Funders see cost-per-verified-outcome. Same data, every stakeholder.

01
Learning · Economics of scale

Most organizations get less lean as they scale. Connect got leaner.

Every other Dimagi program follows the standard pattern: more partners, more supervision, more ops burden. Connect inverted it — and understanding why took three distinct observations stacked on top of each other.

The orthodoxy

"Scale means bigger teams."

The standard development finance assumption: as a program grows, operational costs grow with it. More LLOs to manage, more workers to supervise, more logistics. Every Dimagi program before Connect showed this pattern. We assumed it would continue.

Founders Pledge grant · Early

The starting cost: $2.20 per verified visit

Early in the grant period, cost per verified CHC visit sat at $2.20. Consistent with expectations. We budgeted assuming that number would stay roughly flat as we scaled — and treated flat as a win.

Grant start: $2.20 / visit
Founders Pledge grant · End

$1.70 per visit. 880K+ visits. Team size: flat.

By the end of the 14-month grant period, cost per verified visit had fallen to $1.70 — a 22% reduction. The platform had scaled to a rate capable of supporting millions of child visits per year without substantial growth in Dimagi's team. In Nigeria (with ORS/Zinc co-packs) the average settled at $1.30/visit across 14 contracts. Outside Nigeria (VAS + deworming only) it averaged $0.78/visit across 17 contracts. One Sierra Leone LLO delivered ORS/Zinc for $1.00/visit.

Grant end: $1.70 / visit
Why it worked — three structural reasons

Lean partners, performance contracts, integrated tech

1. LLOs are inherently low-overhead because they're local and lean. 2. Performance-based payment forces costs to track actual delivery, not organizational size. 3. Digital technology is integrated into every step — worker guidance, verification, payment — creating ongoing efficiency gains that compound as the network grows.

Each reason on its own doesn't flip the orthodoxy. Stacked, they produce a different shape: one where scale creates cost reduction instead of cost creep.

The counterintuitive implication

Most cost-effectiveness ceilings are structural

Most development organizations have a ceiling on cost-effectiveness because their architecture ties cost to organizational size. Connect's architecture breaks that tie. Whether it continues to break it at 10× current scale is something the next grant period will tell us. Setup fees are projected to fall from $0.40/visit today to ~$0.10/visit at larger scale.

Source: Founders Pledge Final Report (Feb 2026). Insight 4 in the public insights log.

02
Learning · The displacement question

Campaign work doesn't displace routine health services. The concern was real. The finding was different.

A common funder worry: paying FLWs for campaign visits will crowd out their unpaid routine health work. We took the worry seriously and went looking for evidence. The evidence was shaped by a Western assumption we didn't know we were making.

The funder concern

"Won't paid work crowd out volunteer work?"

A recurring question, raised by multiple funders: if we pay FLWs to do campaign visits, won't they neglect their regular volunteer health work? The concern assumes the two activities are in zero-sum competition for the worker's time.

What we commissioned

24 in-depth interviews in Nigeria

GiveWell funded a displacement survey: 20 FLWs, 2 LLO staff, 2 local health representatives, across Gombe and Sokoto in Nigeria. Interviewed independently. Objective was to measure whether and how much routine work had been displaced by CHC campaign time.

Interview subjects: 24 across 2 states
What the data said

Coverage held because that's how the system works

Campaign work is normal and expected in Nigeria's community health volunteer system. CHVs routinely do campaigns — it's their main income source, not a departure from their role. When FLWs reduced volunteer hours during campaign periods, other volunteers at the facility covered, as they typically do for any campaign. In Gombe specifically, FLWs maintained volunteer hours by shifting their schedules within Connect's flexible 7am-7pm window.

The deeper finding

The question was based on a Western assumption

The displacement concern was built on an assumption about what community health volunteers do that doesn't apply in the Nigerian context. In the actual system, campaigns are not a disruption — they are the system. FLWs reported substantial income increases. Most said CHC had become a primary income source. The reported secondary effects were overwhelmingly positive: technology skills (CommCare app, Google Maps), improved communication, increased confidence.

We now present this finding to every new funder raising the displacement question. The context matters, and the context here is that we were worried about something the local system had already solved.

Source: GiveWell FLW Displacement Survey (Oct 2025). Insight 5 in the public insights log.
