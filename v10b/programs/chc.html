the challenge // why chc

Coverage gaps at the exact households equity requires.

Child health campaigns — vitamin A supplementation, deworming, MUAC screening for acute malnutrition, ORS for dehydration, vaccine promotion — are among the most cost-effective interventions in global health. The science is unambiguous. The delivery, at scale, is where the gap opens.

Conventional door-to-door campaigns hand FLWs a catchment area and a quota. Predictably, workers reach easier households first. The populations most at risk — the remote, the mobile, the marginalized — get missed, exactly the populations the campaign was designed to reach. Monitoring catches this after the money is spent, too late to correct.

CHC on Connect exists to close that gap with mechanics: GPS-enforced microplanning, biometric verification, algorithmic fraud detection, and performance-based contracts with locally-led organizations. The combination produces coverage, thoroughness, and cost numbers that most door-to-door campaigns cannot.

how it works // delivery_stack

The CHC delivery stack.

step 01

LLO activation via EOI

Simple Expression of Interest process: brief scope doc + Google Form + Dimagi network. A single EOI produced ~107 applications.

step 02

Trial Run model

New LLOs get $3K setup for 3K visits. Evaluated on actual delivery. Scaled up or not continued based on field performance.

step 03

Digital training + AI coach

FLWs complete self-paced Learn modules, pass an in-app assessment, then use WhatsApp-based AI coach (Open Chat Studio) for ongoing questions.

step 04

Microplanned delivery

Grid overlay from Google Open Buildings + OSM. FLWs close every Delivery Unit in a Service Area before unlocking the next.

step 05

Four-layer verification

Biometric identity, GPS on every visit, photo capture, algorithmic fraud detection on submission patterns.

step 06

Mobile money payment

FLWs paid per verified visit. $0.78–$1.30 depending on commodity bundle. Direct to worker's mobile money account.

learning 01 // partner_selection

The Trial Run replaced pre-vetting because pre-vetting didn't predict performance.

scope=chc The conventional procurement playbook says vet partners thoroughly before contracting them. We tried it. Capability statements. Reference calls. Site visits. Past portfolio review. We built rank-ordered shortlists from the EOI applications, contracted the top names, and watched the rankings fail to predict who would deliver.

Some highly-vetted partners under-delivered. Some less obvious organizations — smaller, newer, less polished on paper — turned in clean campaigns. The pre-qualification stack was sorting for proposal-writing capacity, which is a real skill but not the one we were paying for.

The Trial Run as a filter

The reframe was to stop trying to predict and start trying to measure. We invented the Trial Run: a small contract — $3,000 setup payment for 3,000 verified child visits — that lets the LLO show, not tell. The Trial is sized to be small enough to underwrite without conviction, but large enough that you cannot fake your way through it. The decision to continue is based on measured field performance, not on the original proposal.

The conversion funnel from the CHC cohort: 107 EOI applications, 37 contracted under Trial Run terms, 24 advanced to full Trials, 14 passed and ran larger campaigns, 10 underperformed and were not continued, and 3 went straight to larger campaigns based on prior measured performance with us. We publish this funnel because diligence readers ask. We also publish it because the alternative — opaque vendor lists — is precisely what funders are trying to escape.

What Trial Run does and doesn't capture

The model captures field execution: can this LLO recruit FLWs, train them on the platform, run a microplanned campaign, hit thoroughness numbers, and clear our verification stack at scale? Those are the binding constraints in delivery. The model does not capture deeper organizational health — financial sustainability, governance, capacity to grow — and we don't pretend it does. Trial Run is a delivery filter, not an institutional certification.

The selection criteria that drive the 65% Trial pass rate are also not yet codified into a model an outside funder could replicate. Today they're judgment calls by program managers, calibrated by the Trial outcomes themselves. That's a real limitation; we name it in the Open Questions block.

learning 02 // coverage_geometry

Microplans turned 0.4 visits-per-child into 1.4 — and pulled coverage from 84% to 94%.

scope=chc The hardest problem in field campaigns wasn't fraud — it was the invisible choice of which households to skip. Without geographic enforcement, FLWs averaged 0.4 visits per estimated eligible child, cherry-picking accessible households and skipping the harder ones. The populations systematically missed were those with lower baseline health coverage. At the exact moment equity was the goal, we were producing convenience sampling.

The grid intervention

Before a campaign, we overlay a grid onto the operational area using Google Open Buildings, OpenStreetMap, and Microsoft data. Cells with at least one building become Delivery Units. DUs group into Service Areas sized for one to two weeks of work. A worker cannot advance to the next Service Area until every DU in the current one is closed — either completing the visits or confirming that no eligible children are present.

The grid takes the choice out of the FLW's hands. Coverage stops being a property of effort and becomes a property of geometry.

What it produced

1.4 visits per estimated child in microplanned cells, vs 0.4 without — a 3.5× increase in thoroughness. Population coverage in visited cells jumped from 82-84% to 94%. To reach 95% of the population, microplanned campaigns needed 91% of cells; non-microplanned needed 72% — revealing how concentrated coverage was in the easy cells before the grid was enforced.

Where the grid breaks down

Microplans failed in dispersed settlement patterns, where workers walked long distances to DUs with no actual buildings. Some DUs fell on inaccessible land — factory grounds, prison grounds. One CHC campaign in CAR removed microplan execution entirely due to partner constraints. The grid is not universal; it suits density. Open work: making the approach robust to sparse or irregular terrain, and finding the threshold beyond which the grid creates more friction than it removes.

learning 03 // adversarial_fraud

We paid people to defeat our fraud detection. 97.5% of real workers scored cleaner than the worst paid faker.

scope=chc.adversarial Most fraud-detection claims in the development sector are aspirational. We ran the adversarial study so we could report a number instead of a promise. We paid experienced FLWs to submit 100 fake visit records each, ran three rounds, coached fakers on what had given them away between rounds, and offered bonuses to the top 25% most convincing fabricators. The escalating coaching is the part that matters: we were not testing against naïve adversaries.

The result

A Random Forest model trained on three data elements — age, gender, and MUAC measurements — achieved an AUC of 0.91 ± 0.09. Only 2.5% of real workers received fraud risk scores higher than even the lowest-scoring fabricator. 97.5% of real workers were cleaner than the worst paid faker. This is statistical discrimination alone — before GPS and photo verification kick in.

How the four layers compound

A fabricated visit on Connect has to beat all four independent verification layers — biometric identity, GPS + microplan constraints, photo capture, and statistical pattern detection. Each layer uses different signal. Coordinating a fake that defeats all of them requires more effort than delivering the visit honestly. That's the design intent: make honest delivery the cheapest path. The adversarial test stresses only the algorithmic layer; the other three compound on top.

Where this generalizes — and where it doesn't

The 97.5% figure is from paid-bonus testing in CHC. Production false-positive rates against unmotivated fabrication aren't yet published; we will when we have a clean cohort comparison. The mechanism — Random Forest on a small set of program-relevant fields — is portable, but the specific fields and AUC curves will differ in non-CHC programs (KMC uses different vital signs; ECD uses behavioral observations).

learning 04 // cost_inversion

Cost per verified visit fell 22% as CHC scaled — and we publish the bundle, not just the average.

scope=chc In the CHC program, cost per verified visit fell 22% — from $2.20 to $1.70 — over the 14-month FP grant window. Connect's architecture (LLO economics + performance-based payment + integrated tooling) is designed to produce cost-reducing scale; CHC is the program where we've measured it. Most platforms report cost-per-FLW-trained or cost-per-program-launched. Those numbers hide the lever that matters: whether the work was done.

The bundle the average hides

The $1.70 weighted average masks a real difference in commodity bundle. Nigeria CHC with ORS/Zinc co-packs averaged $1.30 across 14 LLO contracts. Outside Nigeria — VAS + deworming, no ORS — averaged $0.78 across 17 contracts. Setup fees are running $0.40/visit during the grant and projected to fall to ~$0.10/visit at scale. Fully-loaded: commodity + FLW + LLO + 20% Connect platform fee. Anybody comparing CHC to another program needs the comparable bundle, not a flattened average.

Why we picked verified-visit cost as the metric

The asymmetry between activity-cost and outcome-cost is exactly where most development funding gets eaten. Cost-per-FLW-trained sounds rigorous but tells you nothing about whether the FLW ever delivered a visit. Cost-per-app-installed says even less. Cost-per-verified-visit ties the funder's dollar to the unit of work the program is actually buying. Verified-visit cost can itself be gamed if the verification stack is shallow; we publish the four-layer stack (biometric, GPS, photo, algorithmic) so readers can audit it.

The next test

Whether the same year-over-year cost curve holds in non-CHC programs is open. We're funding an RCT with PRO Impact (IPA evaluation, $1.5M, 2026) to produce a defensible counterfactual. The result is the test of whether the cost numbers and pace claims survive an external evaluator. We will publish whatever it shows.
