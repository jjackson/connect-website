The challenge

Presbyopia is correctable. Distribution is the bottleneck.

Presbyopia — the age-related loss of near-vision focusing ability — is one of the most easily corrected conditions in global health. A pair of off-the-shelf reading glasses, in the correct dioptre, restores near-vision function in a single fitting. The intervention is not the science. The intervention is finding the adults who need them, screening at the right level, and getting the right pair into their hands without a clinic visit they will not make.

Connect Readers is a partnership with RestoringVision, who is donating 15,000 reading-glasses pairs for distribution in northeast Nigeria. Two LLO partners have been finalized — eHA and C3HD — and contract signing is pending as we open this case study. Field-performance data will not exist until the first cohort runs in 2026; this page is therefore lighter on numbers and heavier on what we expect to learn.

Learning · 01 · Platform applied to a new domain

What changes when the visit isn't counseling.

Most of Connect's existing depth is in interventions where the visit is fundamentally a counseling encounter — child health screening, neonatal home support, parenting visits. The verification stack and the training stack were both designed for those shapes. Readers is the first program where the visit is primarily a screening and dispensing event: confirm presbyopia, fit the correct dioptre, hand over the pair, capture verified completion.

That changes a few things. The training-stack question shifts: the FLW needs the screening protocol nailed, but the in-person counseling load is lower than in CHC or ECD. The verification-stack question gains a new dimension: what counts as proof of correct dispensing? GPS, photo, biometric identity, and submission-pattern detection are all there, but the new specific signal is whether the dispensed pair matched the diagnosed need. The honest answer to that question for the first cohort is: we'll find out.

The Trial Run model carries over cleanly. Both LLO partners will start with a small initial contract sized to verified screening + dispensing volume. If the first cohort delivers, larger volumes follow. If not, we learn something specific about what didn't port — which is the most useful thing to learn early.

The interesting question isn't whether the platform works. It's what new shapes the verification stack has to take.

Learning · 02 · The targeting question

Door-to-door against an adult-age target band, without targeting infrastructure.

Most Connect programs target a population that is identifiable by a household visit: children 0–59 months, newborns ≤2000g, families with infants under three. The Readers target population — adults 35+ with near-vision impairment — is harder to find through a door-knock alone. Households contain mixed age bands. Presbyopia varies by individual, not by household. The screening protocol therefore has to be efficient enough to test multiple adults per household without slowing the visit beyond what the LLO economics support.

The first cohort will tell us whether the screening protocol holds quality consistency across novice FLWs trained only digitally — i.e., without an in-person clinic-style apprenticeship. The Learn stack here is the same five-layer model used in CHC and ECD (digital course, knowledge gate, AI coach, peer practice, supervised observation), but applied to a clinical-screening encounter rather than a counseling one. The question of whether that stack produces consistent screening accuracy across a novice cohort is empirical and not yet answered.

We don't yet have field-performance data to publish. We will when the first cohort runs.
