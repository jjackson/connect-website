Q1 · the gap · N11 triangulated ⟶ insights#n11

Why structured at-home KMC follow-up — and why does it barely exist?

Kangaroo Mother Care is one of the most well-evidenced interventions in newborn health. Skin-to-skin contact, exclusive breastfeeding, and structured monitoring for small or vulnerable newborns reduces neonatal mortality by up to 40%1. WHO 2022 guidance recommends KMC for all newborns ≤2,000g. The mechanism is not in dispute.

And yet global KMC coverage for eligible infants is below 5%. The bottleneck is not the in-hospital phase — many facilities have KMC corners and trained nursing staff. The bottleneck is the post-discharge phase: families go home within days of birth, structured at-home follow-up rarely exists, and the small or vulnerable newborn enters the period of highest mortality risk without active monitoring.

Connect KMC is designed to close that post-discharge gap with 4–5 home visits in the first 60 days. Each visit is a structured KMC encounter: weight, temperature, oxygen saturation, danger-sign screening, breastfeeding support, and family coaching. The visit data flows into Connect's verification stack; danger signs auto-trigger referrals. The mechanism is taken from KMC's published evidence base; the platform's contribution is the operational scaffolding that makes 4–5 structured visits actually happen for thousands of families.

What the platform changes about the post-discharge phase is that it makes the visits durable as data, not just as encounters. A nurse seeing a baby once at the hospital can give excellent advice and never know whether that advice survived the trip home. A Connect KMC FLW visiting on day 3, day 10, day 21, day 42, and day 60 produces a longitudinal record of weight gain, feeding difficulties, hypothermia events, and missed milestones that maps directly onto the WHO-recommended monitoring schedule. The 4,005 KMC cases in our system as of March 2026 are also a published evidence base — early evidence, with named gaps (see Q3) — that is itself part of the global library on what at-home KMC follow-up actually looks like at scale.

Q2 · FLW equipment · N19 hard data ⟶ insights#n19

What does an FLW need to actually carry to a KMC home visit, and how do we close the loop on referrals?

Every Connect KMC home visit, the FLW arrives with a portable scale, thermometer, pulse oximeter, and measuring tape2. The Connect app captures each reading. Danger-sign thresholds are encoded in the app: poor weight gain, hypothermia, low oxygen saturation, signs of infection — any of these auto-triggers a referral, and the app prompts follow-up at the next visit to confirm the family acted on the referral.

The equipment standard is a deliberate choice. Pulse oximetry in particular is not standard in many community-health programs because the devices are more expensive and require more care. We include it because hypoxia is one of the leading clinical signs in deteriorating SVNs, and a home visit without it is a much weaker monitoring instrument. The app's job is to make the readings durable: every measurement is timestamped, GPS-tagged, photo-verified where applicable, and tied to the same case ID across the 4–5 visit sequence.

Closing the loop on referrals is the part where the data matters most. When a referral is triggered, Connect notifies the LLO supervisor and tracks whether the family reached the referral site. The next visit prompts the FLW to confirm the outcome. The chain — danger-sign threshold, referral, follow-up confirmation — is the part that separates structured at-home KMC from a check-in. Without that chain, a danger sign is just a data point; with it, a danger sign is an action with a measurable outcome.

The honest gap on equipment: availability across the 6 KMC LLO partners has been uneven. Some partners have full kits with backup oximeter batteries on every FLW; others have shared equipment that rotates between FLWs. We don't yet have a published audit of equipment uptime — what fraction of visits had a working pulse oximeter, for instance, across the 4,005 cases. That audit is on the roadmap, and the data will be published when it lands. We flag it here rather than papering over it because equipment uptime is a precondition for the danger-sign-to-referral chain to work as designed.

Q3 · guardrails · I10 · N10 · N20 hard data ⟶ verify#guardrails ⟶ insights#n10

How do we honestly track a target we are currently failing — without training FLWs to falsify the data?

KMC's enrollment-timing target is the live example for Connect's guardrails-vs-payment-criteria philosophy. The 4 mandatory home visits per SVN is a payment criterion — auditable, enforced, the gate for FLW pay. The "first visit within 3 days of discharge" is a guardrail — audited, not enforced. Punishment for missing the 3-day window would train workers to falsify the discharge date. We chose not to.

Across 4,005 KMC cases as of March 20263, the data: average age at first contact is 12.8 days — well under the 28-day enrollment guardrail. But for half of cases, FLWs are missing the first 3 days. Current performance: 50% within 3 days against a 70% target. The cause is not FLW effort. The cause is that hospital discharge notifications aren't yet integrated, so the FLW often does not learn a baby has been discharged until days later.

Punishing FLWs for this gap would produce falsified timestamps, not faster contact. So we publish the 50%, integrate discharge notifications, and coach FLWs on faster contact. Platforms that punish guardrails don't get honest data; we'd rather report the gap and have it move than report 90% and have it be a fiction. The Verify page treats the philosophy in full.

What's also working: across the same 4,005 cases, the 12.8-day average age at first contact comfortably beats the 28-day target. That number has been stable across LLOs and across the full year of data — it's the evidence that the platform-side scaffolding (LLO activation, FLW dispatch, app prompts) is sound. The gap is upstream of Connect, in the hospital-discharge information flow.

Source: KMC Conceptual Model (March 2026) — 4,005 cases; 50% within-3-days vs 70% target; 12.8-day average age at first contact. [3]
Q4 · visit completion · N20 hypothesis ⟶ insights#n20

Will FLWs sustain ≥4 visits per case at 5,000+ scale?

The KMC payment model pays per the 4-visit minimum. That is the binding criterion: an FLW does not get paid for a case until the four visits are complete. The current cohort is at 3.4 visits-per-case on average — short of the 5-visit guardrail and only marginally clear of the 4-visit floor.

The shortfall is not uniform across LLOs. Some partners are reliably hitting 4.5+; others are clustering at 3.4–3.8. The pattern correlates with FLW caseload: partners running denser caseloads are dropping the fifth visit. The cause appears to be operational rather than refusal-driven — the fifth visit is the easiest to drop when an FLW is juggling too many concurrent cases. Connect's app-side prompts and LLO-side caseload caps are the levers we have available.

The honest projection: at 5,000+ cases scale we expect the average to sit at or above 4 as we tighten LLO-level caseload caps. Whether we hit the 5-visit guardrail is the open question. The 5th visit is the one that captures the late-monitoring data point — weight gain trajectory, late hypoxia, breastfeeding consolidation — and dropping it changes the program's clinical signal. The answer to "will we hit it" is the work of the next quarter.

This is also a deliberate use of the guardrails-vs-payment-criteria distinction (Q3). Pay is gated on 4; 5 is the guardrail. Pushing the 5th visit into the payment gate would create the same incentive to falsify dates that we have refused to create on the 3-day enrollment window. The honest path is operational — caseload caps, FLW prompts, scheduling support — not punitive. We will publish the post-redesign visit-completion data when the next cohort closes, and if the 5th-visit number remains stuck below the guardrail we will redesign the visit cadence rather than punish the gap.

Source: KMC Conceptual Model (March 2026) — visit-completion data across 4,005 cases. [3]

Open questions on KMC

  1. Whether discharge-notification integration alone closes the 50% → 70% within-3-days enrollment gap, or the 3-day target itself needs to be redesigned.
  2. Equipment availability across the 6 KMC LLO partners has been uneven. We don't yet have a published audit of equipment uptime.
  3. Whether KMC FLWs will sustain ≥4 visits per case at the 5,000+ scale; current average is 3.4 against a 5-visit guardrail.
