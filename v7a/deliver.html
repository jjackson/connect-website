How It Works/Deliver

Once workers pass training, they begin delivering services in their communities using a mobile app that guides every interaction — registration, counseling, screening, treatment, distribution. LLOs run the local operations. The platform handles the rest. This page is about how we scale that without lying to ourselves about who we can work with.

01

We stopped pre-vetting partners. Now we let the work do the vetting.

For most of its history, development funding has been filtered through a pre-selection process. Check the track record. Interview the team. Evaluate the capacity. Sign a contract for the campaign and hope. That is standard procurement, and when we started Connect, we did what everyone does. We vetted.

It did not work. Pre-selection was a poor predictor of field performance. Past reputation, team size, organizational maturity — none of it reliably told us whether an LLO would actually show up, run the campaign, and verify results. Some of the best-looking organizations on paper were the ones that dropped off. Some of the smallest and newest were the ones that delivered the most thoroughly.

So we inverted the model. We now contract new LLOs through a Trial Run: a small initial engagement — $3,000 setup fee for 3,000 verified child visits — that costs us relatively little if it fails and tells us almost everything we need to know. Performance-based payment means Dimagi and the funder bear minimal financial risk. If the LLO does not deliver, we do not renew, and the money was not lost on organizational overhead; it was lost on the small fraction of the campaign that was attempted.

Under the Founders Pledge grant we contracted 37 LLOs this way. Twenty-four entered Trial Runs. Of those, 17 completed the campaign well enough to move on to larger contracts. Ten underperformed and were not continued. Three went straight to larger campaigns based on prior track record and were held to the same performance bar. The funder's dollars followed delivery, not promises.

The part of this that matters for the rest of the field is the generality. The Trial Run model is sector-agnostic. It was designed for Child Health Campaigns, and it has been reused without modification for reading glasses distribution, Kangaroo Mother Care, and chlorine dispensers. The structure — small first contract, performance-based payment, scale-up on proven delivery — travels. It is how we can activate new partners in fragile contexts without betting the grant on them.

The caveat is the selection step above Trial Run: the Expression of Interest. A single CHC EOI drew roughly 100 applications, of which we brought 37 to contract. That filtering is still opinion work. The Trial Run only tells us about the ones we already chose to try. The next generation of this model needs a better way to open the top of the funnel without drowning in applications — we are not there yet.

02

The real intervention is not what is in the app. It is what locally-led organizations do with it.

A platform can describe vaccine hesitancy in its training module. It can list the common myths. It can script the counter-arguments. It cannot walk into a village and ask the imam to come to the meeting. That is what the locally-led half of LLO actually means, and we learned how load-bearing it was by watching a vaccine campaign unfold without us stage-managing it.

In the CLP Stage 2 vaccine promotion campaign, seven LLOs operated largely on their own using the Connect platform. Our digital training covered vaccine myths in detail. The FLWs read it, passed the test, and went into the field. What they did next was not in the curriculum. Five or six of the seven LLOs independently leveraged religious leaders, community leaders, or existing community relationships as part of their outreach strategy. We did not ask. We did not train. They did it because they are of the community.

The specifics were vivid. One FLW, countering a rumor about vaccine side effects, used a personal example: "We were immunized, we have children." Another LLO had FLWs who were recommended by the community itself, which meant they arrived with the kind of credibility that an external hire cannot manufacture. A third arranged their meeting with the local religious authority before the door-to-door work even began, so that the first household they visited had already heard the pastor endorse the campaign.

This is why the L in LLO is load-bearing. Dimagi cannot centralize this knowledge. There is no version of the platform where a worker in our Boston office picks the right community elder for Sokoto, Nigeria. Trying to would be worse than not trying: it would claim an authority the platform does not have and cannot earn. The platform's job is to enable this kind of local adaptation — to get out of its way.

What we do with this insight operationally is quiet. We resist building features that would standardize community outreach. We keep the counter-messaging content in Learn as a baseline, not as a ceiling. We leave budget and schedule flexibility for LLOs to adapt to what their own communities need. And we fight the instinct — which every platform eventually has — to turn local craft into a checklist.

Where this gets harder is evaluation. How do you credit the LLO for outreach work that is not in the system? We are experimenting with letting LLOs note outreach investments in the app, but we have not built the right data structure for it. The thing that matters most about our program is also the thing we are worst at measuring.

03

The Central African Republic did not work. Publishing that is part of the practice.

Connect's activation model — remote EOI, Trial Run, performance-based payment — was designed to be portable. The claim, for a while, was that it could be applied to almost any context where a willing LLO and a willing funder lined up. The Central African Republic tested that claim and it did not hold.

We contracted two LLOs in CAR under the standard Connect activation process. Both dropped out. Neither of them failed for a lack of will. They failed because the context itself was harder than the activation model was designed for: staffing instability, unreliable connectivity, and the coordination burden of operating remotely with a platform team thousands of miles away. The remote model — which works well in Kenya, Nigeria, and Uganda — did not transfer.

We could have framed this as a limitation of those particular LLOs. That would be convenient and wrong. The LLOs were not the variable; the operating environment was. What we learned is that activating partners in fragile contexts likely requires dedicated in-country presence, not just a platform and a contract. Remote coordination alone is not enough when the thing being coordinated is itself being interrupted weekly by power outages, staffing turnover, or worse.

We are publishing this for two reasons. The first is the internal one: it shapes how we decide where to activate next. We are not repeating the same approach in similarly fragile geographies without either an in-country Dimagi presence or an LLO partner that is strong enough to absorb the overhead themselves. The second is external. A platform that only talks about its wins is not a platform that can be trusted with the next hard context. If we are asking funders to bet on a cost model that compounds with scale, we owe them a clear picture of where the model does not reach.

The question we are sitting with is what dedicated in-country presence actually costs, and whether it can be structured in a way that does not undermine Connect's core cost advantage — which is that the operations are lean by construction. A permanent in-country team in every fragile geography is unaffordable. A traveling team is partial. We think the answer is that we partner with stronger, higher-capacity LLOs in fragile contexts and invest differently in activation support, but we are early on that, and we do not yet have the evidence that it works any better than CAR did.
