Programs/Kangaroo Mother Care
Maternal & newborn health · Live program

Closing the post-discharge gap for small and vulnerable newborns.

Every year, 2.3 million newborns die in the first 28 days of life — nearly half of all deaths in children under five. Most are born too small or too early. Kangaroo Mother Care can reduce their mortality by up to 40%. Global coverage sits below 5%. Connect KMC is how we close that gap, one post-discharge home at a time.

The challenge.

The story of neonatal mortality is a story of the first 28 days. An estimated 2.3 million newborns die in that window each year — nearly half of all deaths in children under five. 80% are low birth weight. Two-thirds are preterm. These are the babies we call small and vulnerable newborns, or SVNs.

Kangaroo Mother Care — continuous skin-to-skin contact with the mother, exclusive breastfeeding, close follow-up — is one of the most proven interventions in global newborn health. It can reduce neonatal mortality by up to 40%. The WHO recommends it for all newborns ≤2000g at birth. It is cheap, it does not require equipment beyond a wrap, and it works.

And yet KMC coverage for eligible infants remains below 5% globally. The reason is structural. Hospitals provide the initial care, then discharge families within days. What is supposed to happen after discharge — the sustained skin-to-skin contact, the feeding support, the danger-sign screening — happens in homes where nobody is watching. Routine follow-up does not exist. Connect KMC is built to provide the follow-up that does not otherwise happen.

Five pillars

What happens in each of the four (or more) home visits.

Identification at discharge

LLOs partner with health facilities. FLWs meet caregivers before discharge, explain KMC, and invite enrollment. First visit ideally within three days.

Structured home visits

Minimum 4 mandatory visits per SVN; program pays for up to 5; more for high-need cases. All within 60 days. At least one at 28+ days.

Counseling and practice

FLWs help caregivers practice sustained skin-to-skin contact using a KMC wrap the program provides. Counseling on exclusive breastfeeding and hygiene.

Danger-sign screening & referral

FLWs carry a scale, thermometer, pulse oximeter, and measuring tape. Danger signs flagged in the app trigger referrals — auto-prompted for follow-up at the next visit.

Emotional support

Many SVN mothers face stigma and isolation. Consistent FLW presence builds confidence and trust — a component that does not show up in the WHO guidance but shows up in every interview with a family.

WHO alignment

Aligned with the WHO Global Position Paper on KMC, the Every Newborn Action Plan, and SDG 3.2. We work with WHO-informed standards, not against them.

How do you enforce a minimum visit count without turning the rest of the protocol into a lie?

The KMC protocol, as we set it up, has two kinds of rule. One kind is a payment criterion. The other kind is a guardrail. Knowing which is which is the most important design decision we have made in this program.

The payment criterion is clear. A KMC case requires a minimum of four verified home visits per small or vulnerable newborn, all within 60 days of enrollment, with at least one visit occurring at 28 days or later so that the neonatal period is fully covered. If any of those conditions fail, the payment does not move. This is hard-coded in the platform; it is not a preference.

The guardrail is the rule about enrollment within three days of discharge. This matters clinically — the first post-discharge days are when infection, hypothermia, and feeding failure are most common. We have set a target of 70% of cases enrolled within that window. Current performance against that target is 50%. The guardrail is missing. The platform flags it, we track it, we report it to the funder, and we invest in fixing it — through LLO coaching, better scheduling support, and discharge-notification integration with partner hospitals. But we do not withhold payment for it.

The reason we do not withhold is the one we argue on the Verify page and that we have learned is load-bearing across the platform: punishment produces dishonest data. If we made the three-day window a hard payment stop, a worker arriving on day four would face a choice between a truthful record (no pay) and a backdated record (paid). Under financial pressure, enough of them would choose the second option that we would stop knowing whether the three-day target was being met at all. Our 50% would become a statistically perfect 70%, and nothing on the ground would have changed.

Instead we keep the real distribution. We know average age at enrollment is 12.8 days, which is safely under the 28-day target. We know average FLW visits per case is 3.4, below the 5-visit guardrail. We know the early discharge rate is 7.6%, slightly above our 5% target. These numbers are not flattering. They are the data we need to improve the program, and they exist because payment does not depend on them.

What is still unresolved is whether 50% enrollment-within-three-days is moveable with the levers we have — LLO coaching, scheduling support, hospital-discharge API integration — or whether some of the gap is structural. Discharge happens on weekends. Hospitals do not always notify the LLO in time. Families travel home to villages several hours away before the FLW can reach them. We are working on each of these, but we are not yet at 70%, and we are not pretending otherwise.

Source: Connect KMC program data, March 2026 (n = 4,005 cases); Connect insight 10 — guardrails vs hard stops.

Why build a KMC program at all, when the WHO already has guidance for it?

The WHO's 2022 KMC guidance is one of the strongest documents in maternal and child health. It is clear, it is evidence-backed, and it tells facilities almost everything they need to know about how to initiate KMC in the hospital. The reason KMC coverage globally is still below 5% is not that the guidance is wrong. It is that the guidance describes what should happen, and the platform is the part that describes who will do the work after discharge, how they will be paid, and how we will know they did it.

Connect KMC is designed to operate downstream of the WHO guidance. The program aligns with the WHO Global Position Paper on KMC, the Every Newborn Action Plan, and Sustainable Development Goal 3.2 (reducing neonatal deaths to fewer than 12 per 1,000 livebirths by 2030). All of the clinical content in the Learn app — positioning, danger-sign recognition, breastfeeding troubleshooting — is derived from that guidance. We have not invented a clinical practice. We have built the operational spine that lets it reach families who are otherwise on their own.

What Connect adds is the infrastructure the guidance cannot provide. The WHO document cannot tell a Ugandan locally-led organization how to recruit a Frontline Worker, train them digitally, verify their home visits with GPS and biometrics, pay them on time, and generate evidence a funder will accept. That is a different kind of problem — a coordination problem, a verification problem, and a payment problem. Those are the problems Connect solves, and they are what separates a program from a guideline.

The payment architecture is worth naming. $60 per verified completed case, covering up to 5 visits per SVN. Split across the LLO, the FLW, and a 20% Connect platform fee. Payment triggered by digitally-verified home visit completion. That structure matters clinically because it means there is no one in the chain — not the LLO, not the FLW, not the platform — whose income rises when a visit is skipped. The incentive aligns with the guidance.

The open problem is what happens at hospitals that do not engage. KMC initiation in the hospital is the step before discharge, and our program begins after discharge. If a hospital is not starting KMC in its neonatal unit, the whole chain is weaker. We are working with partner hospitals like those served by Preterm Infants Parents Network Uganda (PIPNU) to strengthen the hospital-to-home handoff, but this is not a part of the program Connect itself can fix. The platform is the downstream piece. The upstream piece needs institutional partners who are already moving in the right direction.

Source: WHO Global Position Paper on KMC; Every Newborn Action Plan; Connect KMC Conceptual Model, March 2026.

What does it mean that first-visit-within-three-days is at 50% when the target is 70%?

It means the program is working and the program is incomplete, both at once. We have enrolled over 4,000 SVN cases. The average age at enrollment is 12.8 days, comfortably under the 28-day neonatal threshold. Most babies are being reached. But the specific clinical goal — first visit within three days of discharge, the window where hypothermia, infection, and feeding problems are most lethal — is being hit only half the time.

When we went looking for where the gap was, we found it was not in any single place. It was distributed across the handoff. Hospitals do not always notify the LLO on the day of discharge. Families travel home — sometimes hours by road — before an FLW can be assigned. Weekend discharges are particularly hard: the LLO's scheduling systems are often not staffed to react in real time on a Saturday. FLWs are assigned cases late and the 72-hour window closes before they can be reached.

So we have been rebuilding the handoff. Three interventions are in flight. First, LLO coaching: we are working with partner LLOs on their own scheduling practices — how quickly they can assign a new case to an FLW, what their SLA looks like on a weekend, how they handle discharges from the facility that happen at 5pm on a Friday. This is the lowest-tech of the three and also the most immediately impactful.

Second, discharge notification integration. We are working with partner hospitals to have the discharge event trigger a notification to the LLO inside the same day, rather than through a manual process that batches weekly. This is technically straightforward, operationally complex, and slow — it depends on the hospital's information systems, which are not ours.

Third, scheduling support in the LLO app. When an LLO has three newly-discharged SVNs to assign, which FLW should get which case? The app now surfaces the distance, the current caseload, and the scheduled windows of each FLW so that the assignment is not a manual triage on a WhatsApp group. Small, measurable improvement in the time between discharge and first visit.

What is unresolved is whether these three together will close the 20-point gap or only shrink it. Some of the gap might be structural — some discharges will always happen in contexts that make a 72-hour visit impossible. If that is true, the right answer is to explicitly acknowledge it, report a lower target, and redirect the attention to the absolute number of SVNs reached. We will learn which kind of problem this is by running the interventions for another year and measuring.

Source: Connect KMC program data, March 2026; ongoing coaching work with PIPNU, Nama Wellness, Global Health Innovations, Ansh, BERI, eHealth Africa.
