Learning 01 · Pre-vetting doesn't work · Platform mechanism

LLO performance can't be predicted. Only proven, in small contracts.

The procurement-style approach to partner selection failed us. Pre-2024, we ran the conventional process: review track records, assess team composition, evaluate organizational capacity, reference-check with prior funders, then sign a contract sized to the campaign and hope the diligence was right. This is how development contracting has worked for decades. It's how we started too.

Pre-selection was a poor predictor of field performance1. Organizations with strong track records in other programs stumbled on Connect visits. Organizations that seemed marginal on paper delivered at scale. Team size, organizational maturity, past-funder references — none of it reliably forecasted what actually happened in the field. The signal we wanted — "will this LLO deliver verified visits efficiently?" — wasn't encoded in the signals we were checking.

$3,000 setup. 3,000 verified visits. Performance-based. The cheapest form of evidence about an organization's operational fit.

So we invented the Trial Run. New LLOs receive a small initial contract: $3,000 setup fee for 3,000 verified child visits. Payment is tied to actual delivery. If they can execute, they roll into a larger campaign. If they can't, the financial exposure is capped and the next partner can be tried quickly. We bear minimal risk. They prove it in the field. Pre-selection became a thinner filter; the real selection happens downstream, where the signal actually lives.

Under the Founders Pledge grant, the funnel was clean and public. ~100 EOI applications received. 37 LLOs contracted under Trial Run terms. 24 entered Trial Runs; 10 underperformed and weren't continued. 3 went straight to larger campaigns based on prior evidence from inside the network. 17 completed full campaigns following successful trials1. The 65% conversion through Trial Run is the operating filter.

The Trial Run model has now been reused, without modification, for KMC, Readers, and chlorine dispensers. It's sector-agnostic because it doesn't rely on any domain-specific features of CHC — it relies on the idea that small, performance-based contracts are the cheapest form of evidence about an organization's operational fit. Pre-selection is an expensive way to be wrong. The Trial Run is a cheap way to find out.

Learning 02 · Why the L matters · CHC, CLP Stage 2 vaccine campaign

LLOs navigate vaccine hesitancy with religious leaders. We didn't teach them this.

The most important intervention in a vaccine campaign wasn't in our app. It was what the LLO did with the platform once they were operating in their own community — and we learned the hard way that we couldn't centralize that knowledge.

Our first design instinct, in early 2025, was to handle vaccine hesitancy through content. The Learn app would cover common myths, medical facts, counseling scripts. Read the module, pass the test, you're equipped. That was the plan. It wasn't wrong, exactly — just incomplete in a way that the field made obvious.

Across seven LLOs in the CLP Stage 2 vaccine campaign, we observed that five or six independently leveraged religious leaders, community leaders, or existing community relationships as part of their outreach strategy — before we asked, before the app prompted it1. One FLW countered hesitancy with a personal example: "We were immunized, we have children." Another LLO had FLWs who had been recommended by the community itself, lending credibility before any door was knocked on. The platform didn't teach this. The LLOs already knew it.

The real intervention isn't what's in the app. It's what locally-led organizations do with the platform when they operate in their own communities.

So we stopped writing more content about hesitancy. We started designing the platform to get out of the way. The Deliver layer now assumes the LLO knows how to talk to their community better than we do. Our job is to make the mechanics — scheduling, visit flow, verification, payment — frictionless enough that the LLO can pour their attention into relationships. The more we take operational burdens off, the more attention the LLO can spend on the work that determines whether a family opens the door.

This is why the L is load-bearing in LLO. Dimagi cannot centralize what an imam or a community elder knows about trust in their own community. We can't code it into a Learn module. The platform's value is that it lets locally-led organizations do the work they already know how to do, at larger scale, with tighter verification, and with sustainable funding attached.

Learning 03 · What didn't work · CHC, Central African Republic

Both LLOs dropped out in CAR. We published it anyway.

The Central African Republic deployment is the clearest negative result we have. The model didn't port as cleanly as we thought. It's in the marketing site because platforms that don't publish what failed don't get believed on the rest.

Connect's rapid partner activation had worked in Nigeria, Kenya, Uganda, Tanzania. The supported assumption — backed by the early evidence — was that the same approach would port to any context where LLOs exist and phones reach the field. Trial Run plus remote coordination. Light footprint. Low overhead. The model that had been running cleanly for a year.

Then CAR. Both contracted LLOs dropped out during setup1, due to staffing turnover and connectivity issues. Remote coordination, which had worked elsewhere, couldn't hold things together. The campaign didn't deliver meaningful volume. The partners we'd identified — vetted by the same approach that produced our successful 17 elsewhere — couldn't execute under the conditions on the ground.

Remote-only activation is no longer the default for fragile contexts. The Trial Run assumes basics — reliable connectivity, stable staffing — and CAR didn't have them.

What we changed: we are not repeating the same approach in similarly fragile geographies without dedicated in-country presence. The Trial Run model assumes the partner can execute basic operations — reliable connectivity, stable staffing, remote check-ins. Where any of those assumptions break, the model needs a different shape. Remote-only activation is no longer the default for fragile contexts; it's a deliberate choice we make against country-specific evidence.

The honest framing is that "in-country presence" is a coarse rule learned from one failure. We don't yet have a sharper predictive model for which fragile-context features make remote coordination fail and which don't. Lightweight in-country presence may be sufficient. Deeper structural changes may be required. The CAR experience tells us where the model breaks; figuring out what's enough to fix it is open work for the next round of fragile-context activations.
