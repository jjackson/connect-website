Kangaroo Mother Care Uganda · Kenya · India · Nigeria

Closing the post-discharge gap for the smallest newborns.

Hospitals provide essential care in the first days of life. Then families go home. For small and vulnerable newborns the weeks after discharge are the highest-mortality window and the least-supported point in the continuum of care.

Connect KMC reaches those families where they are — with a portable scale, a thermometer, a pulse oximeter, and a measuring tape, every visit.

The challenge

A 40% mortality reduction the world doesn't reach.

Kangaroo Mother Care is one of the most evidence-backed neonatal interventions in the WHO catalogue. The 2022 WHO guideline recommends KMC for all newborns ≤2000g — preterm or low birth weight — and notes mortality reductions up to 40% when the protocol is sustained through the highest-risk weeks. The science is settled. The delivery is not.

Globally, KMC coverage remains below 5% for eligible infants. Hospitals discharge families within days of birth, often before the kangaroo position has been practiced consistently. Structured at-home follow-up rarely exists. The 28 days after birth — the highest-mortality window in the continuum of care — are also the moment when families have the least clinical support. Connect KMC closes that gap with 4–5 structured home visits in the first 60 days, equipment in every visit, and danger-sign screening that auto-triggers referrals.

Learning · 01 · Design philosophy in practice

The first-visit-within-3-days target is at 50% against 70%. We publish the gap.

Connect's architectural distinction between payment criteria and guardrails — the decision that some quality targets must block payment and others must not — is most visible in KMC, where the gap between the two is currently a 20-percentage-point one. The four mandatory home visits per SVN are a payment criterion. They are hard. The visit must occur and meet payment-criterion checks (identity verified, GPS on-site, minimum duration, expected interaction logged) or it is not paid. There is no soft handling.

The "first visit within 3 days of discharge" is not a payment criterion. It is a guardrail. It is audited, not enforced. Current performance: 50% compliance against a 70% target. We're working on it through LLO coaching, scheduling support, and discharge-notification integration. Not through punishment.

The reason matters more than the number. If we tied payment to the 3-day window, FLWs would falsify visit dates. The platform would show 100% compliance, and we would have no idea what was actually happening. The gap between belief and behavior would close on paper while widening in practice. The whole point of separating payment criteria from guardrails is to keep the measurement honest enough that the program manager can act on it.

A platform that doesn't punish missed guardrails gets the real data it needs to improve the program.

The 50% number is uncomfortable on a marketing page. It is on the marketing page precisely because the alternative — a fake 100% — would be less useful to every stakeholder, including the funder. The work to close the gap is real, and so is the gap.

Learning · 02 · The clinical stack

Every visit, the FLW arrives with the same four instruments.

The KMC home visit is not a check-in. It is a clinical encounter, and the equipment list is the easiest way to make that visible. Every Connect KMC FLW arrives with a weighing scale, thermometer, pulse oximeter, and measuring tape. The CommCare app captures the readings, with structured fields for weight, axillary temperature, respiratory rate, oxygen saturation, head circumference, and feeding observation.

Danger signs — poor weight gain, hypothermia, hyperthermia, signs of infection, jaundice, feeding difficulty — auto-trigger referrals. The app prompts follow-up at the next visit and flags the case for LLO supervisor review. The structure mirrors the WHO 2022 KMC guideline because the protocol is what makes the intervention work; we did not redesign it, we operationalized it.

What's different about delivering this through Connect: the visits are verified the same way every other Connect visit is. GPS, biometric identity, photo capture at delivery, algorithmic anomaly detection on submission patterns. Funders see verified KMC visit counts. LLOs get paid on those counts. FLWs see their cases and earnings in real time.

Across 4,005 KMC cases (March 2026), the average age at first contact is 12.8 days — under our 28-day target. The 3.4 average visits per case sits below the 4-visit guardrail; the gap there is real and being addressed through LLO coaching and case-management improvements.

Learning · 03 · The integration we don't yet have

Closing the 3-day gap depends on an integration the platform doesn't yet own.

The 50% performance against the 70% target on first-visit-within-3-days has a structural cause. Hospitals discharge eligible newborns. The LLO needs to know within hours, not days, that a discharge has happened. In current operations, the discharge notification flow is partial — some hospital partners share data daily, some weekly, and the lag between actual discharge and FLW knowledge is the dominant driver of the missed window.

Pilot integrations with hospital information systems are underway. Where they work, the lag drops to hours and the within-3-days rate improves. Where the integration isn't yet in place, the rate sits at the current 50% — which is close to what we'd predict from the discharge-notification cadence in those facilities. The gap is not, in most cases, a worker-effort gap. It is a system-design gap that requires partner alignment beyond the LLO.

This is the kind of finding that benefits from being published early, not late. The mechanism is named, the cause is identifiable, and the work to close it is underway in a way that funders and partners can audit.

The gap isn't FLW effort. It's that hospital discharge notifications aren't yet integrated.

What we don't know yet.

  1. Whether discharge-notification integration alone closes the 50% → 70% within-3-days enrollment gap, or the 3-day target itself needs to be redesigned for the realities of post-discharge timing.
  2. Equipment availability across the 6 KMC LLO partners has been uneven. We don't yet have a published audit of equipment uptime.
  3. Whether KMC FLWs will sustain ≥4 visits per case at the 5,000+ scale; current average is 3.4 against a 5-visit guardrail.
