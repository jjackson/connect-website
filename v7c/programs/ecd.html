The challenge

Evidence-based parenting support, at a price per child most models can't reach.

The first 1,000 days of life are the most consequential window for brain development. Responsive caregiving, talk-and-play, age-appropriate stimulation — these are cheap interventions with large long-term effects. The problem isn't the science. The problem is access: most caregivers in low-resource settings don't have a consistent source of evidence-based parenting guidance, and most parent-support programs struggle to deliver it at a cost per child low enough to scale.

Connect ECD runs an FLW-delivered home-visit parenting intervention. Pregnant women and caregivers of infants up to 5 months are enrolled. FLWs conduct structured visits covering responsive caregiving, age-appropriate developmental activities, and caregiver-child interaction assessment. The pilot validated the approach with ~400 FLWs and produced a result we didn't expect — one that reshaped what the intervention actually targets.

Question 01 · The targeting question

What if caregivers already hold the right beliefs — and the program has been targeting the wrong gap?

Standard ECD interventions target three domains: caregiver attitudes, caregiver knowledge, and caregiver behavior. The design assumption — almost never challenged — is that attitude is the precondition for knowledge, and knowledge is the precondition for behavior. Build belief first. Then build skill. The Malawi pilot tested that assumption and found something different.

Between April and July 2025, 76 FLWs in Malawi conducted 9,510+ visits. Baseline and endline surveys covered 50 caregivers across three domains. At baseline, nearly all caregivers already held positive attitudes: they agreed that talk-and-play supports brain development, endorsed responsive caregiving, rejected harsh correction. At endline, attitude scores were equally high. The intervention didn't move attitudes — because there was nothing to move. They were already at ceiling.

Where the intervention did move the needle:

  • Knowledge — timing of rapid child development: +33%
  • Knowledge — ways to support mental development: +41%
  • Observed behavior — teaching: +21%
  • Observed behavior — encouragement of autonomy: +21%

The finding reshaped the intervention. We're expanding the pilot from 3 visits to 8-10 visits across 12 months — not because caregivers need more time to be convinced that ECD matters, but because behavior change takes sustained practice, repetition, and feedback. The content weight shifted away from attitude modules toward knowledge scaffolding and behavioral rehearsal. The program is now evidence-guided, not just evidence-based.

The hardest subdomain is encouragement of autonomy, which sits at 47% at endline — the lowest of the four observed behaviors even after a statistically significant gain. It's the open work.

What's still unresolved: The 50-caregiver sample is a pilot-scale N. The hypothesis that ECD interventions should reallocate away from attitude-building toward sustained behavioral practice needs validation at larger scale — which is part of what the expansion is designed to produce.

Sources: ECD Overview (Feb 2026), Malawi pilot baseline/endline surveys (April-July 2025). Insight 7 in the public insights log.

Question 02 · Training competence

If a knowledge test doesn't predict field competence, what does the training layer actually need to do?

The first version of the Learn-app training for ECD was conventional: digital course, multiple-choice assessment, you pass, you deliver. Workers who aced the test still struggled in the field. Handling a skeptical caregiver. Recognizing subtle developmental concerns. Practicing motivational interviewing instead of reading a script. The multiple-choice format was selecting for people who could memorize — and the visit required something else entirely.

Rather than make the test harder, we added layers around it. The current training stack for ECD FLWs:

  1. Self-paced digital learning in the Learn app, offline-capable
  2. In-app knowledge test — must pass to progress
  3. Daily interaction with an AI coach bot built on Dimagi's Open Chat Studio
  4. Practice with peers through the Connect network
  5. One supervised observation visit rated by an LLO supervisor

The AI coach addresses specifically what the test doesn't. It offers scenario-based practice: "a caregiver tells you they're already doing talk-and-play — how do you respond?" It runs motivational interviewing drills: open-ended questioning, affirmations, reflective listening, summarizing. It prompts daily reflection on real visit experiences. And it identifies knowledge gaps through conversation, which often surfaces things multiple-choice questions don't reach.

The stack has been validated with ~400 FLWs across iterative experiments. 85% progress from training to service delivery. 88% score above 70% on their first observed visits. 77% pass the supervised observation. These are not optimized lab numbers — they're from actual ECD rollout across Malawi and Mozambique. The layered stack is what scales without sacrificing field competence.

Knowledge is testable cheaply. Competence requires practice, feedback, and a human observer. The training design has to respect the difference.

What's still unresolved: The 23% who don't pass the supervised observation is real attrition. Whether that's a selection signal (the right people are being filtered out before delivery) or a training-design gap (the stack is still missing something) is an open question the next cohort of data should help answer.

Sources: ECD Overview (Feb 2026). Insight 8 in the public insights log.

Question 03 · Cost per child

How does an 8-session parenting intervention cost $7 per child?

Parent-support programs at this level of intensity — eight structured visits, practical coaching, child assessments — typically cost an order of magnitude more than this. The Connect ECD cost model lands at $7 per child in Nigeria and $18 per child in Mozambique for the full 8-session intervention. The delta from conventional programs comes from three places, and understanding them is worth a paragraph each.

Labor is compensated at fair country standards, not international consulting rates. The cost model assumes 1.25 hours per visit and pays FLWs at the going rate for community health work in their country. Those hours are paid, not volunteered — the FLW is an employed local worker doing skilled work, with sustainable income attached. The difference from international consulting rates is what funds the scale.

LLO overhead is a 40% top-up, not a duplicate organization. LLOs take a 40% top-up over direct visit costs to cover supervision, transport, and local operations. That's structured as a markup on delivered work rather than a fixed-cost infrastructure layer. When delivery slows, overhead slows. The conventional alternative — building parallel oversight structures that persist regardless of delivery volume — is what drives cost-per-child into the hundreds of dollars range in many comparable programs.

Platform cost is a 20% top-up, once. Dimagi takes a 20% platform fee. Training is digital and offline-capable, so adding the 10,000th FLW to the training system costs almost nothing. Verification runs on the same stack CHC and KMC use — there's no per-program build-out of an oversight layer. Integration with mobile money is already built. The marginal cost of running ECD on Connect is much lower than building an ECD-specific stack would be.

The resulting numbers give funders a cost-per-child that's comparable to commodity distribution programs, for an intervention class that has historically been harder to scale economically. And the same architecture that gave CHC its 22% cost reduction over 14 months applies here: as the LLO network and training content mature, the per-visit costs trend downward.

Parenting support programs don't usually scale cheaply because the conventional delivery architecture carries fixed organizational costs. Connect ECD ports the CHC architecture into a new domain — and the cost structure comes with it.
