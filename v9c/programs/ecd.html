When the pilot data changed the program.

250 million children in low- and middle-income countries are at risk of not achieving their developmental potential. 90% of brain development happens by age 5. Parent-focused interventions work. The Connect ECD pilot measured what worked — and what didn't — and reshaped the program around the evidence.

I.
The challenge

Caregivers have the attitude. The gap is knowledge and behaviour.

Evidence shows nurturing care is critical in the first 1,000 days of life. Parent-focused interventions have proven positive effects. But in under-resourced settings, caregivers have limited access to evidence-based parenting guidance. When the Malawi pilot opened, the assumption was we'd need to build attitudes. The baseline data showed we didn't.

II.
Chapter one · Malawi pilot · 50 caregiver surveys

Caregiver attitudes were already correct. The gap was knowledge.

An ECD intervention, by textbook design, should build positive attitudes about responsive caregiving, talk-and-play, developmental support. That was our framing. The baseline data told us otherwise.

Nearly all 50 caregivers surveyed at baseline already held positive attitudes. Attitudes scored high before and after. They did not move — because they were already there.

"We reallocated the intervention. Not because caregivers need more convincing — they know — but because behaviour change takes sustained practice."

Where we moved the needle: +33% on rapid child development timing, +41% on ways to support mental development, +21% observed teaching behaviour, +21% observed autonomy encouragement.

Where we didn't: autonomy still sits at 47% at endline. Language/thinking: 35% correct at endline, up from 26%. Work to do.

What we changed

The 3-visit pilot is expanding to 8–10 visits over 12 months. Program is now evidence-guided, not just evidence-based.

III.
Chapter two · ~400 FLWs validated

Knowledge tests alone don't produce competent FLWs. We layered the model.

Digital training, in-app knowledge test, pass to progress — that was the early design. Workers who aced the test still struggled with in-person counselling nuance. Knowledge ≠ competence. We redesigned.

Now FLWs complete: (1) self-paced digital learning in Learn; (2) an in-app knowledge test, must pass; (3) daily AI coach interaction on Open Chat Studio; (4) peer practice; (5) one supervised observation visit rated by an LLO supervisor.

"The AI coach adds scenario practice, motivational-interviewing drills, daily reflection — things no test can simulate."

IV.
Chapter three · Country-specific cost model

An 8-session ECD intervention costs $7–$18 per child, depending on context.

Cost assumptions: fair hourly rates at country standards (1.25 hrs per visit), 40% LLO oversight top-up, 20% Dimagi platform top-up. The spread reflects local wage rates and logistics — not platform overhead.

This is cost per caregiver served, not per activity planned. It assumes full delivery across 8 sessions. Because payment is performance-based, this is also what funders actually pay when they fund this program.

V.
Chapter four · Malawi · 9,510 visits

76 FLWs delivered 9,510+ visits during the Malawi pilot. Quality held at scale.

Baseline/endline surveys with 50 caregivers measured three domains: attitude, knowledge, observed behaviour. Surveyors watched caregivers interact with their children and scored responsiveness, affection, teaching, and encouragement of autonomy. Behavioural gains were documented in the field, not self-reported.

The next phase reaches 250,000 caregivers over 24 months with 1,000 FLWs, at 8–10 visits per caregiver — a substantial scale-up driven by what the pilot data showed.
