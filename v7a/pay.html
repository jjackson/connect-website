How It Works/Pay

Paid for what was delivered. Verified first.

Connect's pay-for-outcomes model aligns the chain — worker, LLO, funder — around a single thing: a verified delivery. Workers are paid per verified service. LLOs earn only when their workers deliver. Funders bear no financial risk for work that was not actually done. This page is about two findings that make that arithmetic unusually well-behaved.

01

Most organizations get less lean as they scale. Connect got leaner.

There is a pattern in development work so consistent that it is usually treated as a natural law. As programs grow — more countries, more partners, more visits — the operational cost per unit of delivery grows with them. More coordination. More overhead. More compliance. Every other Dimagi program has followed this curve. It is part of what makes scale hard: the bigger you get, the more you pay to stay that size.

Connect did the opposite. Over the Founders Pledge grant period, we scaled to a rate capable of supporting millions of child visits per year without substantial growth in Dimagi's team. These are fully-loaded unit economics — commodity cost, FLW payment, LLO payment, and Dimagi's 20% margin included. Program-weighted average cost per verified visit dropped from $2.20 to $1.70 — a 22% reduction during growth. Nigeria CHC with ORS/Zinc co-packs: $1.30 per visit across 14 LLO contracts. Outside Nigeria (VAS + deworming, no ORS): $0.78 per visit across 17 contracts. One Sierra Leone LLO delivered ORS/Zinc at $1.00 per visit — near the floor of what this work can cost. Different commodity bundles, same operating architecture.

The cost inversion is not a consequence of cutting corners. It is a structural property of three architectural decisions, and it is worth saying which ones. First, LLOs operate with inherently low overhead because they are local and lean. They do not carry the org chart of an international NGO. Second, performance-based payment ensures costs track actual delivery rather than organizational size. No delivery, no cost. This is the opposite of headcount-based funding, where the expenses are fixed and the output is variable. Third, digital technology is integrated into every step of the worker journey — training, guidance, verification, payment. The marginal cost of the 10,000th FLW is close to zero, and the efficiencies compound as the network grows.

The setup fees tell the same story from a different angle. During the grant, setup costs averaged about $0.40 per verified visit. At the scale we are building toward, we expect that to fall to ~$0.10 per visit, because the setup is substantially the same across LLOs and amortizes across more delivery. The curve bends down, not up.

What this implies for funders is counterintuitive. Most development organizations have a structural ceiling on cost-effectiveness: the more they scale, the more operations cost, the more each marginal dollar of impact becomes expensive to produce. Connect's architecture is one of the few examples where scale creates cost reduction instead of cost creep. The implication is that a funder considering committing additional capital should expect their next dollar to buy more, not less, verified impact than the first one did.

The honest caveat is that this is early-stage evidence. One grant period, one set of programs, one platform. We do not claim the cost curve continues bending forever — at some point we will hit structural floors, particularly around LLO activation overhead and commodity costs, which are not ours to optimize. But the direction, over the period we can measure, is clear, and it is the right direction.

02

Campaign work does not displace routine health services. We assumed it might. It doesn't.

There is a long-standing concern in global health funding circles, and it is not a silly one. If you pay Community Health Volunteers to do campaign work — Vitamin A, deworming, vaccine promotion — you worry that they will neglect the routine volunteer health duties that were the reason they existed in the first place. The campaign "crowds out" the baseline. The marginal dollar creates a hidden loss. This is a standard funder concern and we took it seriously.

We took it seriously enough to commission a study. In partnership with GiveWell, we conducted qualitative interviews (n=20 FLWs, n=2 LLO staff, n=2 local health representatives) across Gombe and Sokoto, Nigeria. The sample is small — deliberately small, as the first read — and we are not hiding it. The interviews found zero reported displacement of routine community health work. These findings will be validated by a $1.5M RCT with IPA as evaluation partner in 2026, funded by PRO Impact. We asked directly about displacement. Were workers reducing their routine volunteer hours? Were services being missed? Was campaign work consuming time that should have gone to the normal work of a CHV?

The answer was no, and the reason is that the premise was subtly wrong. Campaign work is normal and expected in Nigeria's community health volunteer system. CHVs routinely do campaigns — immunization drives, deworming cycles, nutrition programs. It is their primary source of income, not a distraction from a baseline. When our FLWs reduced volunteer hours during an active campaign, other volunteers at the facility covered the work, as they typically do during any campaign, because the health system itself already runs on this rotation logic.

In Gombe, specifically, FLWs told us they maintained their volunteer hours by shifting their schedules within Connect's flexible 7am–7pm window. The campaign work filled gaps in the day they would otherwise have spent differently. It did not displace routine volunteer shifts; it rearranged them. This was not what the platform told them to do. It was what made sense given the shape of their week.

What this exposed is that the "displacement" concern was built on a Western assumption about what a CHV does. The assumption is that community health volunteering has a fixed daily shape — a routine rhythm of visits — and that campaigns disrupt the rhythm. In the actual context, that is not how the work is organized. Campaigns are not a disruption of the system. They are the system.

What the interviews surfaced in the other direction is worth naming. FLWs reported substantial income increases — for many, CCC-CHC became a primary source of income. They gained technology skills: comfort with the CommCare app, Google Maps, basic smartphone navigation. They reported increased confidence, improved communication, better standing with the communities they served. None of this was a design goal. It was what happened when a platform paid for verified work instead of for time spent.

We are treating this as evidence, not proof. Routine-work displacement might behave differently in other countries, in other health system structures, or in FLW populations where campaign rotation is less normalized. The PRO Impact-funded $1.5M RCT with IPA beginning 2026 is designed specifically to produce the formal, quantitative answer — powered against the qualitative signal we already have.
