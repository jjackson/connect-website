Step 02 of 04 The delivery layer

Delivery is locally-led. The platform composes around it.

Locally-Led Organizations — not Dimagi — run operations on the ground. Connect provides the rails. LLOs provide the relationships.

The platform supplies app, verification, and payment infrastructure. LLOs supply community knowledge, supervision, and the relationships that decide whether a family opens the door. The shape of that partnership was the single biggest thing we had to learn how to build.

Learning · 01 · Pre-vetting doesn't work

LLO performance can't be predicted. Only proven, in small contracts.

The procurement-style approach to partner selection failed us. We replaced it with the Trial Run — a mechanism for de-risking activation that has now been reused across sectors without modification.

Conventional development contracting assumes you can vet your way to good partner selection. Track record, team size, past-funder references, organizational maturity. You read the dossier, run the references, score the proposal, sign a contract sized to the campaign. This is how the sector has worked for decades. It is, in principle, a sensible approach.

Pre-2024 · Standard procurementVet first, contract big.

We started inside that frame. We reviewed track records and assessed team capacity, then signed contracts sized for full campaign delivery. Then we watched what happened.

2024 · The prediction failureReputation didn't predict performance.

Pre-selection turned out to be a poor predictor of field performance. Organizations with strong track records in other programs stumbled on Connect visits — different operational tempo, different verification load, different incentive shape. Organizations that looked marginal on paper delivered at scale, because what mattered in the field wasn't what their dossier had measured. Team size, organizational maturity, past-funder references — none of it reliably forecasted what actually happened in the field.

The signal we wanted — "will this LLO deliver verified visits efficiently?" — wasn't encoded in the signals we were checking.

Pre-selection as predictive signal: unreliable

Mid-2024 · The Trial Run model$3,000 setup. 3,000 visits. Performance-based.

New LLOs receive a small initial contract: $3,000 setup fee for 3,000 verified child visits. Payment is tied to actual delivery. If they execute, they roll into a larger campaign. If they don't, the financial exposure is capped and the next partner gets a turn. We bear minimal risk; they prove it in the field.

2025–2026 · What it produced37 contracted. 24 ran trials. 17 scaled.

Under the Founders Pledge grant: 37 LLOs contracted. 24 entered Trial Runs; 10 underperformed and weren't continued. 3 went straight to larger campaigns based on prior measured performance from inside the network. 17 completed full campaigns following successful Trial Runs. The Trial Run has now been reused — without modification — for KMC, Readers, and chlorine dispensers. It is sector-agnostic because it doesn't rely on any domain-specific features of CHC. It relies on the idea that small, performance-based contracts are the cheapest form of evidence about an organization's operational fit.

Trial Run conversion: 65% · Reused across 4 programs without modification

Learning · 02 · Why the "L" in LLO matters

LLOs navigate vaccine hesitancy with religious leaders. We didn't teach them this.

The most important intervention in a vaccine campaign wasn't in our app. It was what the LLO did with the platform once they were operating in their own community — and we learned we couldn't centralize that knowledge.

The first time we sat down to design vaccine-hesitancy training for the Learn app, we built it like a textbook. Common myths and the medical responses that counter them. Counseling scripts for the most predictable refusals. A set of motivational-interviewing drills. Read the module, pass the test, you're equipped. That was the plan, and at the time it felt like the obvious shape for the work.

Early 2025 · How we framed the training"Cover vaccine myths in the digital content."

We rolled this content out across the seven LLOs running the CLP Stage 2 vaccine campaign in CHC. The plan was: equip the FLW, observe the encounter, iterate on the content. Then we started looking at what was actually happening in the field.

CLP Stage 2 · What actually happened5 of 7 LLOs mobilized leaders on their own.

Across seven LLOs, five or six independently leveraged religious leaders, community leaders, or existing community relationships as part of their outreach strategy — before we asked, before the app prompted it. One FLW countered hesitancy with a personal example: "We were immunized, we have children." Another LLO had FLWs who were recommended by the community itself, lending credibility before any door was knocked on.

The platform didn't teach this. The LLOs already knew it.

The pattern was specific and consistent enough that it stopped looking like a coincidence. These organizations operate inside the same communities they are visiting. They know which imam matters in which neighborhood. They know which traditional birth attendant the mothers actually trust. They know which compound elder will quietly tell families a campaign is fine, and which one would walk in front of the team to keep them out. None of this is in our app. None of this could be in our app.

LLOs independently mobilizing community leaders: 5–6 of 7

Late 2025 · What we changedStop trying to codify local knowledge.

We stopped writing more content about hesitancy. We started designing the platform to get out of the way. The Deliver layer now assumes the LLO knows how to talk to their community better than we do. Our job is to make the mechanics — scheduling, visit flow, verification, payment — frictionless enough that the LLO can pour their attention into relationships.

This is why the L is load-bearing in LLO. Dimagi can't centralize what an imam knows about trust in his community. The platform's job is to enable it, not replace it. The bigger reframe for the funder pitch: the platform's value isn't that it trains FLWs to counter hesitancy with better facts. The platform's value is that it lets locally-led organizations do the work they already know how to do, at larger scale, with tighter verification, and with sustainable funding attached.

Source: CLP Stage 2 Report (Feb 2026). Insight 6 in the public insights log.

Learning · 03 · What didn't work

Both LLOs dropped out in CAR. We published it anyway.

The Central African Republic deployment is the clearest negative result we have. The model didn't port as cleanly as we thought. It's in this writeup because platforms that don't publish what failed don't get believed on the rest.

Connect's rapid partner activation had worked across Nigeria, Kenya, Uganda, Tanzania. The assumption — supported by the early evidence — was that the same approach would port to any context where LLOs exist and phones reach the field. The Trial Run model presumes basic operating conditions: connectivity that holds long enough to sync visits, staffing stable enough to absorb a six-week ramp-up, and a remote-coordination cadence that can substitute for in-country presence.

2024 · The assumption"The model is portable."

We contracted two LLOs in the Central African Republic for a CHC vaccine deployment. We expected the standard activation arc: setup, training, Trial Run delivery, scale-up. We were wrong by week six.

2024–2025 · What happened in CARBoth contracted LLOs dropped out.

Both LLOs contracted for the CAR deployment exited during setup, citing staffing turnover and connectivity issues. Remote coordination, which had worked elsewhere, couldn't hold things together. The campaign didn't deliver meaningful volume. We published the failure in the Founders Pledge final report because the alternative — a portfolio of only successes — would have been a different kind of dishonesty.

LLOs completing campaigns in CAR: 0

2026 · What we changedFragile contexts likely need in-country presence.

We're not repeating the same approach in similarly fragile geographies without dedicated in-country presence. The Trial Run model assumes the partner can execute basic operations — reliable connectivity, stable staffing, remote check-ins. Where any of those assumptions break, the model needs a different shape. This now shows up in how we evaluate new country invitations: remote-only activation is no longer the default for fragile contexts. Whether "lightweight in-country presence" is sufficient, or whether deeper structural changes are required, is open work.

Source: Founders Pledge Final Report (Feb 2026). Insight 9 in the public insights log.

What we don't know yet.

  1. Whether the 65% Trial Run conversion holds outside CHC — KMC, ECD, and Readers are still on too small a contract base to publish their own.
  2. "Fragile context requires in-country presence" is a coarse rule learned from CAR. We don't have a sharper predictive model yet for which fragile-context features make remote coordination fail.
  3. We have not built a structured framework for capturing what individual LLOs invent locally — the real intervention often lives in their heads, not in our app.
