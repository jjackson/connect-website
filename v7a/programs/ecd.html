A parenting intervention for the first 1,000 days.

250 million children in low- and middle-income countries are at risk of not achieving their developmental potential. Connect ECD is a door-to-door parenting intervention designed to close that gap — and the Malawi pilot taught us where the actual gap was, which was not where we expected.

The challenge.

The evidence on early childhood development is clear. 90% of brain development happens by age 5. Nurturing care — responsive caregiving, talk-and-play, age-appropriate developmental activities — is one of the highest-leverage inputs there is. In low- and middle-income countries, parent-focused interventions have proven positive effects on child outcomes.

The access gap is the problem. Most caregivers have limited access to evidence-based parenting guidance. The formal health system is structured around clinical care, not around a monthly visit where someone sits with a parent and helps them practice a developmentally appropriate interaction. Connect ECD is built to be that visit, delivered at scale, by trained Frontline Workers using the same platform that powers the Child Health Campaign and KMC.

The program is aimed at pregnant women and caregivers of infants up to 5 months. 1,000 FLWs planned. 250,000 caregivers targeted in 24 months. The Malawi pilot, which ran from April to July 2025, was the first serious read on whether the platform could deliver a parenting intervention at all.

Program mechanics

What a Connect ECD visit actually covers.

Structured home visits

Current pilot: 3 visits per caregiver. Expansion plan: 8–10 visits across 12 months. Caregiver registration, ECD counseling, practical support on responsive caregiving.

Developmental activities

Age-appropriate play and interaction activities. Guidance on talk-and-play for brain development. Real-time assessment of caregiver-child interactions.

Five-gate FLW training

Self-paced digital learning → in-app knowledge test → AI coach daily interaction → peer practice → supervised observation visit. 77% pass rate on the observation step.

AI coach bot

On Dimagi's Open Chat Studio. FLWs interact daily (paid). Scenario-based practice, motivational-interviewing drills, tailored feedback from real visit experiences.

We came in to change caregiver attitudes about ECD. What happens when the attitudes are already correct?

We built the first version of Connect ECD around a standard assumption in the parenting-intervention literature. Caregivers in low-resource settings, the assumption goes, have not been exposed to the evidence on responsive caregiving, talk-and-play, and early brain development. The intervention exists to build positive attitudes — to convince parents that these things matter. That is how parenting interventions in similar contexts have historically framed the problem.

The baseline survey from the Malawi pilot, with 50 caregivers, took apart that assumption. We asked whether caregivers agreed that talk-and-play supports brain development. Nearly all of them did. We asked whether they endorsed responsive caregiving. Nearly all of them did. We asked about appropriate child correction, about the importance of early stimulation. Attitudes scored high at baseline and stayed high at endline, because they had not been the thing that needed changing.

So where was the actual gap? It was in knowledge and behavior. Caregivers knew talk-and-play was important, but they did not know the specific timing of rapid brain development — the windows in the first 1,000 days when certain interactions matter most. They did not know the concrete, day-to-day ways to support mental development. And when we watched them at home, the behaviors that would flow from that knowledge — teaching, encouragement of autonomy — were weaker than the attitude scores implied.

We re-pointed the intervention. Less attitude content, more knowledge content, more behavior practice. The endline results, measured after the pilot's three-visit structure: +33% on timing of rapid child development. +41% on ways to support mental development. +21% on observed teaching behavior. +21% on observed encouragement of autonomy. Attitude scores stayed where they were, which was fine, because they had been fine to begin with.

What this means for the program going forward is that we have stopped trying to move the needle on attitudes in Malawi. The 3-visit pilot is expanding to 8–10 visits over 12 months — not because we need more time to persuade caregivers that ECD matters, but because behavior change takes sustained practice. The new session count is the data telling us where the work actually is.

What is still unresolved is a specific sub-finding that bothers us. Encouragement of autonomy — letting a child make small choices, attempt tasks on their own, recover from small failures — is sitting at 47% of the behavioral observation score at endline. Up from baseline, but the lowest of the four subdomains. This is the hardest thing we are trying to change. It correlates with cultural parenting patterns that are not moved by a 3-visit pilot, and quite possibly not by an 8-visit one either. We are writing that uncertainty into the 2026 version of the curriculum rather than optimizing around it.

Source: ECD Overview, Feb 2026; Malawi ECD pilot (April–July 2025, 50 caregivers baseline/endline); Connect insight 7.

Can a digital-only training model produce a competent ECD worker?

Connect ECD was also the program where we most seriously tested whether a layered digital training model could produce field-ready Frontline Workers, because ECD is harder to teach than the procedural programs. A Vitamin A campaign requires a worker to know a dose, a child's age, and a protocol. An ECD visit requires motivational interviewing, developmental sensitivity, and the judgment to read when a caregiver is overwhelmed and when they are ready to practice a new behavior. Those skills do not live in a multiple-choice test.

Our first version of the training was the same multiple-choice model that works elsewhere on the platform, and it failed in the specific way that the Learn page describes. Workers passed the test. They struggled in the field. Knowledge did not equal competence.

So we built the layered model for ECD and validated it here. A Connect ECD worker now moves through five gates: self-paced digital learning in the Learn app, an in-app knowledge test (must pass to progress), daily interaction with an AI coach bot on Dimagi's Open Chat Studio, practice with peers, and one supervised observation visit rated by an LLO supervisor before any paid delivery is allowed.

The AI coach is the part of the stack that did the heaviest lifting for ECD specifically. Unlike a multiple-choice test, the coach practices scenario-based drills, motivational-interviewing technique, and daily reflection on real visit experiences. An FLW who has just come home from a visit can describe what happened in plain language, and the coach will identify the gap in their thinking — a leading question they asked, a danger sign they did not notice, a moment where they could have used reflective listening instead of a direct instruction.

The numbers we have, validated through iterative experiments across roughly 400 FLWs: 85% progress from digital training to service delivery. 88% score above 70% on their first observed visits. 77% pass the supervised observation. These are the live pass rates, not the targets. They are also, importantly, not 100% — which is the point of the model. Workers who cannot do this job are filtered out before a funder pays for work that would not stand up.

What we have not resolved is whether we can make the live supervised observation less expensive. It is the costliest component of the five-gate model because it requires a trained supervisor to travel to the visit, watch, and rate. We are piloting video-submitted visits rated asynchronously, but we are not there yet. The next version of this page will have that result.

Source: ECD Overview, Feb 2026; ECD Learn-app validation with ~400 FLWs across iterative experiments; Connect insight 8.
