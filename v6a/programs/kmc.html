The Lede

Every year, 2.3 million newborns die in the first 28 days of life.

That figure accounts for nearly half of all deaths in children under five. Most of those babies were born too small or too early — 80 percent with low birth weight, two-thirds preterm. They are what the clinical literature calls small and vulnerable newborns, or SVNs. Their first month of life is fragile in a particular way: the period right after hospital discharge, when a family returns home with a baby who still needs careful monitoring and gets, in most of the world, almost none.

One of the most proven ways to protect these babies is Kangaroo Mother Care — continuous skin-to-skin contact, exclusive breastfeeding, and close follow-up. The evidence is unambiguous: KMC reduces neonatal mortality by up to 40 percent. Lower infection rates. Higher breastfeeding rates. The World Health Organization recommends it for all newborns weighing 2,000 grams or less at birth. It is, by any reasonable measure, among the highest-impact interventions in global health.

"KMC coverage remains below 5% for eligible infants globally."

Connect KMC Blog · 2026

Yet KMC coverage remains below 5 percent for eligible infants globally. Hospitals discharge families within days of birth. Structured follow-up at home rarely exists. For parents of SVNs, the transition from hospital to home is, in many places, a cliff: no postnatal visits, no ongoing guidance, no one to call when the baby isn't gaining weight. Connect KMC is built to close that specific gap.

The Model

Five pillars of post-discharge care.

Connect KMC operates through the same LDVP architecture that runs every Connect program. Frontline Workers — drawn from the communities where babies live — complete mandatory digital training, pass an in-app assessment, and are then cleared to deliver. Each worker carries a scale, thermometer, pulse oximeter, and measuring tape. The app guides each home visit step by step, flags danger signs automatically, and triggers referrals when a baby can't be cared for at home.

Locally-Led Organizations — including Preterm Infants Parents Network Uganda (PIPNU), Nama Wellness, Global Health Innovations, Ansh, BERI, and eHealth Africa — partner with health facilities to identify SVNs before discharge. Workers meet caregivers at the hospital, explain KMC, and invite them to enroll.

01

Identifying Small and Vulnerable Newborns

LLOs partner with health facilities. FLWs meet caregivers before discharge, explain KMC, invite them to enroll.

02

Home Visits for KMC Support

FLWs conduct follow-up visits at home — no travel for caregivers. These home visits are the foundation of Connect KMC.

03

Counseling on Skin-to-Skin & Breastfeeding

FLWs help caregivers practice sustained skin-to-skin contact using a provided KMC wrap, counsel on exclusive breastfeeding, troubleshoot feeding, and guide hygiene.

04

Screening for Danger Signs & Referrals

Workers carry a scale, thermometer, pulse oximeter, and measuring tape. Danger signs flagged in the app trigger referrals; the app auto-prompts follow-up at the next visit.

05

Emotional & Social Support

Many SVN mothers experience stigma and isolation. FLWs offer encouragement and practical advice — building confidence and trust.

Early performance

What the program is learning in the field.

As of March 2026, Connect KMC has enrolled more than 4,000 SVN cases across Uganda, Kenya, India, and Nigeria. The honest data tells a useful story — some targets being met, others missed by clear margins. The program is running, not finished.

Average age at enrollment sits at 12.8 days — well under the 28-day target. But only 50 percent of enrollments happen within three days of discharge, below the 70 percent goal. FLWs average 3.4 visits per case — below the 5-visit guardrail. And the early-discharge rate sits at 7.6 percent, slightly above the 5 percent target. These are design problems the program is working on, openly, with its funder.

The Bigger Picture

Aligned with global newborn policy.

Connect KMC sits within a clear global policy frame: the WHO Global Position Paper on KMC, the Every Newborn Action Plan, and Sustainable Development Goal 3.2 — the commitment to reduce neonatal death to fewer than 12 per 1,000 live births by 2030.

The program also demonstrates a structural point about how locally-led organizations can extend high-quality care into homes. Partners are drawn from the communities they serve. Frontline workers come from those same communities. Digital tools give them the job aid, the supervision, and the compensation infrastructure to deliver at quality — backed by a payment model that runs only when the visit is verified.
