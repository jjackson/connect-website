layer 01

Mobile-first, offline-capable

Workers access modules on their own phones, without connectivity. No classroom scheduling. No travel costs. The cost of adding the 10,000th FLW approaches zero.

Locally-relevant content

Training is localized per intervention and region. Content is approved by subject matter experts — the LLO, or an SME identified by Dimagi for that geography.

progression_of_thinking // layered_training

The knowledge test wasn't enough. We had to build around it.

Our first training version assumed that if a worker could pass an in-app test, they could deliver the visit. They couldn't. The gap between knowledge and competence turned out to be the hardest problem in the Learn stack — and the most interesting.

early 2024 · the starting assumption

"Pass the test, start the work."

We built a self-paced digital course inside the Learn app, followed by a multiple-choice knowledge assessment. If the FLW scored above threshold, they were cleared to deliver. That was the whole gate. Simple, scalable, measurable — and we trusted the test.

late 2024 · what the field showed

Competence doesn't equal knowledge

Workers who aced the test still struggled with the nuances of in-person counseling. Handling a skeptical caregiver. Recognizing subtle danger signs. Practicing motivational interviewing instead of reading a script. Supervised observations revealed a category of skill the multiple-choice format couldn't reach.

We were selecting for people who could memorize facts, not for people who could make a hesitant mother feel heard.

observed_gap=knowledge > behavior · cohort=early
2025 · what we changed

A five-layer training stack

Instead of strengthening the test, we added layers around it. Workers now complete self-paced digital learning, pass the in-app knowledge test, interact daily with an AI coach bot (built on Dimagi's Open Chat Studio), practice with peers, and finally complete one supervised observation visit rated by an LLO supervisor. Each layer addresses something the previous one missed.

The AI coach specifically fills the space between text-based knowledge and real encounters — scenario-based practice, motivational-interviewing drills, daily reflection on real visit experiences, and conversational identification of knowledge gaps.

layers_added=4 · gate=multi-layer
2026 · what the data says now

Layered training validated against ~400 FLWs

85% of FLWs progress from training to service delivery. 88% score above 70% on their first observed visits. 77% pass the supervised observation gate. This isn't an ideal test environment — it's ECD, CHC, and KMC rollouts across multiple LLOs and countries. The stack holds.

02
progression_of_thinking // ai_coach_pilot

An AI coach handled 97.8% of FLW queries — and we structured how we evaluate it.

Most "AI for development" claims are handle-rate numbers without an evaluation methodology. Our Kenya CHC pilot ran the chatbot through structured assessment for accuracy and usefulness — separate from how often a human had to step in.

pre-pilot · the question we had

"Can a chatbot replace WhatsApp escalation to a human supervisor?"

FLWs working alone in remote contexts rely on WhatsApp groups for clarification — but supervisors don't scale, response time slips, and context gets lost. We wanted to know if a coach trained on the program's own training content could absorb the routine load.

2025 · what we deployed

Open Chat Studio + WhatsApp, scoped to the program

The coach is built on Open Chat Studio, Dimagi's open chatbot platform. FLWs message it on WhatsApp, the same surface they already use. The bot is grounded in the program's own training content and protocol guides — not generic medical advice — and it logs every interaction for audit.

surface=whatsapp · backend=ocs · latency=5s avg
kenya pilot · 2025 · what we measured

500 of 511 queries resolved without human escalation

In our Kenya CHC pilot, the AI chatbot handled 500 of 511 FLW queries — 97.8% — without requiring escalation to a human supervisor, with a 5-second average response time. Independent evaluation: 88.7% accuracy, 81.5% usefulness across a structured rubric. The number that matters isn't the handle rate alone — it's the handle rate paired with an assessment that says the answers were correct and useful.

handle_rate=97.8% · accuracy=88.7% · usefulness=81.5%
what's next

Failure-mode analysis on the 11 escalations is open

One pilot, one language, one program. We have not yet published the failure-mode analysis on the 11 queries the bot didn't resolve, and we don't yet have evidence that the rate holds for non-English-language deployments. Open questions are listed below — we're not ahead of the data we have.

03
progression_of_thinking // ecd_floor_effect

The training had to move behavior — the attitudes were already there.

Most early-childhood programs lead with attitude change. Our Malawi ECD pilot found caregivers' attitudes were already correct at baseline. The Learn stack had to do something different: close the gap between belief and practice.

2024 · the assumption we walked in with

"Move attitudes, then behavior follows."

The standard ECD intervention design assumes caregivers need to be convinced that early stimulation matters. Train the FLWs to make the case. Track attitude shifts as the leading indicator. Behavior change follows — eventually.

malawi pilot · 2025 · the floor effect

Caregivers already agreed; the gap was practice

In our Malawi ECD pilot — 76 FLWs, 9,510+ visits, a 50-caregiver baseline/endline survey — nearly every caregiver already held positive ECD attitudes at baseline. Belief in the value of early stimulation, talking to infants, responsive caregiving. The intervention didn't need to convince them. It needed to close the gap between belief and routine practice.

baseline_attitudes=already_correct · n=50 caregivers
2025-2026 · what the layered training drove

Knowledge +33%, observed teaching behavior +21%

By endline: knowledge of rapid-development timing rose +33%; observed teaching behavior +21%; encouragement of autonomy +21% (still only 47% at endline). Knowledge moved further than behavior moved — exactly the pattern the layered Learn stack is designed to address. The observed teaching behavior number is the one that matters: caregivers were doing more of the work that early stimulation actually requires, not just describing it.

knowledge_gain=+33% · behavior_gain=+21% · autonomy=47%
what changed in the program design

Doubled visit dosage from 3 to 8-10 across 12 months

If the binding constraint is practice rather than belief, then more visits — not louder messaging — is the lever. We doubled the dosage. The next cohort is the test. Encouragement-of-autonomy at 47% endline is the hardest sub-domain; whether 8-10 visits will move it, or whether it requires a different intervention design, is open.
