═══════════════payment.layers // 01..03═══════════════
layer 01

Mobile money rails

FLWs are paid on the same mobile money networks they already use. M-Pesa, MTN, Airtel Money. No bank account required, no intermediary delay, no conversion friction.

layer 02

Verified-visit unit pricing

Payment is per verified visit, not per FLW enrolled or per training completed. The metric the funder cares about is the metric the system pays on. No proxy.

layer 03

Audit trail end-to-end

Every payment links back to the verified visit that triggered it — biometric ID, GPS, photo, statistical clearance. Funders can audit the chain from disbursement back to the household.

progression_of_thinking // cost_per_verified

Cost per verified visit fell as we scaled. We publish the bundle, not just the average.

Most platforms report cost-per-FLW-trained or cost-per-app-installed. Those numbers hide the lever that matters — whether the work was done. We picked verified-visit cost because the others let activity hide outcome.

2024 · the metric we chose

"We will report cost per verified visit."

The standard development sector metric is cost-per-FLW-trained or cost-per-program-launched — proxies for activity. We rejected them. The asymmetry between activity-cost and outcome-cost is exactly where most development funding gets eaten. If the work is what the funder is buying, the work is what the cost should reflect. Verified-visit cost can itself be gamed if the verification stack is shallow; we publish our four-layer stack so readers can audit it.

2025 · the trajectory

$2.20 → $1.70 over 14 months

In the CHC program, cost per verified visit fell 22% — from $2.20 to $1.70 — as the program scaled. Connect's architecture (LLO economics + performance-based payment + integrated tooling) is designed to produce cost-reducing scale; CHC is the program where we've measured it. The cost-per-activity number, which most platforms publish, hides this asymmetry.

cost_per_verified_visit=$2.20 → $1.70 · window=14 mo
2026 · the bundle the average hides

Nigeria $1.30 with ORS, outside Nigeria $0.78 without

The $1.70 weighted average masks the bundle. Nigeria CHC with ORS/Zinc co-packs averaged $1.30 across 14 LLO contracts. Outside Nigeria — VAS + deworming, no ORS — averaged $0.78 across 17 contracts. Setup fees are running $0.40/visit during the grant and projected to fall to ~$0.10/visit at scale. Fully-loaded: commodity + FLW + LLO + 20% Connect platform fee. We publish the split because anybody comparing programs needs the comparable bundle, not a flattened average.

ng_bundle=$1.30 · non_ng=$0.78 · setup_proj=$0.40 → $0.10
what's still open

Whether the curve holds outside CHC

Whether the same year-over-year cost curve holds in non-CHC programs is open. KMC, ECD, and Readers run on different commodity bundles, FLW dosages, and LLO economics. We're working with PRO Impact (IPA evaluation, $1.5M, 2026) on a cleaner counterfactual. The setup-fee projection ($0.40 → $0.10/visit at scale) is forward-looking; we will publish the converged number when the next cohort closes.

02
progression_of_thinking // displacement_question

Funders ask if paid work crowds out volunteer work. In Nigeria, paid is the volunteer work.

The "crowd-out" concern is one of the most-asked funder questions about performance pay in CHV systems. We commissioned a 24-interview survey in Gombe and Sokoto to answer it with data — and the answer surprised us.

the funder concern

"Paying for visits will degrade volunteer effort."

The classic concern: introduce performance pay into a community health volunteer system, and the unpaid work — the routine support, the relationship-building, the ad-hoc problem-solving — withers. Crowding out. It's a reasonable hypothesis. It's also based on a Western model of volunteer labor that may not transfer.

2025 · the survey we commissioned

24 in-depth interviews in Gombe and Sokoto

We commissioned a FLW Displacement Survey in October 2025 — 24 in-depth interviews across Gombe and Sokoto states, the heart of the CHC vaccine campaign. We asked how paid campaign work interacted with FLWs' usual volunteer responsibilities, on what schedule, with what role substitution.

survey=FLW Displacement · n=24 · geo=GOMBE+SOKOTO
what the survey found

Campaigns are not a disruption of the system. They are the system.

Campaign work in Nigeria isn't a disruption of routine care — it is the routine care. CHVs routinely run campaigns. Other volunteers cover routine work during them. In Gombe specifically, FLWs maintained volunteer hours by shifting schedules within Connect's 7am–7pm window. The "displacement" frame imposed a separation that didn't exist on the ground. The system already worked this way; performance pay didn't change that, it remunerated it.

what's still open

N=24 in two states. We don't claim it elsewhere yet.

The survey is small, scoped, and Nigerian. We expect this to hold in similar community-health settings; we'd re-survey before claiming it elsewhere. The Western "crowd-out" assumption may still apply in countries with different CHV structures — places where volunteer work is more individualized, less embedded in community committees, or more fragile to financial incentives. We are not yet generalizing.

progression_of_thinking // pace_of_pay

880,000 verified visits in 14 months — at a pace conventional NGO supply chains take years for.

A raw visit count is a generic number. The interesting thing is the pace, and how performance-payment plus integrated tooling produces it. We named the comparison out loud — and we name where the comparison breaks down.

2024-2025 · scale we measured

880K+ verified CHC visits across 8 countries

880,000+ verified CHC visits in 14 months across 8 countries — 44 LLOs activated in that window. Performance-payment delivery at a pace that conventional NGO-led VAS supplementation typically achieves over multi-year campaigns in the same geographies. The pace isn't an accident of effort; it's the product of paying for verified outcomes and giving the LLO infrastructure to deliver them.

visits=880K+ · window=14 mo · countries=8 · llos=44
why it scaled this way

Three loops, mutually reinforcing

One: Trial Run filtering let us activate LLOs based on field performance, not RFP scores. Two: integrated tooling (Learn → Deliver → Verify → Pay in one app) collapsed the coordination cost that usually dominates NGO operations. Three: per-verified-visit unit pricing aligned LLO incentives with the funder's: scale = more verified visits, not more grant approvals. None of these alone explains the pace. All three together do.

where the comparison breaks down

The "4 years of NGO output" framing is illustrative, not literal

There isn't a single published comparator that holds the country mix and commodity bundle constant. We use the time-equivalent framing because it captures something real about pace, but we name the limitation: it's a useful illustration, not a peer-reviewed counterfactual. The PRO Impact / IPA evaluation ($1.5M, 2026) is the controlled study that will produce the cleaner number.

2026 · the next test

$1.5M IPA RCT on cost-per-verified-visit

We're funding an RCT with PRO Impact (IPA evaluation, $1.5M) to produce a defensible counterfactual on cost per verified visit. The result is the test of whether the cost numbers and pace claims survive an external evaluator. We will publish whatever it shows.
