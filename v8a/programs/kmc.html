The challenge

Why KMC is a platform problem.

KMC is cheap, clinically established, and chronically under-delivered. The 2022 WHO guideline update formalized immediate-KMC (skin-to-skin contact before the newborn is clinically stable). Facility-based KMC wards are one half of the answer; the other half is what happens after discharge. Small and vulnerable newborns — low-birthweight, preterm, or born with complications — need continued KMC support, breastfeeding counseling, weight monitoring, and early warning for danger signs over the weeks following discharge. That's where home visits come in. That's where Connect comes in.

The Connect KMC program is a 5-visit structured protocol per SVN, delivered by trained FLWs with an equipment bundle (digital scale, thermometer, pulse oximeter) over the post-discharge window. The platform layer handles FLW training (Learn + AI coach), LLO coordination (Trial Run and beyond), visit verification, and performance-based payment tied to completing the visit sequence.

Insight 10 applied · Guardrails

Why the 3-day-post-discharge target is a guardrail, not a payment gate.

◆ Scope: Platform framework · KMC example
Phase 1 — the clinical target

First KMC home visit within 3 days of facility discharge.

The KMC protocol calls for the first home visit within 3 days of discharge. Clinically, this is when the newborn is most vulnerable and when caregiver behaviors (skin-to-skin contact, feeding, danger-sign recognition) are most mutable.

Phase 2 — what we didn't do

We did not make the 3-day window a payment criterion.

The temptation was to require first-visit-within-3-days for payment. It would be clinically justified. It would also have been operationally wrong. If payment depends on the 3-day window, FLWs learn to falsify the visit date. We lose the signal we need to see which discharges got supported on time and which didn't.

Phase 3 — what we did instead

4 mandatory home visits as the payment criterion. 3-day-post-discharge as an audited guardrail.

KMC's payment criterion is 4 mandatory home visits per SVN (with the 5th as a program-wide quality target). The 3-day-post-discharge timing is a guardrail — we audit it, report it, surface it to LLO coaching, but don't block payment on it.

Phase 4 — what the live data looks like

Current: ~50% meeting 3-day window. Target: 70%.

Current performance on the 3-day guardrail is 50% against a target of 70%. We know it's 50% precisely because we're not punishing misses — FLWs and LLOs report accurately. The gap is driven by three causes we're actively working on: facility-to-FLW discharge notification lag, LLO scheduling bottlenecks, and FLW availability constraints. Coaching, scheduling support, and discharge-notification integration are the current levers. If we turned the guardrail into a hard stop tomorrow, the reported number would jump to ~90% and most of the improvement would be fabrication.

Source: KMC Conceptual Model, March 2026.
Program design

The 5-visit KMC protocol.

Each KMC FLW carries a standard equipment bundle: a digital weighing scale, a thermometer, and a pulse oximeter. The 5-visit sequence covers post-discharge assessment, KMC-practice coaching, breastfeeding support, danger-sign surveillance, and developmental follow-up. Each visit is scripted in the Deliver app and backed by the Learn app's KMC content plus AI coach drilling. Verification runs the standard four layers — biometric match, GPS, photo, algorithmic — with the photo element particularly load-bearing (KMC depends on observable caregiver behavior, not just commodity delivery).

  • Visit 1 (within 3 days of discharge, guardrail): post-discharge weight, feeding assessment, skin-to-skin observation, danger-sign counseling.
  • Visit 2 (week 1): weight trajectory, exclusive breastfeeding support, temperature stability.
  • Visit 3 (week 2): SpO2 spot check, feeding adequacy, caregiver confidence check.
  • Visit 4 (week 4): weight, feeding, developmental milestones.
  • Visit 5 (week 6): program exit check, referral triage, handoff to routine care.
