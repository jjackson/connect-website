Insight 01 · Operating model

LLO performance can't be pre-vetted. Only proven.

◆ Scope: Platform

The Trial Run is the single most-reused mechanism we have. It's the reason we can activate new partners in fragile contexts without betting the grant on them.

Phase 1 — standard practice

Carefully vet LLOs before contracting. Track record, team size, organizational maturity.

Our early approach was procurement orthodoxy: due-diligence packages, reference checks, capacity assessments, prior grant history. For each candidate LLO we built a picture of whether they looked like an organization that could deliver — and contracted those that did.

Phase 2 — the pre-selection signal failed

Pre-selection was a poor predictor of field performance.

Looking across our first campaigns, we found that the LLOs who looked right on paper and the LLOs who actually delivered were only loosely correlated. Reputation, team size, organizational maturity — none of it reliably predicted whether an LLO would actually run the campaign well. Some of our best partners came from quiet organizations we'd have overlooked; some of the worst came from organizations with impressive prior work.

Phase 3 — Trial Run

Give every new LLO a small, performance-paid contract. Let delivery be the filter.

We invented the Trial Run model. New LLOs get a small initial contract — $3,000 setup fee for 3,000 verified child visits. Performance-based payment means we bear minimal financial risk if they fail. The contract is structured so that the LLO that doesn't deliver costs us less than the diligence process used to. The contract that does deliver graduates the LLO to a larger campaign, on earned evidence.

Phase 4 — what the portfolio showed

Of 37 LLOs contracted under the model: 24 entered Trial Runs, 10 underperformed, 3 went straight to larger campaigns.

Across 37 LLOs contracted under the Trial Run model, 24 entered Trial Runs, 10 underperformed and weren't continued, and 3 went straight to larger campaigns based on prior track record we had genuine evidence for. A ~60% pass rate on Trial Runs sounds low until you compare it to the counterfactual: without the model we were contracting at ~100% and removing partners mid-campaign, which is worse for everyone.

Phase 5 — sector-agnostic reuse

Same model, four different programs.

The Trial Run turned out to be sector-agnostic. It's been reused for reading glasses distribution (Readers), kangaroo mother care (KMC), and chlorine dispenser monitoring without modification. The exact same contractual structure — small payment-based trial with a graduation path — transfers across programs because the mechanism it's testing (can this LLO coordinate FLWs against a measurable target in their own context) is program-independent.

Source: Founders Pledge Final Report, Feb 2026 — Trial Run model data across 37 LLOs.
Insight 06 · What LLOs add

LLOs navigate vaccine hesitancy with religious leaders. We didn't teach them this.

◆ Scope: CHC · CLP Stage 2 vaccine campaign

This is the insight that clarified why the L in LLO is load-bearing. The platform's job is to enable locally-held knowledge, not replace it.

Phase 1 — the assumption

Digital training covers vaccine myths. FLWs read the content, they're equipped.

Going into the CLP Stage 2 vaccine campaign (run inside the CHC program), we believed the Learn app's module on vaccine hesitancy myths was sufficient preparation. If FLWs had absorbed the content, they'd be able to hold the conversations at the door.

Phase 2 — what LLOs did before we asked

5-6 of 7 LLOs independently activated religious and community leaders.

Of the 7 LLOs in the CLP Stage 2 vaccine campaign, 5-6 independently leveraged religious leaders, community leaders, or existing community relationships as part of their outreach strategy. They did this before we asked. This wasn't a training output. It was operating knowledge the LLO already had and applied without being prompted.

Phase 3 — what that looked like at the door

Personal example, community recommendation, trusted intermediaries.

The tactics varied by context. One FLW countered vaccine hesitancy with a personal example: "We were immunized, we have children." Another LLO had FLWs who were recommended by the community itself, lending immediate credibility that a platform-appointed stranger wouldn't have had. Religious leaders endorsed the campaign in Friday sermons. Community leaders walked the first few houses with FLWs to open doors.

Phase 4 — what we changed

Stop trying to centralize what LLOs already know how to do.

We stopped trying to push "outreach strategy" content from the platform. Instead we built the Learn + AI coach system to be receptive to what LLOs bring — prompting FLWs to reflect on what worked in their context, surfacing successful tactics to other LLOs through the coach, and leaving the actual outreach strategy in local hands.

The underlying point

The real intervention isn't what's in the app. It's what LLOs do with the platform.

This is why the L in LLO is load-bearing. Dimagi cannot centralize this knowledge — the platform's job is to enable it, not replace it. Every insight that tried to turn LLO-held knowledge into platform-shipped content got worse, not better.

Source: CLP Stage 2 Report, Feb 2026 — 7 LLOs, CHC vaccine campaign.
Insight 09 · Published failure

We documented our failure in CAR. Publishing it is part of the practice.

◆ Scope: CHC · Central African Republic contracting

The Trial Run model makes new partnerships low-risk. It does not make every context workable. Here's where it didn't.

Phase 1 — the confident claim

Connect's rapid partner activation model is portable to any context.

After the Trial Run model worked across multiple Nigerian states, Kenya, and early work in other LMIC settings, we had external pressure to claim the activation model was context-agnostic. Internally, we believed it.

Phase 2 — what happened in CAR

Both contracted LLOs dropped out. Staffing and connectivity.

In the Central African Republic, part of the CHC vaccine work, we contracted two LLOs under the Trial Run model. Both dropped out. Primary reasons were staffing (the LLOs couldn't keep field teams stable under security and displacement pressures) and connectivity (intermittent network and power made the Deliver app's verification flow harder to sustain than in other settings). Remote coordination from Dimagi, which had been adequate elsewhere, wasn't enough to compensate for the in-country fragility.

Phase 3 — what we revised

Fragile contexts likely require dedicated in-country presence. We're no longer claiming otherwise.

We revised the portability claim. Activating partners in fragile contexts likely requires dedicated in-country presence — someone who can support LLOs through the exact local disruptions that Trial Run economics alone can't buffer against. We're not repeating the same remote-only approach in similar geographies.

Why this is on a marketing site

Real platforms publish what didn't work.

Presenting only the wins is a tell. The CAR experience is shaping our approach to fragile contexts now, and the funders we want to work with are the ones who prefer reading this over reading around it.

Source: Founders Pledge Final Report, Feb 2026 — CAR LLO contracting, CHC program.
