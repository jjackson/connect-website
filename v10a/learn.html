§1 · Learn · how digital training produces competent FLWs

Knowledge tests didn't produce competent FLWs. We had to layer.

Workers who aced our digital knowledge test still struggled in real visits. So we stopped treating the test as a release gate and built four additional layers around it. Across roughly 400 FLWs of iterative experimentation, 88% now score above 70% on first observed visits2 and 85% progress from training to delivery2. This is the methodology — and the parts that aren't done.

Learning 1 of 3

The test passes. The visit fails.

The first version of Connect's training pipeline was clean. A self-paced Learn app with modules per program. An in-app multiple-choice test at the end. A pass-mark of 70%. We thought: if a worker can answer the questions, they have the knowledge to deliver.

They had the knowledge. They didn't have the competence. Workers who aced the digital knowledge test still struggled with the parts of frontline work that are not test-shaped — handling a skeptical caregiver, recognising subtle danger signs, motivational interviewing during a five-minute visit while a baby cries on a hip. The supervised-observation visit, the last step in our pipeline, kept catching workers the test had passed.

We rebuilt the pipeline around that gap. The test is still there but it has been demoted to a midpoint, not a release gate. The full sequence is now: self-paced learning in the Learn app, the in-app knowledge test, daily interaction with an AI coach bot built on Dimagi's Open Chat Studio3, structured peer practice, and one supervised observation visit rated by an LLO supervisor. The test gates progress to the AI coach. The AI coach is what closes the gap to competence.

What the AI coach adds that the test does not: scenario-based practice rather than recall; motivational-interviewing drills; daily reflection on the worker's actual recent visits; identifying knowledge gaps through conversation rather than four-option multiple choice. In the Kenya CHC pilot, the chatbot — accessed via WhatsApp — handled 500 of 511 FLW queries without escalating to a human supervisor1. Independent evaluation: 88.7% accuracy, 81.5% usefulness, 5-second average response time1. The 11 escalations are the data we care about most — they are how we improve the bot — but the high handle rate is what lets one human supervisor cover an LLO with hundreds of workers without becoming a bottleneck.

Across the layered pipeline, validated with roughly 400 FLWs through iterative experiments2: 85% of workers progress from training to service delivery. 88% score above 70% on their first observed visit. 77% pass the supervised observation. The 23% who don't pass the supervised observation get coaching, not termination — and the AI coach captures the patterns of where they failed for the next iteration of the Learn content.

Learning 2 of 3 · ECD

In ECD, the attitudes were already there. The gap was knowledge and behavior.

The Learn app does more than train workers — it shapes the curriculum that workers then carry into homes. In our Malawi ECD pilot we discovered that a basic assumption baked into the curriculum was wrong, and we redesigned the dosage as a result. This is a Learn-page story because it is about what training is for.

We thought an ECD intervention should build positive caregiver attitudes about responsive caregiving, talk-and-play, and developmental support. The first three-visit pilot was structured around attitude change. Then we ran the baseline survey across 50 caregivers4. Nearly all caregivers already held positive ECD attitudes at baseline. They agreed talk-and-play supports brain development. They endorsed responsive caregiving. The attitude scores were high before we started and high after we finished.

Where we actually moved the needle, in the Malawi pilot endline4: knowledge of the timing of rapid child development rose by 33 percentage points; knowledge of ways to support mental development rose 41 points; observed teaching behavior rose 21 points; observed encouragement-of-autonomy rose 21 points but reached only 47% at endline. The pattern is consistent: attitudes were a ceiling, not a floor. Knowledge moved sharply when it was given. Behavior moved more slowly and unevenly because behavior is not a quiz answer.

So we rebuilt the dosage. The 3-visit pilot is being expanded to 8–10 visits across 12 months — not because we need more time to convince caregivers ECD matters (they know it matters) but because behavior change takes sustained practice. The extra visits are not more talk; they are more observed practice with feedback. The Learn app for ECD now opens with a frank acknowledgement of the floor effect — "you already know this matters" — and routes the curriculum into practical sequencing of rapid-development milestones.

What we have not moved: encouragement of autonomy is still 47% at endline, the hardest sub-domain. We do not yet know whether 8–10 visits will move it, or whether it requires a different intervention design entirely. That is the next iteration. The ECD case is a reminder that training quality is not measured against the test — it is measured against the change in the home.

Learning 3 of 3 · CHC Kenya

The AI coach handles 97.8% of queries. The 2.2% is the data we use most.

The AI coach earns its place in the pipeline because of an empirical handle rate, not a marketing promise. In our Kenya CHC pilot1, the chatbot — built on Dimagi's Open Chat Studio and accessed by FLWs through WhatsApp — handled 500 of 511 FLW queries without escalating to a human supervisor. That is a 97.8% handle rate at a 5-second average response time. Independent structured evaluation rated responses 88.7% accurate and 81.5% useful.

The architectural reason this matters is leverage. A traditional supervisor model collapses at scale: one supervisor per ten FLWs is expensive and slow; one per fifty is cheap and unsupervised. The AI coach as middle layer answers the routine 97.8% — protocol questions, dosage clarifications, "what do I do if the caregiver refuses" scenarios — and routes only the genuinely novel queries to human supervisors. That 2.2% escalation stream is also the dataset we mine to extend the bot. Every escalation is a flagged gap in either the Learn content, the protocol, or the bot's prompt; we triage them weekly.

What we have not yet published: the failure-mode analysis on the 11 Kenya escalations, or whether the 97.8% handle rate holds outside English-language deployments. We are running the bot in non-English contexts now. The Open Chat Studio platform supports multilingual content but the FLW corpus we have used to refine prompts is English-heavy; a clean apples-to-apples comparison across languages is something we expect to publish when the next pilot closes.

The broader claim — and the one this section is making — is not that the AI coach is a finished product. It is that knowledge tests, peer practice, and supervised observation are insufficient on their own to produce competent FLWs at scale, and that an AI coach in the middle layer is the empirically-validated way to make the rest of the pipeline work. The 97.8% is the lever. The pipeline before and after the lever is what makes the FLWs competent.

What we don't know yet

  1. Whether the AI coach or peer practice contributes more to the 77% supervised-observation pass rate — we have not yet published the decomposition.
  2. Whether the 97.8% AI-coach handle rate from the Kenya CHC pilot holds in non-English-language deployments.
  3. Encouragement-of-autonomy is the hardest ECD sub-domain (47% endline). Whether 8–10 visits will move it, or it needs a different design entirely.
