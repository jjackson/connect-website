Method · Three pillars

What the Learn module actually does.

Pillar 01

Mobile-first, offline-capable.

Self-paced training on the worker's own phone. No classroom scheduling. No travel costs. Interactive lessons, practice scenarios, assessment-driven progression.

Pillar 02

Locally relevant content.

Training materials localised per intervention and region. Approved by subject matter experts — typically the LLO or a program-specific SME identified for the geography.

Pillar 03

Assessment-gated progression.

Workers must pass the in-app assessment before they are cleared. Fail means retake. The pass bar is standard across LLOs and FLWs for any given program.

Learnings · Where method changed

Three learnings that reshaped the Learn module.

Each entry is a receipt for a methodological decision. What we thought; what we learned; what we did about it.

01

Knowledge tests alone don't produce competent FLWs.

Malawi · 2025 · ~400 FLWs validated
Source ECD Feb 2026 report
Iterative experiments, 2023–25

Our initial assumption was simple: if a Frontline Worker passes an in-app knowledge test, they are ready to deliver. This is how most digital training operates. It is also how our first pilots were structured.

Iteration after iteration told us something different. Workers who aced the test still struggled with the nuances of in-person counselling — how to handle a sceptical caregiver, how to recognise the subtle danger signs that don't appear in a multiple-choice question, how to practice motivational interviewing instead of lecturing. Knowledge ≠ competence.

We layered the model. Self-paced digital learning in Learn; an in-app knowledge test (must pass); daily interaction with an AI coach built on Open Chat Studio; practice with peers; one supervised observation visit rated by an LLO supervisor. Five components, not one.

What we thought
Pass the test → ready to deliver. Digital-only, scalable.
What we learned
Test measures recall. Field demands judgement, posture, patience.

02

Caregiver attitudes were already correct. The gap was knowledge.

Malawi Pilot · 50 caregiver surveys · Apr–Jul 2025
Source ECD Overview, Feb 2026
Baseline/endline surveys, Malawi

An ECD intervention, by textbook design, should build positive attitudes about responsive caregiving, talk-and-play, developmental support. The baseline data told us otherwise.

Nearly all 50 caregivers surveyed at baseline already held positive attitudes. They agreed that talk-and-play supports brain development. They endorsed responsive caregiving. Attitudes scored high before and after. Attitudes didn't move — because they were already there.

The 3-visit pilot is expanding to 8–10 visits over 12 months. Not because caregivers need convincing that ECD matters — they know — but because behaviour change takes sustained practice.

03

An AI coach turned out to be the part of training that compounds.

Kenya pilot · 2025 · 511 queries
Source FP Final Report, Feb 2026
Independent evaluation, 2025

The early Learn module was a closed loop: course, test, pass. Useful, but finite. Once a worker passed, training ended. Real learning, of course, happens on the job — when something surprises a worker and they need guidance, fast, in context.

We built an AI coach on Dimagi's Open Chat Studio. It sits between Learn and Deliver. FLWs ask questions via WhatsApp — dosing, eligibility, procedure — and get immediate, context-appropriate guidance.

What we changed Daily AI coach interaction is now part of ECD as a paid activity. Ongoing support scales without adding supervisors. The coach identifies knowledge gaps through conversation — not multiple choice.
