ECD: caregivers' attitudes were already correct. Knowledge and behavior were the gap.

Connect's ECD program was designed around an assumption — that caregivers needed attitude change to engage in talk-and-play and responsive caregiving. The Malawi pilot baseline scrambled that assumption. 50 caregivers, baseline and endline. The page below treats four questions the program had to answer once the floor effect emerged — and one open question we still have not closed.

Q1 · the floor effect · I7 · N6 hard data ⟶ learn#ecd-floor ⟶ insights#n6

Were caregivers' ECD attitudes already where we needed them, and was the curriculum aimed at the wrong gap?

The standard ECD intervention thesis says caregivers undervalue talk-and-play and need attitude change. We designed the first three-visit pilot around attitude shift — modules and FLW prompts emphasizing why responsive caregiving matters, why the first 1,000 days matter, why language exposure matters. Then we ran the baseline survey across 50 caregivers in Malawi1. The data scrambled the thesis.

Nearly all 50 caregivers already held positive ECD attitudes at baseline. They agreed talk-and-play supports brain development. They endorsed responsive caregiving. The attitude scores were a ceiling, not a floor — high before we started and high after we finished. The intervention didn't need to convince them — it needed to close the gap between belief and practice.

Where we actually moved the needle, in the endline panel: knowledge of the timing of rapid child development rose +33 percentage points; knowledge of ways to support mental development rose +41; observed teaching behavior rose +21; observed encouragement-of-autonomy rose +21 but reached only 47% at endline. The pattern was consistent: knowledge moved sharply when given, behavior moved more slowly because behavior is not a quiz answer.

This finding — that the floor effect is on attitudes, not on knowledge or behavior — is a publishable insight. Almost every ECD pitch leads with attitude change. Naming the floor effect publicly forces a sharper conversation about what the intervention is actually for: closing the gap between belief and practice, not building belief from scratch. We have used it to redesign the curriculum and the dosage (Q2 below), and we have used it to recalibrate how we describe ECD to funders — the program's value is not in convincing caregivers that talk-and-play matters; the program's value is in helping them do it more often, more consistently, with feedback.

Source: ECD Overview (Feb 2026) — Malawi pilot, baseline and endline survey across 50 caregivers. [1]
Q2 · dosage redesign · N6 triangulated ⟶ insights#n6

How do we redesign dosage when the gap is behavior, not belief?

Once the floor effect was visible, the 3-visit pilot stopped making sense as designed. Three visits is enough time to deliver knowledge content, but not enough time to coach behavior change in any sustained way. Behavior change requires repeated practice with feedback — not more lectures.

We expanded to 8–10 visits across 12 months. The extra visits are not more talk; they are more observed practice with feedback. The Learn content for ECD opens with an explicit acknowledgement of the floor effect — "you already know this matters" — and routes the curriculum into practical sequencing of rapid-development milestones. The FLW visit script is now built around observation: watch the caregiver demonstrate a talk-and-play interaction, give specific feedback, repeat next visit.

The redesign is also a methodology commitment. We treat the dosage decision as evidence-driven, not as a default. Three visits is the cheapest version of the program; 8–10 visits is the version that matches the actual gap. Cost per child rose accordingly — the published range is $7–$18, depending on partner geography and bundle. We accept the higher number because the intervention's measured effect requires the dosage; reducing dosage to lower the headline cost would defeat the purpose.

What's still uncertain about the redesign: we have not yet completed an endline on the 8–10-visit cohort across the multi-country expansion. The hypothesis behind the redesign is well-grounded in the Malawi data — knowledge moved sharply, behavior moved unevenly, and behavior change is what additional dosage is supposed to produce. But the empirical confirmation that 8–10 visits actually moves the autonomy sub-domain (Q4) is something we will publish as the data lands. If it doesn't move, the dosage redesign was directionally right but the visit content needs to change too. We are tracking the question rather than declaring it closed.

Source: ECD Overview (Feb 2026) — dosage redesign rationale and cost-per-child range.
Q3 · FLW competence · I8 · N5 hard data ⟶ learn#layered ⟶ insights#n5

If knowledge tests don't predict competence, what does, for an ECD FLW?

ECD FLWs were the cohort that first surfaced the knowledge-vs-competence gap that became Connect's layered Learn pipeline. Workers who aced the digital ECD knowledge test — danger signs, milestone timing, encouragement strategies — still struggled with the parts of an ECD visit that are not test-shaped: handling a caregiver who already knows the content but isn't doing it, recognising the difference between disengagement and exhaustion, sequencing a five-minute play interaction in a noisy household.

The pipeline now in use: self-paced Learn modules, in-app multiple-choice test (a midpoint, not a release gate), daily AI-coach interaction on Open Chat Studio, structured peer practice, and one supervised-observation visit. Across roughly 400 FLWs of iterative experimentation across ECD, CHC, and KMC: 85% progress from training to delivery; 88% score above 70% on first observed visits; 77% pass supervised observation. The 23% who don't pass get coaching, not termination.

For ECD specifically, the AI coach is doing more behaviorally-focused work than in CHC: practice scenarios for caregiver counseling, motivational interviewing drills, troubleshooting "I know I should do talk-and-play but I'm tired" responses. The AI coach is not the whole pipeline; it is the middle layer that handles the routine 97.8% of FLW queries (the platform-wide handle rate from the Kenya CHC pilot) so human supervisors can focus on the genuinely novel cases.

The supervised observation is the binding constraint on the ECD pipeline. An LLO supervisor watches an FLW conduct a real ECD visit in a real home and rates the worker on a structured rubric — caregiver engagement, milestone-appropriate activity selection, age-appropriate language, danger-sign recognition. Workers who pass go to service delivery. Workers who don't pass get coaching, and the patterns of what they failed on get pushed back into the Learn content for the next cohort. This is the part of the pipeline that no test, AI coach, or peer practice can substitute for — and it is the reason the 77% pass rate is the metric we optimize on, not the 88% first-visit number.

Source: Connect Learn pipeline metrics across ~400 FLWs spanning ECD, CHC, KMC; FP Final Report Kenya pilot for AI-coach handle rate.
Q4 · the hardest sub-domain · N6 hypothesis ⟶ insights#n6

Why is encouragement-of-autonomy the hardest sub-domain — and will more dosage move it?

Across the four behavior sub-domains the Malawi endline measured, encouragement-of-autonomy was the laggard. It rose +21 percentage points (matching observed teaching behavior) but reached only 47% at endline — well below the rates we saw on the other behaviors. The gap is meaningful: encouragement-of-autonomy maps to the part of ECD that takes longest to change because it's about how a caregiver shapes a moment of independent exploration, not about whether they know exploration matters.

We do not yet know whether 8–10 visits will move the autonomy number, or whether autonomy requires a different intervention design entirely. The hypothesis behind the dosage expansion is that observed practice across more sessions, with specific feedback, can move it — that's why the expanded program leans on practice rather than content. But the data hasn't come in yet.

If the next endline (Mozambique and Nigeria, in addition to Malawi follow-on) shows autonomy moving on the 8–10-visit dosage, the dosage redesign was the right answer. If it doesn't move, the design has to change — and we will publish the redesign rationale rather than soft-pedaling the gap. This is the question we are most uncertain about on the program.

One reason we suspect autonomy is genuinely harder than the other sub-domains: encouragement-of-autonomy lives in moments most caregivers don't think of as "ECD interactions" at all — letting a toddler choose what to play with, watching them struggle to solve a puzzle without intervening, narrating their own choices as a model. These are continuous behaviors, not discrete events the FLW can observe-and-grade in a single visit. The intervention may need to evolve from FLW-observed practice toward something closer to caregiver self-recording or peer-group reinforcement. We are not yet ready to commit to that redesign — but we are ready to publish that the current design has not closed the gap, and that we are watching the next cohort's autonomy numbers more carefully than any other endpoint.

Source: ECD Overview (Feb 2026) — endline behavior sub-domain breakdown; dosage redesign hypothesis.

Open questions on ECD

  1. The 47% endline on encouragement-of-autonomy is the hardest sub-domain. Whether 8–10 visits will move it is open.
  2. The Malawi pilot is N=50 caregivers. Whether the attitude-already-correct finding holds in Mozambique and Nigeria contexts is open.
  3. Whether AI coach contribution to the 77% pass rate generalizes outside ECD — we have not run the controlled comparison.
