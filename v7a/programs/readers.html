Programs/Readers
Health / Livelihood · Launching 2026

15,000 pairs of reading glasses. A test of the platform in a new domain.

Presbyopia — age-related near-vision loss — affects billions globally. In underserved communities, the lack of affordable reading glasses limits productivity, education, and economic participation. Connect Readers is a partnership with RestoringVision to close that gap in Northeast Nigeria, and it is also our first real test of the platform outside child and maternal health.

The challenge.

Presbyopia is one of the most widespread and easily-treated conditions in global health. A pair of reading glasses with the right diopter restores near-vision for a cost that is effectively zero per unit when donated at scale. The barrier is not the lenses. It is the distribution: traditional vision care programs require people to travel to clinics, and people who live far from clinics — in rural communities, in market towns, in small villages — do not travel.

For many recipients, Connect Readers will provide their first pair of corrective lenses. That pair is the difference between being able to read a Bible, count money at market, thread a needle, sort a harvest, or not. The economic and educational consequences of uncorrected near-vision loss compound quickly in occupations that depend on fine visual work.

The Readers program inverts the access model. Rather than ask presbyopic adults to come to a clinic for screening, Frontline Workers screen and fit door-to-door, in the same way CHC workers do vitamin A and deworming. The performance-based payment model means the cost of the program tracks actual distribution, not organizational overhead.

Program mechanics

How a Readers FLW delivers a pair of glasses.

Standardized screening

Mobile-guided near-vision screening. FLWs follow a simple protocol to identify candidates and determine appropriate lens strength, from +1.0 to +3.0 diopters.

Digital training

Workers complete the standard Connect Learn module before going door-to-door. Self-paced. Offline. Knowledge-test gated. Same five-gate model used across the platform.

Door-to-door distribution

FLWs conduct household visits in assigned areas. Screen adults 35+. Fit glasses of appropriate strength on-site. Complete distribution in a single visit.

Verified payment

GPS + biometric verification per distribution. FLWs receive mobile money payments for each verified screening and distribution. Supply chain managed by the LLO.

What does it mean that the same platform can deliver a Vitamin A dose and a pair of reading glasses?

It is one thing to describe a platform as general-purpose. It is a different thing to actually apply it to a new domain and watch what breaks. Readers is that test. The program is designed to distribute 15,000 pairs of reading glasses in Northeast Nigeria, and it is the first Connect program outside child and maternal health. The question we are actually asking is whether the LDVP mechanics are sector-agnostic or whether they are quietly shaped by the child-health programs that built them.

The structural answer, so far, is that they are more general than we sometimes remember. The Trial Run model is already working on a different domain — KMC home visits and reading glasses are nothing alike clinically, but they are the same kind of problem operationally. A small initial contract. Performance-based payment. Verification stack adapted to the specific delivery (biometrics + GPS + a photo of the fitted glasses, rather than biometrics + GPS + a MUAC measurement). Scale-up on proven delivery. None of the platform primitives needed to be rebuilt.

What had to be adapted was the content in Learn and the specific verification signals. Near-vision screening is a short clinical protocol — identify candidates over 35 or with self-reported near-vision difficulty, perform a standardized screening, match them to the appropriate diopter. That protocol went into the Learn app. It is shorter than the ECD or KMC curriculum, which is why we expect FLW training throughput on Readers to be relatively fast.

Connect Readers is also a partnership-heavy program in a way the others have not been. The glasses themselves are donated at scale by RestoringVision. The supply chain — receiving the glasses, allocating to FLWs, tracking inventory through the distribution — is managed by the LLOs (eHA and C3HD). Dimagi provides the platform, RestoringVision provides the commodities, the LLOs provide the delivery infrastructure. The program's cost-effectiveness depends on all three of those relationships functioning well, and our track record on that kind of three-way coordination is one of the things we are watching most closely.

The honest thing to say about Readers is that we do not yet have pilot results. LLOs are finalized. Campaign planning is complete. Dates are pending final contract signing. What this page will look like in twelve months — once the glasses are in houses and the verification data is in — will be substantially different. We are publishing a sparser page now rather than pretending we have results we do not.

What do we expect to learn from Readers that we could not learn from CHC or KMC?

CHC and KMC are both programs where the patient is a child or a newborn, and the caregiver is a secondary participant. Readers is different. The patient is an adult — often an older adult, often someone engaged in an economic activity that reading glasses will immediately affect. That shift changes several things about how the platform needs to behave, and it is the source of what we expect to learn.

The first open question is how verification works when the beneficiary is an adult rather than a child. Our fraud detection work in CHC is built on demographic data specific to children — age, gender, MUAC. The feature set for a Readers distribution is different: the candidate's self-reported age, the diopter fitted, the outcome of the near-vision screening. We will need to retrain the algorithmic layer on these features, and we expect the initial AUC on adversarial data to be lower than the CHC number (0.91), because we have less accumulated data. We will post that number when we have it.

The second is how the door-to-door coverage model applies to a population that is more dispersed than all children under five. Presbyopia is common in adults over 35, but not universal, and the screening step is more informative than, say, a Vitamin A visit to a household with an eligible child. Microplanning may need to be parameterized differently — the question is not "visit every household" but "screen every adult over 35 in every household" — and we are not yet sure whether the DU structure transfers directly or needs adjustment.

The third is the economic-impact side. Vitamin A is a public-health intervention. Reading glasses are a public-health intervention and a livelihood intervention at the same time. A tailor who can see small stitches again earns more income. A farmer who can read seed labels works with less error. We are interested in whether we can measure that second-order effect alongside the direct visual-acuity effect, and whether the evaluation framework we use for CHC can extend to livelihood outcomes. This is an open research question, not a settled part of the design.

What we will not be able to claim for Readers until we have run the program is whether the 22% cost inversion we saw in CHC generalizes. The economics of donated commodities are structurally different from funded commodities. The cost per distribution should be low because RestoringVision is contributing the glasses, but the setup costs, the verification costs, and the LLO overhead are borne by Connect. Whether the total cost per verified distribution falls as we scale — the way CHC did — is precisely the kind of claim we will not make until we have evidence.
