Closing the post-discharge gap for the smallest newborns.

Hospitals provide essential care in the first days of life. Then families go home. For small and vulnerable newborns — low birth weight, preterm — the weeks after discharge are the highest-mortality window and the least-supported point in the continuum of care. Connect KMC reaches those families where they are.

The challenge

2.3 million newborn deaths a year. Most after discharge.

Every year, an estimated 2.3 million newborns die during the first 28 days of life, accounting for nearly half of all deaths in children under five. Most are small and vulnerable newborns (SVNs) — 80% low birth weight, two-thirds preterm.

Kangaroo Mother Care — continuous skin-to-skin contact, exclusive breastfeeding, and structured follow-up — reduces neonatal mortality by up to 40%. The WHO recommends KMC for all newborns ≤2000g at birth. Yet global KMC coverage remains below 5%. The practice is proven. The delivery infrastructure isn't.

Hospitals discharge families within days. Structured follow-up at home rarely exists. Caregivers return with a small, fragile baby — navigating feeding, temperature regulation, and infection prevention on their own. Connect KMC operates in exactly this gap.

How it works

Five components. All running on Connect.

01

LLOs partner with health facilities. FLWs meet caregivers before discharge, explain KMC, and enroll eligible families in the program.

02

FLWs conduct structured follow-up visits at home — no travel for families. Minimum 4 visits per SVN, all within 60 days of enrollment.

03

Counseling on KMC

Sustained skin-to-skin contact with a KMC wrap the program provides. Exclusive breastfeeding support, hygiene guidance, feeding troubleshooting.

04

Danger-sign screening

Every visit includes a weighing scale, thermometer, pulse oximeter, and measuring tape. Danger signs flagged in the app trigger referrals with auto follow-up.

05

Emotional & social support

Many SVN mothers experience stigma and isolation. FLWs offer encouragement and practical advice, building confidence and trust.

06

WHO-aligned standards

The program aligns with the WHO Global Position Paper on KMC, the Every Newborn Action Plan, and SDG 3.2 (neonatal mortality <12 per 1,000).

Learning 01 · The post-discharge gap · KMC program design

Building a program around the 60 days after hospital discharge.

The conventional KMC model places the practice inside the hospital: nurses teach, mothers learn, families get sent home with a pamphlet. It works — while they're there. It breaks the moment the discharge papers are signed.

Connect KMC treats the 60 days after discharge as the actual intervention window. LLOs partner with health facilities to identify eligible SVNs — typically ≤2000g at birth. FLWs meet caregivers before discharge, explain the program in the local language, and enroll them with informed consent. From that moment, the schedule is structured: minimum 4 home visits per SVN, all within 60 days, with at least one visit at 28+ days so the full neonatal period is covered1.

FLWs arrive at the family's home carrying a clinical kit — portable weighing scale, thermometer, pulse oximeter, measuring tape — and a phone running CommCare. Each visit has a defined workflow: weigh and measure the baby, screen for danger signs (hypothermia, poor weight gain, signs of infection), observe and coach skin-to-skin technique with a program-provided KMC wrap, counsel on exclusive breastfeeding, and offer social support. Danger signs flagged in the app trigger a facility referral. The app auto-prompts follow-up at the next visit, so no family falls between systems.

The post-discharge gap isn't a feature of health systems we can wait to fix. It's the window in which SVNs die. Connect KMC treats it as the thing we're delivering into.

The 60-day structure is not arbitrary. It mirrors WHO's recommended follow-up window for SVNs and aligns with when mortality risk remains highest2. By operating entirely after discharge — in the home, on a schedule families can meet without traveling back to overwhelmed facilities — the program reaches the point in the continuum where coverage has been closest to zero.

Learning 02 · Guardrails without gaming · KMC live performance

Auditing quality without corrupting the data we need.

The temptation in a performance-based system is to attach a sanction to every quality target. If you want workers to visit within 3 days of discharge, don't pay them if they miss the window. It feels like accountability. The consequence is that workers learn to submit visits dated for the 3-day window — and the platform sees 100% compliance on a measure that has stopped measuring anything.

Connect KMC draws a deliberate line between two classes of check. Payment criteria are hard: a visit is paid only when identity is verified, GPS confirms the worker was at the home, the visit met a minimum duration, and the required data was captured. Guardrails are soft: they trigger audits, performance conversations, and targeted LLO support — but they don't block payment1. The distinction is small on paper and large in behavior.

First-visit-within-3-days currently sits at 50% against a 70% target. We have that real number because we chose not to get a fake 100%.

The live example: the program requires a minimum of 4 home visits per SVN — payment criterion, enforced. The guardrail "first visit within 3 days of discharge" — audited, not enforced. Current performance against that guardrail sits at 50%, against a 70% target. Early discharge rate sits at 7.6%, against a 5% target. Average number of visits per case is 3.4, against a 5-visit guardrail. SVN enrollments average 12.8 days old at first contact (under our 28-day target).

Those numbers are uncomfortable. They're also real. Because we chose to get the real data instead of a cleaner-looking fake, we can actually work on the gaps: integrating discharge notifications with facility partners, adding scheduling support into the FLW workflow, and coaching specific LLOs where the gap is largest. We'd rather publish a 50% number we're moving than a 100% number that's lying.

Learning 03 · Clinical standards · KMC, WHO 2022 alignment

Why align with WHO guidance when the delivery looks different from anything WHO has implemented before?

The WHO's 2022 KMC guidance is explicit on the clinical core: continuous skin-to-skin contact, exclusive breastfeeding, close follow-up for newborns ≤2000g at birth, emphasis on the post-discharge period1. Where WHO guidance is less explicit — because it can't be — is on the delivery mechanism. How exactly do you get consistent, high-quality post-discharge follow-up at scale in low-resource settings? That's implementation work, and it's where Connect KMC innovates without diverging from the clinical standard.

The alignment is structural. Minimum 4 home visits per SVN matches WHO's recommended follow-up density. The clinical workflow — weight monitoring, danger sign screening, breastfeeding counseling, referral when needed — follows WHO's recommended content. KMC wraps enable sustained skin-to-skin contact, per WHO's guidance. All visits within 60 days of enrollment, with at least one at 28+ days, covers the neonatal mortality window. The program also aligns with the Every Newborn Action Plan and SDG 3.2 (neonatal death <12 per 1,000 livebirths by 2030).

The clinical work matches the best available guidance. The delivery architecture makes that work economically feasible at scale.

What's different is how the work gets coordinated, verified, and paid for. WHO guidance doesn't describe performance-based payment tied to GPS-verified home visits. It doesn't describe digital training with in-app assessments. It doesn't describe algorithmic fraud detection on submission patterns. Those are platform-layer innovations that serve the clinical standard — they're not replacements for it.

For funders, the alignment gives a clear answer to "is this an experimental intervention, or a proven one?" The intervention is proven. The delivery model is the novel thing. For clinical partners, alignment means the care families receive is the care WHO would have them receive — it just arrives at their door instead of requiring them to travel back to a facility that can't absorb the volume.

What's open

What we don't know yet.

01
Whether discharge-notification integration alone closes the 50% → 70% within-3-days enrollment gap, or the 3-day target itself needs to be redesigned.
02
Equipment availability across the 6 KMC LLO partners has been uneven. We don't yet have a published audit of equipment uptime.
03
Whether KMC FLWs will sustain ≥4 visits per case at 5,000+ scale; current average is 3.4 against a 5-visit guardrail.
