Connect/How It Works/02 Deliver

Trained workers. Local organizations. Mobile-guided delivery.

Trained FLWs deliver services in their communities using a mobile app that guides every interaction. LLOs manage local operations — approvals, procurement, recruitment, community relationships. Connect manages everything else. What follows is what that division of labor has taught us.

Learning 01 / Partner activation

LLO performance can't be pre-vetted. Only proven.

The question we had

Dimagi's traditional approach to partner selection followed standard procurement practice. Review the organization's track record. Assess team size and composition. Evaluate financial and operational capacity. Score the application. Select the winners. Sign contracts. Begin work.

This process is disciplined and defensible. We wanted to test whether it actually predicts field performance.

What we tried

Across the Founders Pledge grant period we tracked pre-contract scoring against post-contract delivery across 37 contracted LLOs. We looked for the correlation procurement practice assumes exists: higher pre-contract score → better delivery performance.

What we found

Pre-selection was a poor predictor of field performance. Past reputation, team size, organizational maturity — none of it reliably predicted whether an LLO would actually deliver. Some well-credentialed organizations stalled. Some lesser-known applicants outperformed. The signal we needed was not in the application.

This is not a Dimagi-specific finding — it's a structural limitation of document-based procurement. But in our case the cost of getting it wrong is high: a non-performing LLO eats staff time, delays a campaign, and burns trust with the funder.

What changed

We invented the Trial Run model. New LLOs get a small initial contract — $3,000 setup fee for 3,000 verified child visits. Performance-based payment means Connect bears minimal financial risk if the LLO fails. Of the 37 LLOs contracted under this model: 24 entered Trial Runs; 10 underperformed and were not continued; 3 went straight to larger campaigns based on prior track record; 17 completed campaigns following successful trials.

The Trial Run model has turned out to be sector-agnostic. We've reused it unchanged for reading glasses distribution, KMC, and chlorine dispensers. The economics of pay-for-verified-delivery mean this kind of graduated activation has no downside for the funder.

What's still open

65% of Trial Runs converted to larger contracts. We don't yet know whether that hit rate is optimal — too high and we're wasting Trial Run capacity on organizations we should have rejected earlier; too low and we're being too generous upfront. We're collecting signal on what the upstream EOI could filter to improve the hit rate without reverting to full pre-vetting.

SourceFounders Pledge Final Report · Feb 2026 — 37 contracted LLOs, Jan 2025–Feb 2026.
Learning 02 / Locally-led means locally-led

LLOs navigate vaccine hesitancy with religious leaders. We didn't teach them this.

The question we had

The CLP Stage 2 vaccine promotion campaign ran across seven locally-led organizations in Nigeria. Connect provided the training content — vaccine safety, answers to common hesitancy questions, the clinical case for immunization. The assumption baked into the curriculum was that if FLWs knew the material, they could handle the conversations that happen at the doorstep.

After the campaign, we asked LLOs to describe what actually worked.

What we tried

Debrief interviews with all seven LLO partners. We asked them to walk us through the outreach strategies they used — which ones produced uptake, which ones fell flat, what they'd change. We did not script the question in a way that biased toward any particular answer.

What we found

Of the 7 LLOs, 5–6 independently leveraged religious leaders, community leaders, or existing community relationships as part of their outreach strategy. They did this before we asked. One FLW described countering hesitancy with a personal example — "We were immunized, we have children" — a form of social proof the curriculum did not cover. Another LLO had recruited FLWs who were recommended by the community itself, lending immediate credibility.

The effect is not subtle. Vaccine hesitancy in these contexts is often mediated by trust in institutional messaging. Religious and community leaders already hold that trust. The LLO's strategy was to route training content through existing trust networks rather than compete with them. The curriculum never told them to do this. They knew it from context.

What changed

The material change is what we didn't do. We did not rewrite the training to prescribe religious-leader engagement. We did not build a feature in the app to require it. Instead, we shifted how we think about the platform's job: the platform's job is to enable the LLO's local knowledge, not replace it. That framing has affected how we scope training content for every subsequent program — we now aim to cover the technical floor and leave room for LLOs to layer local strategy on top.

What's still open

We have a strong qualitative signal and a weak quantitative one. We can't yet say, campaign-to-campaign, how much of uptake variation is explained by religious-leader engagement vs. the technical content vs. the demographics of the target area. A more structured analysis is in scope for the 2026 RCT.

SourceCLP Stage 2 Report · Feb 2026 — seven LLO debrief interviews, Nigeria vaccine campaign.
Learning 03 / Limits

We documented our failure in CAR. Publishing it is part of the practice.

The question we had

Connect's rapid partner activation model — remote EOI, Trial Run, pay-per-delivery — had worked across several stable contexts. The operating assumption was that the model would extend to any geography with mobile connectivity and a functioning LLO ecosystem.

The Central African Republic was the first fragile-context test of that assumption.

What we tried

Two CAR-based LLOs were contracted to deliver a CHC campaign. Standard Trial Run structure. Onboarding handled remotely. Tooling, supervision, and payment infrastructure identical to what we used in Nigeria and Kenya.

What we found

Both contracted LLOs dropped out. The causes included staffing challenges — key team members unable to travel or stay engaged in the active campaign window — and connectivity problems that made the app-based workflow unreliable in parts of the operational area. Remote coordination, which was sufficient in more stable contexts, was not sufficient here. The quality of the partners was not the issue; the environment and our operating model's fit with it were.

What changed

We've stopped replicating the pure-remote activation approach in similarly fragile contexts. Future work in DRC, Sierra Leone, and CAR will include dedicated in-country presence — either through a Dimagi field staff posting or a local partnership that performs the same function. The Trial Run model remains sector-agnostic, but it is not geography-agnostic.

What's still open

We are still working out the threshold. Which contexts can run fully remote? Which need light-touch in-country support? Which need a dedicated field team? The honest answer right now is: we don't know, and we'd rather say that than invent a tidy framework that would be wrong.

This insight is in a public marketing page because publishing what didn't work is part of the operating discipline. Funders and partners have a right to see the pattern, not a sanitized version of it.

SourceFounders Pledge Final Report · Feb 2026 — CAR campaign retrospective.
