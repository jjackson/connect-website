Memo / Programs / ECD

Program · Early Childhood Development · Malawi · Nigeria · Mozambique · Founders Pledge

Caregivers' ECD attitudes were already correct. The gap was knowledge and behavior.

The Connect ECD pilot in Malawi was designed to shift attitudes about responsive caregiving and developmental support. We measured the baseline. The attitudes were already there. What needed work was knowledge of timing and observed parenting behavior. We rebuilt the dosage in response: 3 visits became 8–10 across 12 months. This page is the working memo on what the pilot moved, what it didn't, and what the next iteration is testing.

Learning 1 of 3 · the floor effect

The attitude survey saturated at baseline. We moved knowledge and behavior, not beliefs.

The first version of Connect ECD was structured around attitude change. The pilot curriculum, the visit script, the supervisor coaching prompts — all of them assumed that the gap between current and desired caregiving behavior was an attitudinal gap. Caregivers who did not believe responsive caregiving mattered would, with sufficient sensitization, come to believe it, and the behavior would follow.

Then we ran the baseline survey across 50 caregivers in the Malawi pilot panel1. Nearly all caregivers already held positive ECD attitudes at baseline. They agreed that talk-and-play supports brain development. They endorsed responsive caregiving. They affirmed the importance of the first 1,000 days. The attitude scores were high before the intervention started and high after the intervention finished. The instrument was saturated.

Where we did move the needle, in the same Malawi pilot endline1: knowledge of the timing of rapid child development rose by 33 percentage points; knowledge of ways to support mental development rose 41 points; observed teaching behavior rose 21 points; observed encouragement-of-autonomy rose 21 points but reached only 47% at endline. The pattern is consistent and structural. Attitudes were a ceiling, not a floor. Knowledge moved sharply when given. Behavior moved more slowly and unevenly because behavior is not a quiz answer.

The corollary is that ECD interventions designed primarily to shift attitudes are aimed at the wrong dependent variable in this population. The caregivers in our pilot did not need to be convinced that ECD matters. They needed practical sequencing of milestones (knowledge), specific routines for talk-and-play (knowledge), and sustained practice with feedback to make those routines habitual in their household (behavior).

We rebuilt the program around that finding. The original 3-visit dosage is being expanded to 8–10 visits across 12 months — not to give caregivers more time to be convinced (they are already convinced), but because behavior change takes sustained practice. The extra visits are observed practice with feedback, not more talk. The Learn app for ECD now opens with a frank acknowledgement of the floor effect — "you already know this matters" — and routes the curriculum into milestone sequencing.

Learning 2 of 3 · FLW competence

FLWs who passed the digital test still struggled in homes. We layered an AI coach.

The same finding appeared on the FLW side of the program, in a different shape. Connect's first ECD training pipeline was a self-paced Learn app and an in-app multiple-choice knowledge test with a 70% pass-mark. Workers who passed the test were released to deliver. We expected the test to be a sufficient release gate.

It wasn't. FLWs who aced the digital test still struggled with the parts of an ECD visit that are not test-shaped — handling a skeptical caregiver, recognizing subtle developmental delays, practicing motivational interviewing during a 30-minute visit while the child is present. The supervised-observation visit, the last step in our pipeline, kept catching workers the test had passed.

What we changed is the layered model described on the Learn page. The test is still there but is now a midpoint, not a release gate. After the test, FLWs interact daily with an AI coach bot built on Dimagi's Open Chat Studio2. The coach runs scenario-based practice: a skeptical caregiver, a child showing late motor milestones, a household with multiple competing children. It runs motivational-interviewing drills. It identifies knowledge gaps through conversation rather than four-option recall. After the AI coach, FLWs do peer practice and one supervised observation visit rated by an LLO supervisor.

Across the layered pipeline, validated through ECD and other Connect programs3: 85% of FLWs progress from training to service delivery. 88% score above 70% on first observed visits. 77% pass the supervised observation. The 23% who do not pass receive coaching, not termination — and the AI coach captures the pattern of where they failed for the next iteration of the Learn content.

What we have not yet decomposed: how much of the 77% supervised-observation pass rate is contributed by the AI coach versus the peer-practice stage versus the Learn content itself. The pipeline is layered, the validation is end-to-end, and we cannot yet tell a funder "the AI coach contributes X% of the lift" — only that the pipeline as a system produces the numbers above.

Learning 3 of 3 · the residual

Encouragement of autonomy is still 47% at endline. We don't know yet whether 8–10 visits will move it.

The Malawi pilot moved every measured sub-domain. It moved encouragement of autonomy least. At endline, observed encouragement of autonomy reached 47% — up from baseline, but still the lowest score of any sub-domain in the panel1. The number is the residual the next iteration of ECD has to address.

"Encouragement of autonomy" in this context is the observed behavior of letting a young child attempt a task — feeding, dressing, walking to a near object — with caregiver scaffolding rather than caregiver substitution. Caregivers in the pilot were observed doing this 47% of the time at endline. In the other observed-behavior sub-domains, endline scores were higher. Teaching behavior reached endline scores in the upper range of the instrument; encouragement of autonomy did not.

The hypothesis behind the dosage rebuild — 3 visits to 8–10 across 12 months — is that this particular sub-domain is sustained-practice-limited rather than knowledge-limited. Caregivers know that letting a child attempt is good. They have a habit of substituting because substitution is faster, especially in time-pressured households. Habit replacement requires repeated, observed practice with feedback over months, not weeks. The 8–10-visit schedule is designed to provide that.

What we do not yet know is whether the dosage rebuild is sufficient. The honest possibility is that encouragement of autonomy is not sustained-practice-limited but design-limited — that the activity is asking caregivers to make a trade-off (slower task completion for developmental benefit) that is harder to make in their daily reality than other sub-domains. If 8–10 visits do not move the 47% number meaningfully, the next iteration is to redesign the ECD intervention around the constraint, not to push harder on the existing form.

The pilot is also expanding from Malawi (76 FLWs, 9,510+ visits) into Nigeria and Mozambique1. The 47%-at-endline finding from a 50-caregiver Malawian panel does not automatically generalize; whether the attitude-floor and behavior-residual pattern holds in those contexts is a question we will answer with the next round of baseline/endline panels. We will publish the cross-country comparison whether or not it is flattering.

What we don't know yet

  1. The 47% endline on encouragement-of-autonomy is the hardest sub-domain. Whether 8–10 visits will move it is open.
  2. The Malawi pilot is N=50 caregivers. Whether the attitude-already-correct finding holds in Mozambique and Nigeria contexts is open.
  3. Whether AI coach contribution to the 77% pass rate generalizes outside ECD — we have not run the controlled comparison.
