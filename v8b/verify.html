III. Verify · Platform essay · April 2026

Four layers of proof.
A platform that paid
people to defeat it.

Every verified visit passes biometrics, GPS, photo, and an algorithmic fraud-detection model that we stress-tested by paying experienced frontline workers to beat it. This essay covers the adversarial test, microplanning for equitable coverage, and the guardrails-versus-hard-stops distinction that keeps the data honest.

Scoped: Platform (fraud detection, guardrails framework). CHC-scoped numbers named as such. · Source: FP Final Report, Feb 2026; KMC Conceptual Model, March 2026
Insight 03 · Adversarial fraud testing · Platform

We paid people to defeat our fraud detection. Then we watched them fail.

◆ Scope · Platform

Every platform claims fraud detection. Most do not test theirs adversarially. We did — and we designed the test so that "cheating worked" would have been a finding we'd have had to publish. Before the test, Connect's verification story was biometric match at the door, GPS fix on the visit, photo of the commodity or client — three independent signals, difficult to fake all three simultaneously, probably sufficient. "Probably sufficient" is the phrase you use when you haven't actually tried to break the system.

The question we wanted to answer was whether we needed a fourth layer that did not depend on GPS, timestamps, or photos at all — a statistical layer that could catch fabrication even if the other three were gamed. To know whether that layer would work, we had to build fakers good enough to push back on it.

The setup. We paid experienced frontline workers to submit 100 fake visit records each. We ran three rounds. After each round we coached the fakers on what had given them away, improving their ability to fabricate plausible data. We offered bonuses to the top 25% most convincing fabricators to make sure they took it seriously. This is the kind of adversarial testing that most organizations only do when a regulator makes them.

The finding. Using three data elements — age, gender, and MUAC measurements — a Random Forest model achieved an AUC of 0.91 with a standard error of 0.09. Only 2.5% of real workers received fraud-risk scores as high as the lowest-scoring fabricator. Out of 33 adversarial fakers across the three rounds, we detected them with near-perfect discrimination using data patterns alone — before GPS and photo verification even kicked in.

What this means in production is that Connect's verification is empirically validated rather than assumed. Because verification has four independent layers — biometric, GPS, photo, algorithmic — a fabricated visit has to beat all four simultaneously. The failure modes of the four layers are different, which is the point. They do not correlate. Stacking them multiplies difficulty rather than adding it. Faking the biometric, forging a plausible GPS trace, producing a photo that survives image analysis, and producing MUAC measurements that do not trip the statistical model — across a campaign of thousands of visits — is a problem we have not observed in the wild.

Insight 02 · Equitable coverage · CHC

Workers naturally cluster. Microplans force them outward.

◆ Scope · CHC (Founders Pledge evaluation)

Verify is not only about whether a visit happened. It is about which visits happened. A platform can verify 100% of a cherry-picked sample and still fail at equity. Microplanning is how Connect gets coverage that is not convenience-sampled — and the finding below is specifically from the CHC program, where the evaluation was run.

Early CHC campaigns gave FLWs a defined operational area and a target number of visits. The assumption was that workers, incentivized per verified visit, would spread evenly across the area to maximize their yield. Standard per-visit economics should produce uniform coverage.

What workers actually did was cherry-pick. Given a neighborhood, they averaged 0.4 visits per estimated eligible child — visiting accessible households and skipping the harder ones, whether harder meant farther from the road, complicated language, or households known to resist. The populations missed were systematically the ones with lower baseline health coverage. We were convenience-sampling at the exact moment equity was the goal. This is not an unusual failure mode in per-visit-paid systems; it is the default failure mode.

The fix was to overlay a microplan layer on the operational area. Before a campaign we generate a grid using Google Open Buildings, OpenStreetMap, and Microsoft building-footprint data. Grid cells containing at least one building become Delivery Units. DUs group into Service Areas sized for one to two weeks of work. A worker cannot move to the next Service Area until they have closed every DU in the current one — either by completing visits or by confirming no eligible children are present at that DU.

In the CHC program, microplanning produced 1.4 visits per estimated child in microplanned cells versus 0.4 without — a 3.5× increase in coverage thoroughness. Population coverage rose from 82-84% to 94%. The per-visit payment did not change. What changed was that progress through the grid became the gate, not visit count alone.

Where the microplan didn't work. Dispersed settlement patterns broke the grid. Workers walked long distances to DUs that contained no actual buildings. Some DUs fell on inaccessible land — factory grounds, prison grounds, private estates. One campaign removed microplan execution entirely due to partner constraints. The grid is powerful where density supports it; the platform supports falling back to area-based assignment where it does not. We do not claim microplanning is a universal answer. We claim it is a powerful default for contexts where building density and data coverage align — and CHC is where we measured its impact.

Insight 10 · Design philosophy · Platform

Guardrails are not hard stops.

◆ Scope · Platform framework (KMC as live example)

The Verify surface has to decide what counts as a valid visit. The intuitive design is to make every quality target a blocker: miss the target, reject the visit, withhold payment. It is clean, punitive, and legible to funders. It is also the design that produces the worst data.

Platforms that punish missed targets do not get honest data. If first visit must be within 3 days of discharge for payment to clear, FLWs learn to falsify visit dates to hit the window. We would not know they were late. We would not know which LLOs were running into which kinds of delays. We would have the appearance of good performance and we would lose the signal we need to actually improve the program.

The distinction we drew is between payment criteria — hard, must be met to be paid — and guardrails — audited, trigger performance conversations and LLO coaching, but do not block payment. The distinction is a platform configuration framework; every program on Connect inherits it and configures its own set.

The live example is in the KMC program. KMC's payment criterion is 4 mandatory home visits per small-and-vulnerable newborn. KMC's key guardrail is "first visit within 3 days of discharge." We audit the guardrail. We do not enforce it. Current performance on the 3-day guardrail is 50% against a 70% target. We know it is 50% precisely because we are not punishing misses — FLWs and LLOs report accurately. The 20-point gap is driven by three causes we are actively working on: facility-to-FLW discharge-notification lag, LLO scheduling bottlenecks, and FLW availability constraints.

The gain from keeping the guardrail soft is the real data to improve the program. We see every 3-day miss as it happens. We can diagnose why. We can fix the cause through coaching, scheduling support, and discharge-notification integration. A hard-stop design would have erased the signal. The distinction sounds like a policy nuance; it is actually the thing that lets us learn.
