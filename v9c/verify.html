Every service confirmed. Every dollar earned.

I.
The method

Three layers. One closed loop.

Layer 01

Biometric & identity.

Unique identification confirms real people received real services. PersonalID prevents duplicates, ghost beneficiaries, re-submission under different names.

Layer 02

GPS & coverage.

Geolocation confirms where a visit happened. Microplan execution pushes workers door-to-door, not convenience sampling. A Service Area can't be closed until every Delivery Unit in it is addressed.

Layer 03

Algorithmic detection.

AI-powered analysis across submissions flags anomalies. Independent of GPS and photo layers — a fabricated visit has to beat all four to pass.

II.
Chapter one · CHC microplanning · 2024–26

Workers naturally cluster. Microplans force them outward.

Give FLWs a target area and a visit quota. They'll figure it out. That was the assumption. In practice, workers cherry-pick. Given a neighbourhood, they average 0.4 visits per estimated eligible child — visiting accessible households and skipping the harder ones. The populations missed were systematically those with lowest baseline coverage.

"Convenience sampling at the exact moment equity was the goal."

Microplanning closes this. Before a campaign, Connect overlays a grid onto the operational area using Google Open Buildings + OpenStreetMap + Microsoft data. Grid cells with buildings become Delivery Units. DUs group into Service Areas sized for 1–2 weeks of work. A worker cannot move to the next Service Area until every DU in the current one is closed — by completed visits or by confirming no eligible children are present.

The result: 1.4 visits per estimated child in microplanned cells vs 0.4 without. A 3.5× increase in thoroughness. Population coverage jumped from 82–84% to 94%.

Where it didn't work: dispersed settlement patterns (workers walking long distances to DUs with no actual buildings); inaccessible land (factory grounds, prison grounds). One campaign removed microplan execution entirely due to partner constraints.

What we changed

Microplanning is now default for CHC campaigns. It is how verification drives equitable coverage — not just a monitoring tool. The populations funders care most about reaching are exactly the ones most likely to be skipped without a grid.

III.
Chapter two · Adversarial testing · 3 rounds · 33 fabricators

We paid people to defeat our fraud detection. Then we watched them fail.

Our verification stack — biometrics, GPS, photos — handles fraud risk. Good enough. That was the assumption. But what if a motivated insider tried to fake data? Could statistical patterns alone — no GPS, no timestamps, no photos — tell us?

We paid experienced FLWs to submit batches of 100 fake visit records each. We ran three rounds. After each round, we coached the fakers on what gave them away. We offered bonuses to the top 25% most convincing fabricators. The kind of adversarial testing most organisations only do when a regulator makes them.

"Using three data elements alone, a Random Forest model achieved AUC 0.91 ± 0.09 — before GPS and photo verification even fired."

Only 2.5% of real workers received fraud risk scores higher than the lowest-scoring fabricator. Near-perfect discrimination using data patterns alone.

Fraud detection isn't a promise. It is an empirically validated layer. And because verification has four independent layers (biometric, GPS, photo, algorithmic), a fabricated visit has to beat all four to pass.

IV.
Chapter three · KMC · Design philosophy

Guardrails are not hard stops. This is a design philosophy.

When a FLW misses a target — first visit within 3 days of discharge, say — the simple response is to reject the visit or withhold payment. This is what most platforms do. We don't.

We distinguish payment criteria (hard — must be met to be paid) from guardrails (soft — trigger audits and performance conversations, but don't block payment). The KMC program has 4 mandatory home visits per SVN as a payment criterion. But the guardrail "first visit within 3 days of discharge" is audited, not enforced. Current performance on that guardrail is 50% against a 70% target. We're working on it — through LLO coaching, scheduling support, and discharge notification integration — not through punishment.

"Platforms that punish missed guardrails don't get honest data."

FLWs would learn to game the system — falsify visit dates to hit the 3-day window, for example. A platform that separates audits from payment gets the real data it needs to improve.

The deeper point: verification exists to serve the program, not to discipline workers. We built the separation so we can keep learning.

What we changed

Payment criteria and guardrails are distinct fields on every program spec. Audits happen monthly; performance conversations happen in person with the LLO. Mobile-money payouts never block for a guardrail miss — only for a payment-criterion miss.
