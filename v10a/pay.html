PILLAR 01

Performance-based payment

Workers paid per verified service. KMC: ~$60 per completed case. CHC: $0.78–$1.30 per verified visit depending on commodity bundle. The value and the action are coupled.

PILLAR 02

Direct mobile money

Payments flow to workers' mobile money accounts. No intermediaries. No skimming. No cash logistics. Earnings visible in the Connect app in real time.

PILLAR 03

End-to-end transparency

From funder to frontline, every dollar is tracked. Workers see earnings. LLOs see disbursements. Funders see cost-per-verified-outcome. Same data, every stakeholder.

Learning 01 · Economics of scale · CHC numbers, platform mechanism

Most organizations get less lean as they scale. Connect CHC got leaner.

Every other Dimagi program follows the standard pattern: more partners, more supervision, more ops burden, more cost per delivered outcome. Connect CHC inverted it during the Founders Pledge grant period — and understanding why took three structural observations stacked on top of each other. Each on its own doesn't flip the orthodoxy. Stacked, they produce a different shape.

The starting cost was $2.20 per verified CHC visit, early in the grant. We budgeted assuming it would stay roughly flat as we scaled — flat-as-program-grew was already a win against the conventional pattern of cost creep. By the end of the 14-month grant, cost per verified CHC visit had fallen to $1.70 — a 22% reduction1, while visit volume grew toward 880,000+. Dimagi's team size during the same period was flat.

The headline number masks bundle-level asymmetry that's worth naming. In Nigeria (with ORS/Zinc co-packs included in the commodity bundle), the average settled at $1.30/visit across 14 LLO contracts. Outside Nigeria (VAS + deworming only, no ORS), it averaged $0.78/visit across 17 contracts1. One Sierra Leone LLO delivered ORS/Zinc for $1.00/visit. Setup fees during the grant averaged $0.40/visit and are projected to fall toward ~$0.10/visit at larger campaign scale, accelerating the trend further.

In CHC, scale created cost reduction instead of cost creep. The architecture is what produces that shape — three structural reasons stacked.

Three structural reasons this works in CHC. (1) LLOs operate with inherently low overhead — they're local, lean, and not carrying the fixed costs of a Dimagi-style central organization. (2) Performance-based payment forces costs to track actual delivery, not organizational size; an LLO that hires three extra staff without delivering three staff worth of visits gets paid the same as one that didn't. (3) Digital technology is integrated into every step — worker guidance in CommCare, automated verification, mobile money disbursement — so adding campaign volume compounds efficiency instead of compounding coordination overhead.

Most development programs have a structural ceiling on cost-effectiveness. The more they scale, the more ops burden grows, until marginal cost-per-outcome stabilizes at a level that's hard to reduce further. Connect CHC shows what the other architecture looks like in practice — one of the few measured examples we can point to where the inversion is not just a projection but a measured result. Whether the same year-over-year curve holds in non-CHC programs is an empirical question we measure per program, never one we extrapolate.

Learning 02 · The displacement question · CHC, GiveWell survey in Nigeria

Campaign work in Nigeria isn't a disruption of routine care — it is the routine care.

A common funder worry: paying FLWs for campaign visits will crowd out their unpaid routine health work. We took the worry seriously and went looking for evidence. The evidence was shaped by a Western assumption we didn't know we were making — and it's worth tracing the finding because it changes how every new funder conversation about CHC starts.

The concern, raised by multiple funders, is that paid and volunteer work compete for the same time. If we pay FLWs to do CHC visits, won't they neglect their regular community health volunteer (CHV) work? Zero-sum on time. Linear on incentive. That model assumes a particular structure of the underlying CHV system.

GiveWell funded a displacement survey: 20 FLWs, 2 LLO staff, 2 local health representatives, across Gombe and Sokoto states in Nigeria. Interviewed independently. Objective was to measure whether and how much routine work had been displaced by CHC campaign time1.

In the Nigerian CHV system, campaigns are not a disruption — they are the system. The "crowd-out" concern was built on an assumption that didn't apply.

What the data showed: campaign work is normal and expected in Nigeria's CHV system. CHVs routinely do campaigns — it's their main income source, not a departure from their role. When CHC FLWs reduced volunteer hours during campaign periods, other volunteers at the facility covered, as they typically do for any campaign. In Gombe specifically, FLWs maintained volunteer hours by shifting their schedules within Connect's flexible 7am-7pm window. Coverage held because that's how the system works, not despite the campaign payment.

The deeper finding was about the question itself. The displacement concern was built on Western assumptions about what community health volunteers do that don't apply in the Nigerian context. In the actual system, campaigns are not a disruption — they are the system. FLWs reported substantial income increases. Most said CHC had become a primary income source. Reported secondary effects were overwhelmingly positive: technology skills (CommCare app, Google Maps), improved communication, increased confidence.

We now present this finding to every new funder raising the displacement question for CHC. The context matters — and the context here is that we were worried about something the local system had already solved. Whether the same finding holds in CHV systems with different structural shapes is the careful framing: we expect it to in similar settings, and we'd re-survey before claiming it elsewhere.

Sources:

  1. GiveWell FLW Displacement Survey (Oct 2025) — 24 interviews across Gombe and Sokoto states, Nigeria. Insight 5 in the public insights log.
