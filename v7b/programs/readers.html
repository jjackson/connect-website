Readers

Near-vision screening and reading glasses distribution for adults 35+ experiencing presbyopia. 15,000 pairs donated by RestoringVision. Northeast Nigeria, via eHA and C3HD. This is the platform applied to a new sector — and the first section of this page honestly lays out what we expect to learn rather than what we already know.

The challenge

Presbyopia — age-related near-vision loss — affects billions globally. In underserved communities, the lack of access to affordable reading glasses limits productivity, education, and full participation in economic activities that require near vision: reading, sewing, agriculture, market work. For many recipients of a program like this one, it is their first pair of corrective lenses.

Traditional vision care programs typically require people to travel to clinics. Door-to-door distribution with performance-based payment inverts that model: FLWs go to the communities that need help most, and their compensation depends on verified delivery. Connect's job is to make the delivery architecture work — not to invent new clinical practice.

How it works

The familiar four steps, a new intervention content.

01 / Learn

Standardised screening training

FLWs complete mobile-guided training on near-vision screening technique, lens-strength determination, and fitting protocol. Self-paced, offline-capable, with in-app knowledge testing before clearance to deliver.

02 / Deliver

Door-to-door distribution

FLWs conduct household visits in assigned areas, screen adults 35+, fit glasses of the appropriate strength, and complete the distribution on-site. No clinic visit. No follow-up logistics.

03 / Verify

GPS + biometric verification

Each distribution is verified through the standard Connect four-layer stack — biometric identity, GPS, photo where appropriate, and algorithmic pattern detection. The verification stack is unchanged from the child-health programs.

04 / Pay

Mobile money per distribution

FLWs receive mobile money payments for each verified screening and distribution. LLOs manage the supply chain — receiving glasses from RestoringVision, allocating to FLWs, tracking inventory.

Learning 01 · What we expect to learn

Does the CHC Trial Run model port cleanly to a vision-care distribution campaign? We think yes. We're about to find out.

Connect's prior programs — CHC, KMC, ECD — share a common intervention grammar. Each is a structured sequence of FLW-delivered activities in a household, each captured by the same Deliver app architecture, each verified by the same four-layer stack, each paid per verified unit. Readers is the first Connect program in the vision-care sector, and the first where the "service" being delivered is a physical product — reading glasses — distributed in a single visitProgram design note · Readers EOI · 2025Unlike CHC or ECD where the service is counselling, screening, or commodity administration (VAS, deworming), a Readers distribution is a one-visit transaction that includes screening + product fit + handoff. The follow-up logistics that other programs require don't exist for Readers. rather than a series of follow-up encounters.

The hypotheses we are testing with this program are structural ones. First: does the Trial Run LLO-activation model work when the activation question is "can this LLO manage a glasses supply chain" rather than "can this LLO manage a commodity-free child-health campaign"? Second: does the four-layer verification stack produce useful signal on a single-visit distribution, where the "repeat visit" check that the stack uses elsewhere doesn't apply? Third: does the microplanning coverage mechanism need adaptation for a population-35-plus target, given that building-footprint data doesn't tell us who lives where by age?

We're running this program with two LLOs finalised via EOI: eHA and C3HDConnect Readers program page · April 2026eHA (eHealth Africa) and C3HD were selected from an Expression of Interest process. Both have prior presence in Northeast Nigeria; neither has prior Readers-specific experience. This is intentional — the question of sector portability is the one we want the program to answer., both in Northeast Nigeria. Campaign dates are pending contract signing as of April 2026. We will publish what we learn — on each of the three hypotheses above — in the same public form we've used for CHC and ECD. If the answer to any of them is "no," that will be in the report.

Learning 02 · Why door-to-door matters

Donated glasses are cheap. Getting them to rural adults over 35 is not — unless you invert the model.

Glasses are donated at scale by RestoringVisionRestoringVision partnershipRestoringVision is a nonprofit that donates ready-to-use reading glasses globally. 15,000 pairs committed to this program. LLOs receive the glasses, manage the local supply chain (inventory tracking, allocation to FLWs), and bear no per-unit product cost.. LLOs manage the supply chain — receiving glasses, allocating to FLWs, tracking inventory. The performance-based payment model means operational costs scale with actual delivery, not with organisational overhead. Unit economics are therefore structurally favourable before the first pair is distributed.

The constraint is reach, not supply. Traditional vision care programs rely on clinic-based distribution: individuals travel to a central location to be screened and fitted. In rural or underserved contexts, the travel cost — time, transport, opportunity cost — is often prohibitive for the adults most likely to need presbyopia correction. A 55-year-old farmer who needs +2.0 reading glasses will not reliably travel for them. The glasses sit in inventory.

Door-to-door distribution with performance-based payment inverts this pattern. FLWs go to the communities that need help most. Their compensation depends on verified delivery — so the incentive structure rewards reach, not effort. A successful Readers FLW is one who reaches households a clinic-based distribution would never touch. For many recipients, this will be their first pair of corrective lenses — immediately restoring their ability to read, work with small objects, and participate in economic activities that require near visionPresbyopia intervention literatureThere is a substantial body of evidence on the economic and livelihood impact of near-vision correction in underserved populations. Readers' clinical theory of change is well-established; what Connect is testing is the delivery architecture, not the intervention itself..

The composite economics — donated product, LLO-managed supply chain, FLW-delivered door-to-door distribution, pay-per-verified-distribution — mean the Readers unit cost is dominated by FLW compensation and LLO coordination, not by procurement. The program's launch scale is 15,000 glasses; the architecture is built to handle considerably more if the first campaign validates the structural hypotheses.
