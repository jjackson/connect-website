Memo / Programs / Readers

Program · Vision Care · Nigeria · RestoringVision · 15,000 pairs target

15,000 reading-glasses pairs through Connect's verified-payment rail. A new domain on the same platform.

The Readers program is the platform applied to a non-clinical commodity. RestoringVision supplies the inventory; Connect runs the FLW recruitment, training, screening, distribution, and verified payment. The structure is the same — Trial Run, microplanning where applicable, photo and biometric verification, performance-based pay — applied to a domain Connect has not previously served. There is little measurement to publish yet. This page is the working memo on the design and the open questions before the first cohort runs.

Learning 1 of 2 · platform fit

Reading glasses are a verified-distribution problem. Connect is a verified-distribution platform.

Presbyopia — the loss of close-vision sharpness that begins for most adults around 35–45 — is among the most cost-effective interventions in global health by quality-adjusted-life-year per dollar. A pair of reading glasses costs single-digit dollars at supplier scale. The intervention is binary in clinical effect: a presbyopic adult who can read again can return to work, manage household finances, supervise their children's schoolwork. The barrier is not the inventory. The barrier is finding the right adults and verifying that the inventory reached them.

That is a Connect problem shape. The Connect platform was built around verifying that an FLW reached the right person and that the right intervention was delivered. The CHC adversarial-fraud test, the photo-verification layer, the biometric FLW identity, the GPS check at submission — all of these were calibrated for child-health visits, but the verification mechanics generalize. A reading-glasses distribution visit produces a verifiable record: an adult of presbyopic age, a near-vision screening result, a photo of the glasses being fitted, a GPS location. The same four verification layers apply.

The RestoringVision partnership supplies 15,000 reading-glasses pairs and the supplier-side logistics. Connect handles FLW recruitment via the LLO model, training (a Learn module on near-vision screening with a tumbling-E chart), the visit verification stack, and the verified-payment rail. The two LLO partners (eHA and C3HD) have been finalized; contract signing is the gating step before the first cohort. Once contracts are signed, Trial Run terms apply — same $3,000 / 3,000-visits structure, calibrated to glasses-pair distribution rather than child visits.

The design choice that matters is door-to-door distribution. Vision-screening models that depend on adults walking into a clinic have a recruitment ceiling determined by local health-seeking behavior. The Readers program inverts that: FLWs with the screening tool and the inventory walk to the household. The cost per verified pair is structurally lower than clinic-based distribution and the reach into populations that do not currently engage with formal health services is structurally higher. We will know how much higher when the first cohort runs.

Learning 2 of 2 · what we'll learn

The first cohort will tell us three things we do not yet know.

The Readers program is the most honestly pre-evidence program in this catalog. There is no pilot. There is no Trial Run yet. The Trial Run terms apply to the first cohort, by design — the model is that the first cohort is the Trial Run. So this section is not a list of measurements; it is a list of three things we expect the first cohort to answer.

First, whether the screening protocol holds quality consistency across novice FLWs trained only digitally. The Connect Learn pipeline produces FLWs validated to deliver child-health visits. Whether the same pipeline produces FLWs who can administer a near-vision screen accurately enough to dispense the right diopter strength is the open question. The Learn module for Readers is built on a tumbling-E near-vision chart and a brief diopter-selection algorithm. Pass-mark on the in-app test is calibrated higher than for CHC because dispensing the wrong strength is a more direct individual-level harm than missing a CHC visit. Whether that pass-mark is set right is what the supervised-observation visits in the first cohort will tell us.

Second, whether door-to-door distribution finds the right adults — those of presbyopic age, with the kind of near-vision impairment a reader can correct — at sufficient rates without targeting infrastructure. The CHC microplanning machinery uses building data to bound the operational area; for Readers, we have not yet decided whether to use the same machinery or a coarser household-sampling rule. The first cohort will test a low-instrumentation version. If yield-per-visit is sufficient, the model stays low-instrumentation. If it is not, microplanning gets ported.

Third, whether the verified-pair cost structure converges on a number competitive with clinic-based distribution. The supplier-side cost (the glasses themselves) is fixed. The variable cost is FLW time per verified pair, the LLO overhead, and the Connect platform fee. Whether that converges to a low-single-digit-dollar all-in cost — the level at which the Readers program is procurement-comparable to alternative vision-care delivery — is empirical and we will publish whatever number the first cohort produces.

The reason this section is short is that there is nothing dishonest about saying we don't know yet. The Readers program is a deliberate test of whether the Connect platform generalizes outside community health. If it does, the next domain after this one will look easier. If it doesn't, we will publish the failure and limit the platform's claimed range to the domains where it has been measured.

The three open questions

Screening accuracy from digitally-trained FLWs. Yield from door-to-door without microplanning. Cost-per-verified-pair convergence.

What we don't know yet

  1. LLOs finalized (eHA, C3HD); contract signing pending. Field-performance data won't exist until the first cohort runs.
  2. Whether the screening protocol holds quality consistency across novice FLWs trained only digitally — first cohort will tell.
  3. Whether door-to-door distribution finds the right adults (35+, presbyopic) at sufficient rates without targeting infrastructure.
