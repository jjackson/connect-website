Dispatch 1 of 2 · The platform applied

The platform applied to a new domain. Verified-payment for vision care.

Connect Readers is the platform applied to a new domain: door-to-door distribution of low-cost reading glasses for adults with uncorrected presbyopia. The funder is RestoringVision, a partner with deep operational experience in low-income vision care. The target is fifteen thousand pairs delivered through Connect's verified-payment model, with two LLOs finalized — eHA and C3HD — and contract signing pending. Field-performance data does not yet exist, because the cohort has not started running. This dispatch is therefore deliberately short and deliberately scoped: it describes what the program is being asked to do, the design-time decisions we have made, and the open questions we know we are entering with.

The reason Readers is in the catalog at all, at this stage, is that the design questions are interesting. Door-to-door distribution of reading glasses asks the platform to do something it has not done before: targeting on a demographic feature (adults thirty-five and older, presbyopic) rather than a household composition (children under five, pregnant women, post-discharge newborns). The verification stack is similar to CHC — biometric, GPS, photo, algorithmic — but the algorithmic layer needs to be retrained on a different signal set. We do not yet know how well the existing model architecture will generalise, and we will publish what we learn when the data arrives.

A scope discipline note before this page goes any further. The numbers on every other program dispatch in this catalog are measurements. The numbers on this page are targets, contracted intentions, and design choices. We mark each one. When the first cohort closes, we will rewrite this dispatch with the same shape as CHC, KMC, and ECD: a timeline of what we tried, what we found, what we changed, and what is still open.

Dispatch 2 of 2 · What we'll measure first

The first cohort answers three questions. We are honest about which one we expect to fail.

Three questions are under design as the first cohort begins. Each one is a piece of the platform we have not yet stress-tested in adult vision care, and each one will get a published answer when the data arrives. The first is whether the existing screening protocol holds quality consistency across novice FLWs trained only digitally. CHC FLWs and KMC FLWs are layered through the AI-coach pipeline described on the Learn dispatch, and the protocols they execute have been refined over years. The Readers screening protocol — near-vision testing, prescription matching to a small range of reading-glasses powers — is shorter and more constrained, but the population is different and the stakes of misclassification are different. We will measure inter-FLW concordance early.

The second is whether door-to-door distribution finds the right adults at sufficient rates without targeting infrastructure. The CHC microplanning layer works because the target population (children under five) is a known fraction of the household composition, and the building data is enough to estimate it. Adults thirty-five and over with presbyopia are not estimable from building data. We will run the first weeks with a wider geographic footprint and measure pair-per-household-visited rates; if the rate is too low, we will redesign towards facility-anchored distribution rather than door-to-door, which is the operational fallback already on the table.

The third is the verification question. The algorithmic layer in CHC — Random Forest on age, gender, MUAC — has no obvious analogue for verifying that a pair of reading glasses was distributed and fitted to a person who needed them. The candidate replacement is a photo of the recipient with the prescribed glasses on, plus the biometric confirmation that the person is who the FLW says they are, plus the location confirmation that the visit happened where it was supposed to. Whether that three-layer stack is sufficient to deter fabrication at the same level as the CHC four-layer stack is an open question. We expect to need at least one round of adversarial testing on Readers before we publish a verification AUC for it.

What we'll measure first

  1. Inter-FLW screening concordance.
  2. Pair-per-household-visited rate.
  3. Verification AUC under adversarial test.

Operational fallback

Facility-anchored distribution rather than door-to-door, if pair-per-household rates are too low.
