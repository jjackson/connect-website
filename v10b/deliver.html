Learning 1 of 3 · Trial Run

How procurement screening became a $3,000 pilot.

Conventional procurement says vet partners thoroughly before contracting. We tried it. Past reputation, team size, and organizational maturity did not predict whether an LLO would deliver. So we redesigned the funnel to pay small for real work.

Phase 1 — Early 2024 The conventional procurement screen Pre-qualify on past reputation, team size, organizational maturity, financial statements. Standard funder-grade due diligence.

Connect started where most platforms start: pre-qualification of partners. Standard funder-grade due-diligence checklists. Past CHV-campaign experience. Audited financials. References from other implementers. Team size and field presence. The implicit theory was that an LLO that had run campaigns before would run our campaigns. The implicit theory was clean and the screening was repeatable. It also produced biased screens — partners we knew were systematically advantaged over partners we did not — and the biases tracked geography more than capability.

Phase 2 — 2024 Reputation didn't predict performance Past reputation, team size, organizational maturity — none of it reliably predicted whether an LLO would actually deliver verified visits at the contracted rate.

The first cohort came in. We watched what happened. Past reputation, team size, organizational maturity — none of it reliably predicted whether an LLO would actually deliver verified visits at the contracted rate. Some highly-rated organizations underperformed. Some smaller organizations with thinner paperwork delivered on time and at quality. The signal we had paid for in the diligence stage was not the signal that mattered in execution. We could either keep refining the diligence or we could replace it.

The signal we paid for in diligence was not the signal that mattered in execution.
Phase 3 — Late 2024 The Trial Run replaces procurement screening $3,000 setup fee for 3,000 verified child visits. Real Connect verification stack. Performance-based.

The Trial Run is a small contract — three thousand dollars in setup fee for three thousand verified child visits — that an LLO runs on the actual Connect verification stack a full campaign would use. There is no synthetic pilot signal. The verified visits during a Trial Run are real production data, and the LLO is paid per visit. The setup fee is paid for participation. The conversion is paid for delivery.

We do not run a Trial Run as a courtesy. The thirty-five percent of LLOs that do not pass the Trial Run are paid the setup fee, not the per-visit pay; they are not continued. That is the cost of replacing a screening document with a measurement.

Phase 4 — 2026 (CHC) The published funnel 107 EOIs → 37 Trial Run contracts → 24 Trials → 14 graduated to larger campaigns. 65% conversion. We publish what we drop.

One hundred and seven EOI applications. Thirty-seven contracted under Trial Run terms. Twenty-four advanced to full Trials. Fourteen graduated to larger campaigns. Three additional LLOs went straight to larger campaigns based on prior measured performance from earlier Connect programs. We publish this conversion rate because the alternative — opaque vendor lists and post-hoc rationalisation — is what funders are trying to escape.

→ Sources: Founders Pledge Final Report (Feb 2026), CHC program. The 65% figure is CHC; KMC, ECD, and Readers are still on too small a contract base to publish their own conversion yet.

Learning 2 of 3 · CLP Stage 2 vaccine campaign

5 of 7 LLOs invented religious-leader outreach. We did not teach them.

A short timeline on what locally-led organizations do that a centralized model cannot — and the structural reason Connect tilts capability into the local partner.

Phase 1 — 2025 The CLP Stage 2 vaccine campaign launches 7 LLOs running vaccine-hesitancy outreach inside CHC. We provide the workflow logic, the verification stack, the payment guardrails. We do not provide a "religious-leader engagement module."

The Country-Led Pathways Stage 2 vaccine campaign within CHC launched with seven Locally-Led Organizations running outreach across communities with documented vaccine hesitancy. Connect provided the workflow logic in the Connect app, the verification stack underneath it, and the payment guardrails — all the platform-level pieces. The framing of the conversations, the channel of trust, the local language and idiom, and the relationships with health-facility staff were left to the LLOs because the LLOs are the ones who understand the communities they work in.

Phase 2 — 2025 An outside evaluator went to the field The pattern was not in our platform telemetry. It surfaced because an external evaluator interviewed FLWs and LLO leadership directly.

Of the seven LLOs in the campaign, five to six independently leveraged religious leaders, community leaders, or existing community relationships as part of their vaccine-hesitancy outreach. They did this without prompting from Dimagi, without it being in the Learn modules, and without it being on the supervisor checklist. One FLW countered hesitancy with a personal example: "We were immunized, we have children." Another LLO had FLWs who had been recommended by the community itself, lending immediate credibility before a single conversation about vaccines.

If Dimagi had written a "religious-leader engagement module" and pushed it through the Learn app, it would have been a cartoon of what those LLOs already understood about their own communities.
Phase 3 — 2026 The platform's job is to enable, not centralize Connect controls workflow logic, verification, payment. LLOs control framing, channel, language, relationships.

The split is what makes forty-four LLOs in fourteen months tractable without forty-four Dimagi field offices. It is also why the tooling looks the way it looks: the Connect app is opinionated about what a verified visit is and indifferent about how the visit is initiated. The pattern is locally-grounded knowledge that does not survive being centralized; if we tried to capture and publish it as a "module," we would flatten the very thing that makes it work.

→ Sources: CLP Stage 2 Report (Feb 2026), CHC vaccine campaign — 7 LLOs evaluated through field interviews and outcome data.

Learning 3 of 3 · CAR · what failed

Both LLOs in CAR dropped out. We don't repeat the same model.

A timeline of an honest failure. Why we now flag fragile contexts as needing dedicated in-country presence — and what we still don't have a sharper rule for.

Phase 1 — 2024 CAR enters the CHC vaccine campaign Two LLOs contracted in the Central African Republic. Trial Run terms. Remote coordination from Dimagi.

Connect contracted two Locally-Led Organizations in the Central African Republic for the CHC vaccine work. Both came in under standard Trial Run terms. The coordination model was the same one that had been working in Nigeria and Kenya: remote weekly check-ins, supervisor escalation through WhatsApp, periodic field visits by a Dimagi program manager. The implicit assumption was that the model that worked in Nigeria would work in CAR — that the platform plus the LLO plus remote support were enough.

They were not. Both LLOs dropped out. The proximate causes were staffing concentration (one or two people carrying most operational load, and unable to absorb a single departure) and connectivity issues (mobile-network coverage and electricity reliability that intermittently broke the supervisor escalation channel). The deeper cause was that the Nigeria model assumed a baseline of organizational stability and connectivity infrastructure that did not exist in CAR. Remote coordination was not enough; the platform's leverage requires that there be something local to leverage.

Phase 2 — 2025 The new rule Fragile contexts are flagged as needing dedicated in-country presence before activation. We don't repeat the same model in similarly difficult geographies.

The rule we adopted after CAR is honest and coarse. We have not yet built a sharper predictive model for which fragile-context features make remote coordination fail. The features we suspect matter — single-point-of-failure staffing, electricity reliability, mobile-network coverage, road access during rains, currency volatility for FLW payments — are all measurable, and we are starting to track them as part of pre-contracting diligence in fragile contexts. But "in-country presence" as a rule is descriptive of what we did about CAR; it is not yet a model that an outside funder could use to predict where Connect should and should not deploy.

The cost of getting this wrong is significant. The two CAR LLOs were paid the setup fee but did not deliver, the campaign window in those communities was lost, and the children we had hoped to reach in that cycle were not reached. Publishing the failure is not penance; it is the input to the next iteration. We do not repeat the same model in similarly difficult geographies. Where we cannot place dedicated in-country presence, we are slower to contract.

→ Sources: Founders Pledge Final Report (Feb 2026), CHC program — CAR cohort outcomes documented.

What we don't know yet

  1. Whether the 65% Trial Run conversion holds outside CHC — KMC, ECD, and Readers are still on too small a contract base to publish their own.
  2. "Fragile context requires in-country presence" is a coarse rule learned from CAR. We don't have a sharper predictive model yet.
  3. We have not built a structured framework for capturing what individual LLOs invent locally — the real intervention often lives in their heads.
