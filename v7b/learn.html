Connect/How It Works/01 Learn

Competence before compensation.

Before any frontline worker delivers a service through Connect, they complete mandatory digital training and pass an in-app knowledge assessment. No classroom time. No placeholder credentials. What follows is what we've learned designing that step.

Learning 01 / Methodology

Knowledge tests alone don't produce competent FLWs. We tried. They didn't.

The question we had

The original Connect Learn design treated training as an information-transfer problem. Build the module, have the worker read it, give them a multiple-choice assessment, let them progress if they pass. The assumption: if an FLW can correctly answer questions about ORS dosing or KMC positioning, they can deliver the service competently.

We suspected — but had not yet measured — that this assumption was wrong.

What we tried

We ran the digital-only pipeline across several programs. FLWs self-paced through Learn modules, took a gated knowledge test, and proceeded to service delivery if they passed. Supervisors shadowed a sample of first visits and rated them against the same rubric we'd expect from a traditionally-trained worker.

What we found

Workers who passed the knowledge test still struggled with the nuances of in-person counseling — how to handle a skeptical caregiver, how to recognize subtle danger signs, how to practice motivational interviewing. Knowledge ≠ competence. The test measured whether the worker had absorbed the content. It did not measure whether they could apply it under pressure in a real household with a real caregiver.

This mismatch shows up most visibly in programs where the "right answer" depends on context — vaccine hesitancy counselling, responsive caregiving in ECD, breastfeeding troubleshooting in KMC. A worker can ace a quiz on motivational interviewing principles and still fail to use them at the doorstep.

What changed

We layered the model. The current Learn pipeline has five gates, not one:

(1) Self-paced digital learning in the Learn app. (2) An in-app knowledge test — must pass to progress. (3) Daily interaction with an AI coach bot, built on Dimagi's Open Chat Studio, that runs scenario-based practice and motivational-interviewing drills. (4) Peer practice. (5) One supervised observation visit rated by an LLO supervisor before the worker is cleared to earn.

The AI coach is the key addition. It converts learning from a one-shot event into a daily, conversational practice. It asks about yesterday's visits. It role-plays a reluctant caregiver. It catches knowledge gaps that multiple-choice can't.

What's still open

We have strong evidence this layered model produces competent delivery — 88% of FLWs scored above 70% on first observed visits, 85% progressed from training to service delivery, validated across ~400 FLWs in iterative ECD experiments. What we don't yet know is how much each layer contributes independently. The coach bot is expensive to run at scale; the supervised observation is expensive to staff. A cleaner ablation study is planned for 2026.

SourceECD Overview · Feb 2026 — Malawi pilot, 76 FLWs, iterative experiments with ~400 FLWs total. CLP Stage 2 Report · Feb 2026.
Learning 02 / Program design

We learned caregiver attitudes were already correct. The gap is knowledge and behavior.

The question we had

An ECD intervention exists to change something. The question a training designer has to answer is: change what? Build positive attitudes about responsive caregiving? Teach specific practices? Shift observable behavior? Each implies a different curriculum, a different visit structure, and a different cost.

We structured the Malawi pilot to measure all three — attitude, knowledge, behavior — so we could let evidence decide where the intervention's weight should land.

What we tried

A 3-visit ECD pilot across 76 FLWs who conducted 9,510+ visits. Baseline and endline surveys with 50 caregivers. Attitudes measured by agreement scales. Knowledge measured by domain-specific questions. Behavior measured by observed caregiver-child interaction rated against a rubric (teaching, encouragement of autonomy, affection, responsiveness).

What we found

Caregivers already held positive ECD attitudes at baseline. Nearly all agreed talk-and-play supports brain development. Nearly all endorsed responsive caregiving. Attitude scores were high before the intervention and high after. The pilot moved them slightly, but attitude was not the gap.

Where the intervention actually moved the needle: knowledge of timing of rapid child development (+33%), knowledge of ways to support mental development (+41%), and observed behavior — teaching (+21%), encouragement of autonomy (+21%). The last of these, encouragement of autonomy, remained the hardest subdomain, ending at only 47% at endline. It's the one that requires sustained practice rather than one-time exposure.

What changed

We reallocated the intervention. The Malawi pilot used 3 visits per caregiver. The expansion is moving to 8-10 visits across 12 months — not because we need more time to convince caregivers ECD matters (they know), but because behavior change takes sustained practice.

Curriculum content shifted weight away from attitude-framing (already correct) toward specific practices (teaching behaviors, autonomy encouragement, timing-of-development knowledge) that we have evidence we can move.

What's still open

The baseline-positive-attitude finding comes from 50 caregivers in Malawi. It may not generalize to Nigeria and Mozambique where the program is also running. We are running baseline surveys in those contexts before committing to the same curriculum reallocation. It is also possible the attitude scales we used were too easy — socially desirable responses can mask genuine uncertainty.

SourceECD Overview Report · Feb 2026 — Malawi pilot, April–July 2025, 50-caregiver baseline/endline survey.
Learning 03 / Scale

Ongoing support can be AI-delivered. We verified that with 511 real queries.

The question we had

A digital training model scales asymmetrically on the first run — the 10,000th FLW takes the module at the same marginal cost as the 100th. Ongoing support does not. When a worker is in the field and has a question — an unexpected caregiver response, a dosing edge case, a danger sign they haven't seen — who answers?

The traditional answer is an LLO-employed supervisor with WhatsApp open. That answer scales linearly. It contradicts Connect's operating premise.

What we tried

We built a conversational AI chatbot on Dimagi's Open Chat Studio platform and gave FLWs a WhatsApp channel to it. The bot's knowledge base included the program-specific training content, dosing tables, decision trees, and sample transcripts of supervisor responses to common questions.

FLWs were told: use the chatbot first. If it can't answer, it escalates to a human supervisor.

What we found

Across the Kenya CHC pilot, the bot handled 500 of 511 queries (97.8%) without human escalation. Average response time: 5 seconds. An independent evaluation scored the responses at 88.7% accuracy and 81.5% usefulness — not perfect, but well inside the band where field workers would rather get an instant adequate answer than wait hours for a perfect one.

A parallel finding from the CLP Stage 2 vaccine campaign: the seven locally-led organizations operating largely without direct Dimagi support rated the digital training resources at 9–9.5 out of 10. All seven completed their campaigns. The self-service path works when the materials are built for it.

What changed

The AI coach is now part of the standard Learn/Deliver pipeline for ECD and is being rolled into CHC and KMC. Daily coach interactions are a paid FLW activity — we want workers using it, reflecting on visits, asking questions. The bot is a first-line support layer, not a replacement for human supervision.

What's still open

511 queries is not an enormous evaluation set. We're continuing to score bot outputs in every program that deploys it, with quarterly audits against a reference panel. The 11 queries that did escalate are the interesting ones — we want the bot to know when it doesn't know, and we're still tuning the threshold.

SourceFounders Pledge Final Report · Feb 2026; CLP Stage 2 Report · Feb 2026.
