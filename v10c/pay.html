Cost per verified visit fell 22% as CHC scaled. The architecture is built for this curve.

The question we had

The standard NGO-scaling pattern is that operational costs grow with output — more workers, more managers, more field offices. If a verified-payment platform's economics are different, it should produce a measurably different curve as it scales. Did Connect, in CHC during the FP grant period, actually invert that curve?

What we tried

We instrumented cost-per-verified-visit across the FP grant period, separated by commodity bundle, and reported it openly. We chose verified-visit cost rather than cost-per-FLW-trained or cost-per-LLO-enrolled because the latter two hide the lever that matters — whether the work was actually done. The asymmetry between activity-cost and outcome-cost is where most development funding gets eaten.

What we found

In CHC during the FP grant period1, cost per verified visit fell from $2.20 to $1.70 — a 22% reduction — as visit volume scaled to the 880,000+ figure. Dimagi's team did not substantially grow during that window. The architectural reasons are platform-level: LLOs have inherently low overhead (local, lean), performance-based payment ties operational cost to delivery rather than organizational size, and integrated digital tooling produces compounding efficiency gains as the same code path runs across more campaigns.

The bundle separation matters for procurement comparisons. Inside CHC, Nigeria visits include an ORS/Zinc co-pack and across 14 LLO contracts averaged $1.30 per verified visit fully loaded. Outside Nigeria, the bundle is VAS plus deworming with no ORS, and across 17 contracts averaged $0.78. The $1.70 weighted average is the right number for "CHC during the FP grant period." It is the wrong number for "what would a similar campaign cost in country X." Procurement readers need the bundle.

What changed

We now report cost-per-verified-visit as the primary cost metric, with bundle separation, fully loaded (commodity + FLW + LLO + 20% Connect platform fee). We treat cost-per-FLW-trained and cost-per-app-installed as supporting metrics, not the headline. The setup-fee component, currently averaging about $0.40 per visit during the grant, is projected to converge toward $0.10 per visit at scale as Trial Run amortizes across larger campaign volumes.

What's still open

Whether the 22% year-over-year curve holds in non-CHC programs is an open empirical question we will publish as data accumulates from KMC, ECD, and Readers. The setup-fee projection ($0.40 → $0.10/visit) is forward-looking, not measured; we will publish the converged number when the next cohort closes. Verified-visit cost can also be gamed if the verification stack is shallow — we publish our four-layer stack (biometric, GPS, photo, algorithmic) so readers can audit it.

Source: Founders Pledge Final Report (Feb 2026), CHC §Cost Reduction — bundle separation across 14 + 17 LLO contracts. [1]
Learning 2 of 3 · Platform methodology · N18 triangulated ⟶ insights#n18

We chose verified-visit cost reporting because cost-per-FLW-trained hides the lever that matters.

The question we had

What metric, reported in public, makes the funder due-diligence story honest rather than flattering? Most platforms report cost-per-FLW-trained, cost-per-app-installed, or cost-per-enrolled-LLO. Each of those is easy to drive down through activity that does not correspond to outcome. Should we report them, and if not, what's the alternative we can defend?

What we tried

We picked cost-per-verified-visit as the primary metric and held it across every public report, including in the Insights index on this site. We report total verified visits (the denominator), the commodity bundle (so readers can compare like-for-like), and fully-loaded cost (so the platform overhead and LLO economics aren't hidden). We publish the four-layer verification stack so readers can audit the denominator's integrity.

What we found

The asymmetry between activity-cost and outcome-cost is large enough that most platforms reporting only activity-cost are reporting on a metric a funder cannot use. Cost-per-FLW-trained makes a platform with high training throughput and low delivery throughput look efficient — it isn't. Cost-per-verified-visit refuses that gap by definition. The 22% CHC reduction matters because it is on the verified-visit denominator; the same number on cost-per-FLW-trained would be a much weaker claim.

What changed

Our public reporting is now standardized on verified-visit cost, with bundle separation and fully-loaded scope. The metric choice is itself a public commitment — we have written it into our funder-facing methodology so a future Connect that drifts toward easier metrics has to do so against an explicit prior commitment.

What's still open

Verified-visit cost can itself be gamed if the verification stack is shallow. The four layers (biometric, GPS, photo, algorithmic) compound, but each has its own failure modes — photo on low bandwidth, GPS in dense urban canyons, biometric across age groups. We publish them so readers can audit; we have not yet published the per-layer false-positive rates in production.

Source: FP Report methodology section; insights.md framing across Insight 4 and Pay-page logic. [1]
Learning 3 of 3 · Nigeria FLW Displacement Survey · I5 · N15 hard data ⟶ insights#n15

Campaign work in Nigeria isn't a disruption of routine care — it is the routine care.

The question we had

Western development discourse routinely raises a "crowd-out" concern: paid campaign work distorts the volunteer health system, draws CHVs away from routine duties, and degrades the broader CHV ecosystem. Is that concern accurate in the contexts where Connect operates, and have we measured it rather than asserted it?

What we tried

We commissioned 24 in-depth interviews in Gombe and Sokoto2, the two Nigerian states where CHC is densest, in October 2025. The protocol asked CHVs about their volunteer hours during campaign weeks vs non-campaign weeks, who covered routine work during campaigns, and whether paid campaign work had displaced any volunteer work over time.

What we found

The framing was wrong. Campaign work is not a disruption of Nigeria's CHV system — it is the system. CHVs routinely run campaigns; routine work is partly carried by other volunteers covering during campaign weeks and partly maintained by the same CHVs by shifting their schedules within Connect's 7am–7pm visit window. In Gombe specifically, FLWs maintained volunteer hours during campaign weeks by working campaigns in the morning and routine duties in the late afternoon. The paid-vs-volunteer dichotomy that the "crowd-out" concern rests on doesn't match the way the work is actually organized in these states.

What changed

The displacement question is now answered for these contexts with named evidence — the Western "crowd-out" assumption is questioned in our public materials rather than asserted as a constraint. Pay-page material now leads with the survey rather than apologizing for paid work.

What's still open

N=24 in two Nigerian states. We expect this to hold in similar community-health settings; we'd re-survey before claiming it elsewhere. The Western "crowd-out" assumption may still apply in countries with different CHV structures — Bangladesh, parts of East Africa with stronger public-sector CHV cadres. Re-surveying before transferring the finding is a methodology commitment, not a hedge.

Source: FLW Displacement Survey (Oct 2025) — N=24 interviews, Gombe and Sokoto, CHC. [2]
