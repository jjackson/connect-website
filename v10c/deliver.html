§2 · Deliver · how locally-led organizations actually scale

We invented the Trial Run because pre-vetting LLOs didn't predict performance.

Conventional procurement says vet partners thoroughly before contracting. We tried it. Past reputation, team size, and organizational maturity did not reliably predict whether an LLO would deliver. So we built the Trial Run: $3,000 setup for 3,000 verified visits, performance-paid. 107 EOIs. 37 contracts. 24 Trials. 14 campaigns. 65% conversion — published. And one cohort failure (CAR) that taught us when remote coordination breaks.

Past reputation didn't predict performance. The Trial Run replaced procurement screening.

The question we had

If a Locally-Led Organization presents a strong proposal — experienced staff, prior CHV-campaign references, a reasonable cost model — does that information predict whether they will actually deliver verified visits at scale? Conventional procurement assumes yes. Our first three CHC campaigns assumed yes. After three campaigns we stopped pretending.

What we tried

The standard pre-qualification stack: organizational maturity questionnaire, reference checks with prior funders, financial-management diligence, leadership interviews. We graded LLO applications on this stack and selected the highest-graded for full campaign contracts. The grading process was structured and defensible. It also did not predict performance. The signal we were screening on was decoupled from the outcome we were paying for.

What we found

The cleanest published view of the alternative is the CHC funnel1: 107 EOI applications, 37 LLOs contracted under Trial Run terms ($3,000 setup fee for 3,000 verified child visits, performance-based), 24 advanced to full Trial Runs, 14 graduated to larger campaigns. Three additional LLOs went straight to larger campaigns based on prior measured performance from earlier Connect programs. The published 65% Trial-Run conversion is not an intent; it's the output of a measurement on the same Connect verification stack a full campaign would use. There is no synthetic pilot signal — the data we collect during a Trial Run is real production data.

The 35% that didn't pass were paid the setup fee, not per-visit pay; they were not continued. Why this matters in practice: CHC works in 8 countries with very different LLO ecosystems. Nigeria has many candidate partners with prior CHV-campaign experience; Sierra Leone has fewer; Central African Republic has effectively none. Pre-vetting at scale across that variance was producing biased screens — partners we knew were systematically advantaged over partners we didn't. Trial Run terms remove the bias by paying small for real and rewarding only delivery.

What changed

Trial Run is now the platform's default path to LLO activation. It has been reused on Readers, KMC, and chlorine work, not just CHC. The procurement document is no longer the screen; the first 3,000 verified visits are. We publish the 65% conversion because the alternative — opaque vendor lists — is what funders are trying to escape.

What's still open

The selection criteria that drive the 65% conversion are program-manager judgment calls today, not codified into a model an outside funder could replicate. We have a checklist of red flags from the failed cohort (staffing concentration, single-point-of-failure dependence, cash-flow fragility) but it is descriptive, not predictive. Whether the 65% holds outside CHC is also open — KMC, ECD, and Readers are still on too small a contract base to publish their own conversion.

Source: Founders Pledge Final Report (Feb 2026), CHC §LLO Activation Model — full funnel published. [1]
Learning 2 of 3 · CHC / CLP Stage 2 · I6 · N7 hard data ⟶ insights#n7

5 of 7 LLOs leveraged religious leaders for vaccine outreach. We did not teach them this.

The question we had

What does the L in LLO actually do that a centralized model cannot — concretely, in a campaign, measured against a specific community-health outcome? "Locally-led" is rhetorically easy and operationally hard to evidence.

What we tried

The CLP Stage 2 vaccine campaign within CHC. 7 LLOs in the campaign, evaluated by an outside research team going to the field rather than by Dimagi inferring from app logs. We did not push a "religious-leader engagement module" through the Learn app. We did not tell partners to recruit religious leaders. We watched what they did.

What we found

Of the 7 LLOs in the campaign2, 5–6 independently leveraged religious leaders, community leaders, or existing community relationships as part of their vaccine-hesitancy outreach. They did this without prompting, without it being on the Learn modules, and without it being on the supervisor checklist. Specific examples: one FLW countered hesitancy with a personal example — "We were immunized, we have children." Another LLO sourced FLWs the community itself recommended, lending immediate credibility before a single conversation about vaccines.

The pattern is locally-grounded knowledge that does not survive being centralized. If Dimagi had written a module — "engage religious leaders; here are five talking points" — it would have been a cartoon of what those LLOs already understood about their own communities. The platform's job, evidently, is to enable this rather than to centralize it.

What changed

The 5-of-7 finding is now a referenced data point in how we describe the L-in-LLO to funders. Operationally, it has firmed up an architectural rule: Connect controls workflow logic, the verification stack, and payment guardrails. LLOs control FLW recruitment, supervision schedules, the framing of outreach, and the relationships with health-facility staff. The split is what makes 44 LLOs in 14 months tractable without 44 Dimagi field offices.

What's still open

We have not built a structured framework for capturing or sharing what individual LLOs invent locally. The "real intervention" often lives in their heads, not in our app. The 5-of-7 finding was surfaced by an outside evaluator going to the field, not by the platform. Building a feedback loop that surfaces those inventions without flattening them is on the roadmap, and we are wary of doing it badly.

Source: CLP Stage 2 Report (Feb 2026), CHC vaccine campaign — 7 LLOs evaluated, qualitative interviews. [2]
Learning 3 of 3 · CHC / CAR · I9 · N8 triangulated ⟶ insights#n8

Both LLOs in CAR dropped out. We now flag fragile contexts as needing in-country presence.

The question we had

The Trial Run model assumes baseline connectivity, baseline LLO staffing capacity, and a partner that can convert digital tooling into operational delivery. In a fragile context — active conflict, weak telecom, limited candidate-LLO pool — does that assumption hold?

What we tried

We contracted two LLOs in the Central African Republic for CHC vaccine work. We coordinated remotely from the CHC program management team. Same Connect stack, same payment terms, same Trial Run conversion gate.

What we found

Both LLOs dropped out3. Three features showed up together in the post-mortem: staffing concentration (a single key operator at each LLO whose unavailability halted the campaign), connectivity fragility (the verification stack assumes intermittent-but-recoverable network; the actual conditions were below that floor), and concurrent emergency-response load on the partner organizations themselves. Remote coordination was not enough. There was no recoverable middle path: the campaign did not partially deliver, it stopped.

What changed

We now flag fragile contexts as requiring dedicated in-country presence before activation. We do not repeat the same remote-coordination model in similarly difficult geographies. The CAR cohort is named in the FP report — we publish failures because the methodology breaks if we don't.

What's still open

"In-country presence" is a coarse rule. We don't yet have a sharper predictive model for which fragile-context features make remote coordination fail — single-operator staffing? Specific connectivity thresholds? Concurrent emergency load? We have qualitative red flags from the CAR post-mortem but not a quantitative threshold. The next fragile-context campaign we contract will test whether the in-country-presence rule is sufficient or whether we need finer screens on top of it.

Source: Founders Pledge Final Report (Feb 2026), CHC §Vaccine Campaigns — CAR cohort post-mortem. [3]
