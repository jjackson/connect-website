§2 · Deliver · activating LLOs and getting visits done

We invented the Trial Run because pre-vetting didn't predict performance.

Connect's delivery model rests on Locally-Led Organizations (LLOs) — small partner organizations that recruit, supervise, and pay frontline workers in their own communities. Conventional procurement says vet partners thoroughly before contracting. We tried that. It didn't work. This page is the alternative we built and the place it has already failed.

Learning 1 of 3

Past reputation didn't predict field performance. So we test on small contracts.

The first version of LLO selection followed standard procurement practice. We checked track records. We assessed teams. We evaluated organizational maturity. We had references and documentation and capacity statements. We contracted only the LLOs that scored well on those criteria. And then, in the field, the predictions failed.

Past reputation was a poor predictor of whether an LLO would actually deliver on a Connect campaign. Team size was a poor predictor. Organizational maturity was a poor predictor. Some highly-credentialed LLOs underperformed; some low-profile LLOs delivered cleanly. The signal we were screening on was decoupled from the outcome we were paying for. After three campaigns we stopped pretending the screening worked.

What we built instead is the Trial Run. A new LLO gets a small initial contract — $3,000 setup fee for 3,000 verified child visits. Performance-based payment means Dimagi bears minimal financial risk if the LLO fails. The Trial Run uses the same Connect verification stack as a full campaign, so the data we collect is real data, not pilot data. And the LLO that finishes a Trial Run has earned the next contract not on a credential but on an outcome.

The published numbers from CHC1: 107 EOI applications, 37 LLOs contracted under Trial Run terms, 24 advanced to full Trials, 10 underperformed and were not continued, 3 went straight to larger campaigns based on prior measured performance from other Connect programs. 14 LLOs ran larger campaigns successfully. We publish the conversion rate — 65% pass — because the alternative is the opaque vendor list that funder due-diligence is trying to escape. The 35% that don't pass aren't blamed; they aren't continued.

The Trial Run is the operational reason Connect can scale. It has now been reused — without modification — for reading-glasses distribution with RestoringVision, KMC, and chlorine-dispenser campaigns. The same $3,000 / 3,000 visits structure (or its program-specific equivalent) is the entry door for new partners. We are not the bottleneck on partner discovery. The funnel is.

Learning 2 of 3 · CHC / CLP Stage 2

LLOs invented vaccine-hesitancy outreach with religious leaders. We didn't teach them this.

Connect's Learn pipeline covers vaccine myths, dosage, cold-chain handling, the contraindication checklist. We thought that would cover the demand-side problem too — if the FLW knows the science, they can answer the parent's question. The CLP Stage 2 vaccine campaign showed us that the structural intervention against hesitancy doesn't live in the FLW. It lives in the LLO.

Of 7 LLOs in our CLP Stage 2 vaccine campaign (CHC program)2, 5 to 6 independently leveraged religious leaders, community leaders, or existing community relationships as part of their outreach strategy. They did this before we asked. One FLW countered hesitancy with a personal example — "We were immunized, we have children." Another LLO had FLWs who were recommended by the community itself, lending immediate credibility before a single conversation about vaccines began.

The pattern is locally-grounded knowledge that does not survive being centralized. If Dimagi had written a "religious-leader engagement module" and pushed it through the Learn app, it would have been a cartoon of what those LLOs already understood about their own communities. The platform's job is not to tell the L in LLO what to do. It is to handle the parts that don't generalize — the verification stack, the payment rails, the supervision pipeline — so that the LLO can spend its judgment on the parts that don't transfer between contexts.

This is why Connect tilts capability into the LLO, not into the central org. The LLO controls FLW recruitment, supervision schedules, the framing of outreach, the local language and idiom, and the relationships with health-facility staff. Connect controls the workflow logic, the verification, and the payment guardrails. The split is what makes 44 LLOs in 14 months possible without 44 Dimagi field offices.

The honest gap: we do not yet have a structured framework for capturing and sharing what individual LLOs invent locally. The 5-of-7 finding was found by an outside evaluator going to the field. It was not in our app, our Learn modules, or our supervisor dashboards. The "real intervention" was in heads, not screens. Building a feedback loop that surfaces those inventions without flattening them is on the roadmap, and we are wary of doing it badly.

Learning 3 of 3 · CHC / CAR

Both LLOs in CAR dropped out. We now flag fragile contexts as needing in-country presence.

The Trial Run model assumes a baseline. Connectivity sufficient to upload visit data within a campaign cycle. LLO staffing capacity sufficient to recruit and supervise FLWs from a distance. A working financial rail. Where any of those baselines is below threshold, the model breaks.

In the Central African Republic, both contracted LLOs in our CHC vaccine work dropped out3. The reasons were a combination of staffing — partners had teams committed to other emergency-response work and could not free FLWs for a Connect campaign — and connectivity, which made remote coordination from Dimagi's side untenable. There was no recoverable middle path. The Trial Run terms protected the budget; they did not protect the campaign.

What we changed: Connect now flags fragile contexts as requiring dedicated in-country presence before activation, not as a bias correction after activation. We do not repeat the same remote-coordination model in geographies with similar profiles. For the next deployment in a CAR-shaped context we are designing a model that combines local staff embedded with the LLO for the first 90 days with the standard Trial Run terms after that, and we will publish whether it works.

The reason this section is on the public site, rather than in an internal lessons-learned document, is that the CAR experience now shapes the public commitments we will and will not make. Connect can activate partners rapidly in a wide range of contexts. It cannot do so in every context, and the geographies where it cannot are knowable in advance. Funders considering campaigns in fragile states deserve that prediction explicitly, not buried in an addendum.

The honest limit: "in-country presence" is a coarse rule. We do not yet have a sharper predictive model for which fragile-context features make remote coordination fail. Connectivity is one. Active conflict is another. Concurrent emergency-response load on partner organizations is a third. The next iteration of the model is probabilistic, not categorical, and it will be informed by the next cohort of activations.

What we don't know yet

  1. Whether the 65% Trial Run conversion holds outside CHC — KMC, ECD, and Readers are still on too small a contract base to publish their own.
  2. "Fragile context requires in-country presence" is a coarse rule learned from CAR. We don't have a sharper predictive model yet.
  3. We have not built a structured framework for capturing what individual LLOs invent locally — the real intervention often lives in their heads.
