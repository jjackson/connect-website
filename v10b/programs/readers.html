the challenge // why readers

Presbyopia is solvable. Distribution isn't, yet.

Presbyopia — the age-related decline in near vision starting around age 35-40 — affects most adults globally and is corrected with off-the-shelf reading glasses. The intervention is cheap, durable, and life-altering for adults whose livelihoods depend on close work: tailors, teachers, traders, farmers reading instructions on inputs. The science is settled. So is the supply: 15,000+ pairs available through our partnership with RestoringVision.

The bottleneck is delivery: who screens, who distributes, who verifies that the right person received the right correction, and who gets paid for the work. In northeast Nigeria, formal optometry services are rare; an adult who needs reading glasses today typically does without. Connect Readers is the platform applied to that gap.

This page is honest about state: the program is in activation, not steady-state. LLOs are finalized; contract signing is pending. Field data won't exist until the first cohort runs. We're publishing the design now and will publish performance as it lands.

how it works // delivery_stack

The Readers delivery stack.

step 01

FLW digital training

Connect's standard layered training, customized for near-vision screening protocols and lens-strength selection.

step 02

Door-to-door screening

FLWs identify presbyopia in adults 35+ using a standardized near-vision card, app-guided.

step 03

Lens-strength selection

Workers fit lens strength from a stocked set of standard diopter ranges — the off-the-shelf approach the science supports.

step 04

On-site distribution

Glasses dispensed in the same visit. Recipient ID, GPS, and photo capture the distribution.

step 05

Four-layer verification

Same biometric + GPS + photo + algorithmic stack as the rest of Connect, validating the verified distribution.

step 06

Per-pair payment

FLW paid per verified distribution to a confirmed-presbyopic adult, on mobile money.

learning 01 // platform_portability

Readers is a clean test of whether the LDVP cycle generalizes outside child health.

scope=readers · pre-launch Most of Connect's measured outcomes come from CHC and ECD — child-and-caregiver-centered interventions. Readers is the first program where the recipient is an adult, the intervention is durable goods rather than a service, and the outcome (correctly fitted reading glasses on a presbyopic adult) is measurable in a single distribution rather than tracked across multiple visits. The LDVP cycle should still hold; the test is whether it does.

What the platform brings to a different domain

The four-layer verification stack — biometric, GPS, photo, algorithmic — was designed against the failure modes of community health work. Readers exercises a different shape of failure: the wrong adult receiving glasses (e.g., distribution to a non-presbyopic recipient who could pass for one), or a stocked lens strength selected outside the screening result. Photo capture of the dispensed pair plus the recipient's reading test result, paired with ID verification, should catch these. We don't yet have the field data to prove that.

What's structurally different

Readers also shifts the program economics. CHC pays per visit, regardless of commodity. Readers pays per verified pair distributed to a presbyopic adult — a unit that is more like a transaction than a relationship. Whether that creates incentives for over-screening (workers tagging marginal cases as presbyopic to reach distribution counts) is a real question; the screening protocol and verification stack are the controls. We'll publish the false-positive rate when the first cohort produces enough data to compute it.

learning 02 // what_we_expect_to_learn

The first cohort answers three questions we cannot answer yet.

scope=readers · pre-launch The page header says "field_data=pending" because it is. Listing what we expect to learn is more useful than fabricating a learning we don't have. There are three questions the first cohort should answer cleanly, and one we suspect it won't.

Question 1 — Does novice digital training transfer?

Whether the screening protocol holds quality consistency across novice FLWs trained only digitally is a real test. Connect ECD and KMC have layered training (digital + AI coach + supervised observation) calibrated against ~400 FLWs. Readers will use the same model. The question is whether near-vision screening — a more technical task than the conversational ECD work — transfers cleanly to FLWs who learn it through a phone app. The supervised observation gate is the binding constraint; we'll publish first-cohort pass rates when they exist.

Question 2 — Does door-to-door find the right adults?

Door-to-door delivery in CHC is targeting children 0-59 months — a population easy to identify by demographics. Door-to-door in Readers needs to find presbyopic adults, which means age 35+ self-reported, then a screening confirmation. Whether door-to-door distribution finds the right adults at sufficient rates without targeting infrastructure is open. Targeted approaches (e.g., starting at marketplaces, schools, or religious gathering points where adults concentrate) may outperform pure door-to-door — we'll measure both if the first LLO splits its method.

Question 3 — What's the Trial Run conversion in a new program?

The 65% Trial Run conversion is a CHC number. Readers is too early to publish its own. The first 2 LLOs (eHA, C3HD) are finalizing contracts and will run their Trials in 2026. We'll publish the conversion rate as a Readers-specific number when there are enough LLOs in the funnel to publish it — not before.
